Abstract 1Time perception and decision-making are interrelated 2processes that are critical to much of human and animal 3behavior. Although the behavioral importance of this link 4between the processes has been established, the neural 5underpinnings of temporal decision-making are unclear. 6The current study leverages human intracranial electro-7physiology to investigate the neural contribution of tem-8poral processing to decision-making under risk. To probe 9temporal decision-making, we use a version of the Bal-10 loon Analogue Risk Task, in which participants inflate vir-11 tual balloons with the goal of reaching maximal balloon 12 size but stopping inflation before the balloon pops. Points 13 are awarded based on inflation duration of successful tri-14 als, such that points awarded are linearly related to bal-15 loon size. By decoding temporal features of the task on a 16 trial-by-trial basis, we find neural representations of time 17 during balloon inflation. Specifically, we find that both me-18 dial and lateral temporal regions of the brain encode the 19 majority of this time-related signal. Importantly, we can 20 decode the progression of time leading up to the outcome 21 of a successful decision (pressing a button to stop infla-22 tion before the balloon pops). This encoding of time de-23 pends on an active decision to end the trial: we cannot 24 decode time during passive observation of balloon infla-25 tion to a known maximal size. To validate these results 26 further, we use a temporal convolution network to predict 27 participants’ response (i.e., time of button press) based 28 on the brain activity leading up to the response. We find 29 that we can predict the moment of response with high ac-30 curacy and temporal resolution. The model predicts the 31 timing of successful decisions but not the timing of bal-32 loon pops. Together, these findings reveal neural repre-33 sentations that encode the progression of time, specifi-34 cally in relation to an active decision. 35 

# Introduction 36 Integrating temporal and sensory information is essen-37 tial to build and update models of the external environ-38 ment and internal state (Clark, 2013), making timing a 39 vital component of predictive decision-making. The im-40 portance of time-keeping has led to a long-time search 41 for neural “clocks” or other neural mechanisms for tem-42 poral processing. Various candidate regions have been 43 implicated—from cerebellum (Ashe and Bushara, 2014) 44 to medial temporal regions (Pastalkova et al., 2008; Tsao 45 et al., 2018; Umbach et al., 2020) to striatum (Bakhurin 46 et al., 2017; Mello et al., 2015; Toso et al., 2021), and 47 even sensory cortex (Rabinovich et al., 2022; Salvioni 48 et al., 2013). However, these studies focus mainly on 49 neural correlates of externally imposed time intervals, 50 while ethological decision-making often involves self-51 generated durations, such as deciding when to execute 52 an action. 53 Indeed, time and decision-making are inextricably 54 linked, with both processes bidirectionally influencing 55 each other. For instance, decision-making under time 56 pressure has been shown to enhance risk-seeking be-57 havior (Young et al., 2012), while conversely, impul-58 sive traits influence an individual’s perception of time 59 (Wittmann and Paulus, 2008). Although existing litera-60 ture describes this behavioral relationship between time 61 perception and decision-making, the neural basis of hu-62 man temporal decision-making is still unknown—partly 63 due to limitations of conventional fMRI approaches, 64 which lack the temporal resolution needed to track 65 rapidly unfolding neural dynamics that subserve pre-66 cisely timed decisions. 67 The present study sought to uncover temporal pro-68 cessing in the human brain during risk evaluation and 69 timing-dependent decision making. To this end, we 70 leveraged high temporal resolution intracranial electro-71 physiology in the human brain, and applied a version 72 of the Balloon Analogue Risk Task (BART). Tradition-73 ally used to study impulsive behavior and risk-seeking 74 (Lejuez et al., 2002; Rao et al., 2008), this task is also 75 ideal for investigating temporal decision-making. Unlike 76 other decision-making tasks, for which accuracy is the 77 primary measure and timing is secondary, in BART, high 78 task performance requires accurate time interval estima-79 tion and successful learning of the relationship between 80 time interval and sensory stimulus. 81 1

# Methods 97 

## Subjects 98 18 neurosurgical patients undergoing clinical monitoring 99 for epilepsy participated in the study. All subjects pro-100 vided informed consent prior to participation, both for 101 implantation of microelectrodes and for participation in 102 cognitive tasks. All experimental procedures were ap-103 proved by the Institutional Review Board. Only patients 104 aged 18 and over participated in the experiments. No 105 patients with major neuroanatomical anomalies or cog-106 nitive deficits were included. 107 

## Behavioral Paradigm 108 Patients performed a version of the Balloon Analogue 109 Risk Task (BART; Fig. 1a). For each trial, a colored 110 circle “balloon” appeared on the computer monitor, and 111 subjects were instructed to press a button on a video 112 game controller to start inflating a balloon. Each balloon 113 could inflate up to a predetermined size (jittered across a 114 probabilistic distribution) before popping. However, prior 115 to the pop, subjects could choose to press the button 116 again at any point to stop inflation. When subjects suc-117 cessfully stopped inflation before the balloon “popped”, 118 they were awarded points ($). These successful trials 119 were called bank trials. The value of the reward was lin-120 early related to the duration of inflation, and hence the 121 size of the balloon at the end of the inflation. If subjects 122 did not stop inflation in time, and the balloon popped, no 123 points were awarded. These error trials were pop trials. 124 Red balloons were programmed to pop at the smallest 125 size, orange balloons popped at a medium size, and yel-126 low balloons inflated the longest before popping. In ad-127 dition to these active trials, there were also risk-free con-128 trol trials, which were guaranteed to result in a bank out-129 come with no action required from the participant. Dur-130 ing these trials, colored balloons appeared in the center 131 of a grey ring; after the first button press, the balloons 132 2

## Analysis of LFP power 166 The Python MNE library was used to analyze LFP data 167 (Gramfort et al., 2013; Larson et al., 2025). Data pre-168 processing included removing noisy channels, notch fil-169 tering to remove power line noise (60 Hz and 120 Hz 170 harmonic), and common average referencing. Data was 171 epoched to only include relevant time windows. Fre-172 quency bands were split as follows: alpha (8 – 13 Hz), 173 beta (13 – 30 Hz), gamma (30 – 70 Hz), and high gamma 174 (70 – 140 Hz). Power spectra were calculated using the 175 Welch method, with a window of 0.1 seconds. 176 

## Decoding 177 

Linear classification We used Python scikit-learn 178 software (Pedregosa et al., 2011) to train linear models 179 to perform support vector classification to classify out-180 come or time bins. For outcome decoding, we subsam-181 pled to ensure equal numbers of bank and pop trials. 182 When training and testing on di !erent data (e.g., train-183 ing on time bins around outcome; testing on earlier time 184 bins), no cross-validation was performed. Otherwise, 185 K-fold cross validation was performed within-subject for 186 linear decoders: each dataset was divided into 4 test-187 train splits based on trial index; timepoints within the 188 same trial were kept together. Linear classifiers were 189 trained on a subset (3/4) of trials for each subject, for 190 each fold. Classifiers were then tested on the held out 191 trials. Classification performance was quantified using 192 one-sample two-sided t-tests comparing each subject’s 193 decoding accuracy to chance. Each timepoint and fre-194 quency band constituted a single feature. For linear de-195 coders, we identified feature importance through Haufe-196 transformed coe "cients of the linear SVM, using the 197 MNE python library to extract spatial patterns from the 198 coe "cients Haufe et al., 2014. 199

# Results 283 To study temporal processing in association with deci-284 sion making, we recorded intracranial local field poten-285 tials (LFP) from the brains of 18 neurosurgical patients 286 (Fig. 2) performing a risky time-dependent decision-287 making task (BART; Figs. 1; S1). Participants “inflated” 288 virtual balloons (by pressing a button to start and stop 289 inflation), with the goal of inflating each balloon as much 290 as possible before it popped (Fig. 1a). Each balloon 291 color was associated with a di !erent degree of risk, 292 based on its likelihood of popping at various sizes: red 293 balloons popped at the smallest size, orange balloons 294 were intermediate, and yellow balloons could inflate to 295 the largest size before popping (Fig. S1b). For each 296 balloon color, the maximum size at the time of the pop 297 was stochastic and variable across trials (S1c). Over-298 all, participants showed satisfactory behavioral perfor-299 mance on the task, especially for orange and yellow 300 balloons, which allowed longer average inflation times 301 (Figs. 1b,c; S1). Notably, inflation times, particularly for 302 yellow balloons, exhibited substantial variation (Figs. 1c; 303 S1a). 304 

## Risk is assessed during stimulus 305 

## presentation. 306 First, we considered the neural representations of the 307 three trial types (red, orange, and yellow balloon colors) 308 at di !erent time windows in the trial. We used the nonlin-309 ear encoder and dimensionality reduction tool CEBRA 310 (Schneider et al., 2023) to generate low-dimensional 311 embeddings corresponding to the three balloon colors, 312 with color as the supervisory signal (Fig. 3a; Figs. S2, 313 S3 ). We examined both the cue period and the final 314 three seconds of the inflation period. For both cue and 315 inflation, we considered only successful trials, so both 316 analyses included the same trials. We found that the 317 brain states clustered such that the trial types were sep-318 arated (Fig. 3a), and we observed two notable features 319 of the clustering. First, despite similar performance (per-320 cent banked) for yellow and orange balloons (Fig. 1b), 321 the most distinct cluster of neural data was during the 322 time bins related to viewing the yellow balloons. Sec-323 ond, the clustering was stronger for the cue period than 324 during inflation (Fig. 3b). This result suggests a neu-325 ral representation of risk that evolves over time but is 326 particularly strong while assessing the cue and before 327 initiating the inflation. 328 5

## Decision-related representations extend 329 

## backwards in time. 330 We investigated neural representations of trial-level out-331 come: bank (successful trials ended by the participant 332 via a button press) or pop (unsuccessful trials that ended 333 unexpectedly with a balloon pop before the subject’s de-334 cision to end the trial). We trained a linear support vec-335 tor machine (SVM) to classify pop vs. bank trials (for 336 active trials of all balloon colors) using neural activity 337 from a 100 ms window centered on the outcome (50 338 ms prior – 50 ms after outcome). We then applied this 339 trained classifier to non-overlapping data from the imme-340 diately preceding 100 ms window (150 – 50 ms before 341 outcome). Within this temporally distinct window, out-342 come could be predicted with 66.1% accuracy (Fig. 4a; 343 one-sample two-sided t-test, p = 2.13 × 10 →7; bank tri-344 als: 62.67%, p = 1.17 × 10 →3; pop trials: 69.08%, p345 = 1.07 × 10 →6). Thus, outcome-related representations 346 are already present in the moments leading up to the 347 outcome, suggesting that these signals are related to 348 the imminent temporal decision. 349 Next, we sought to identify the neurophysiological cor-350 relates of this representation. We examined the acti-351 vation patterns (Haufe-transformed coe "cient weights 352 of the linear classifier (Haufe et al., 2014)) to uncover 353 the underlying spatial patterns that carry the decision-354 related information (Fig. 4b,c). We found the strongest 355 signals in temporal regions—both lateral and medial 356 temporal lobe (MTL). Parietal and sensorimotor areas 357 also contributed to these anticipatory signals, albeit to 358 a lesser extent, suggesting a sensorimotor component 359 to the preparatory state. In sum, these findings not only 360 reveal encoding of imminent decision, but also suggest 361 that this decision-related signal is temporally-extended 362 backward in time. 363 

## Neuronal population activity tracks the 364 

## progression of time. 365 So far, we only considered aspects of the upcoming 366 decision; next, we asked whether the progression of 367 time was itself represented in the neural activity pat-368 terns. First, we examined the representations of rela-369 tive time on bank trials, which started and ended with 370 a button press. We used CEBRA to generate low-371 dimensional embeddings of the brain state trajectories 372 over the course of the entire inflation period (Fig. 5a, 373 left), with time-in-trial split into 10 bins (i.e., the initial but-374 ton press to start inflation was labeled time 0, and the 375 final button press to stop inflation was labeled time 9). 376 To quantify the encoding of relative time, we trained CE-377 BRA models to predict time-in-trial, but used 4-fold trial-378 level cross-validation, see Methods and Supplemental 379 Methods). After fitting a model on each training set, we 380 performed the same transformation on the test set to 381 generate a test embedding. From the test embeddings, 382 we used a K-nearest neighbors (KNN) classifier to de-383 code time-in-trial and saw above chance decoding per-384 formance (Fig. 5a, right; yellow balloons: one-sample 385 two-sided t-test, p = 0.0031; red balloons: p = 0.0003), 386 even for yellow-balloon trials, which had high variability 387 in inflation times (Fig. S1a). 388 Since we found evidence of temporally-extended 389 preparatory signals prior to a decision, as well as 390 relative time representations during balloon inflation, 391 we next asked if the neural activity also contained 392 information about the absolute progression of time 393 toward a decision. We trained CEBRA models on 394 neural data during the 3 seconds leading up to the 395 button press, with time-to-outcome (split into 0.1 396 second time bins) as the supervisory signal (Fig. S4). 397 Here, we only analyzed yellow-balloon bank trials, 398 which were long enough to ensure >3 second inflation 399 for most trials but still displayed substantial variation in 400 inflation time (Fig. S1a). Cross-validated model training 401 followed by KNN decoding resulted in above-chance 402 time decoding (Fig. 5b; p = 0.0015). 403 To determine whether this time-tracking relies on sim-404 ilar underlying neural processes as the encoding of 405 the temporally-extended decision discussed above, we 406 repeated the time-to-outcome decoding using a linear 407 SVM for improved interpretability. A linear SVM could 408 predict time-to-outcome with above-chance accuracy 409 (two-sided one sample t-test, p = 0.00014 for 0.5 sec-410 ond time bins), corroborating the CEBRA results. Anal-411 ysis of the spatial patterns underlying the time decod-412 ing (Fig. 6a) revealed neuroanatomical signal distribu-413 tion similar to that of the temporally-extended outcome-414 identity decoder (Fig. 4b,c). This similarity is expected 415 given that both classifiers are trained to decode time-416 and decision-related information: the neural state that 417 evolves during the time leading up to the button press 418 should carry anticipatory information about the decision 419 to press the button. However, there were subtle (though 420 not significant) di !erences between the signal distribu-421 tions. For instance, sensorimotor signals may have con-422 tributed more to the outcome identity decoder than to the 423 time decoder (Fig. 6b, left), possibly because a motor 424 action occurred during the time bin used to train the out-425 come identity decoder. Meanwhile, MTL signals were 426 important for both classifiers, (Fig. 6b, right), consistent 427 with prior reports of time encoding in MTL regions (Tsao 428 et al., 2018; Umbach et al., 2020). 429 Interestingly, time-to-outcome was only linearly de-430 codable on active trials (Fig. 6c, top; p = 0.00014), not 431 passive (control) trials (bottom; p = 0.67) in which reward 432 was guaranteed and no action was required to stop in-433 flation. ## Neural signals are time-aligned to active 441 

## decisions. 442 Above, we demonstrated time decoding relative to an 443 active decision on bank trials. We would not expect neu-444 ral activity to predict outcome-aligned time on pop tri-445 als, when temporally structured neural signals anticipate 446 an unexecuted decision planned for an unknown future 447 timepoint. However, we were unable test this assump-448 tion, due to the following limitations of the SVM time de-449 coding: 1) we only considered yellow balloon trials, to 450 ensure >3 second inflation for most trials, and as a re-451 sult, 2) we only attempted to decode time on bank trials, 452 as there were not enough yellow pop trials. 453 Thus, to assess whether neural population activity 454 contains temporally structured information su "cient to 455 predict the timing of an imminent decision but not an un-456 expected pop event, we trained a nonlinear causal tem-457 poral convolutional network (TCN) to decode the time of 458 trial response (Fig. 7a). This model allowed us to predict 459 the moment of trial outcome based on cumulative data 460 over the time course of the trial, for all three balloon col-461 ors. 4-fold cross validation showed that the TCN model 462 can accurately predict the time of response specifically 463 on bank trials (accuracy = .94 ± .012, MAE = 157 ± 52 464 ms) (Fig. 7b). The model’s prediction error was signifi-465 cantly higher for 3 individuals (Fig. S5a), and correlated 466 with specifically longer trials that indicate distraction be-467 tween cue and response (Fig. S5b). 468 As a control, we attempted to decode the time of pop 469 events (which are stochastic and should not be repre-470 sented by any brain signal). This control analysis would 471 confirm that we are in fact decoding neural representa-472 tions of temporal events rather than an artifact. Indeed, 473 the TCN model could not predict the time of pop out-474 comes (accuracy = .17 ± .018, MAE = 669 ± 61 ms): we 475 observed a significant di !erence in accuracy and MAE 476 between TCN predictions of outcome time for bank vs. 477 pop trials ( t-test, p < 0.001) (Fig. 7b). Qualitatively, the 478 TCN detected a gaussian of probability around the tar-479 get event in active trials, but failed to identify a uniform 480 probability around balloon pops (Fig. 7c). These results 481 indicate that causal data accumulate in the LFP to allow 482 prediction of meaningful choice behavior. 483

Consistent with prior demonstrations that 492 decision-related neural signals can precede motor ac-493 tions (e.g., Libet et al., 1983; Fried et al., 2011), this sig-494 nal extended backward in time at least 100 ms: a linear 495 decoder trained at the time of the outcome could pre-496 dict pop vs. bank outcome based on neural data 100 497 ms earlier. 498 Next, we sought to identify and characterize neural 499 representations of time progression, which require tem-500 porally evolving structured trajectories in neural state 501 space that track the passage of time. Note that the ex-502 istence of such representations is not inevitable. Since bal-534 loon size increased predictably over time, participants 535 likely used visual feedback about the rate and extent of 536 inflation to anchor their temporal predictions. We be-537 lieve that this visual feedback does not supplant the role 538 of timing, but rather facilitates it, since temporal pre-539 diction is critical for high accuracy in the task. Since 540 the maximum balloon size is randomized across trials, 541 responding based on only on balloon size would be 542 an unsatisfactory strategy. Meanwhile, calculating in-543 stantaneous pop probability (based on a mental com-544 parison between current balloon size and a internally-545 constructed goal template) is likely too slow to yield high 546 accuracy. However, future experiments that decouple 547 elapsed time from visual balloon inflation will be use-548 ful for distinguishing purely internal timing mechanisms 549 from temporally structured sensory-feedback signals. 550 Inherent constraints of research in human patients 551 also complicated our ability to pinpoint the precise 552 functional-anatomical correlates of human time process-553 ing. The electrode placement was determined purely 554 based on clinical need; therefore, the spatial coverage 555 of our electrophysiological recordings was both limited 556 and highly variable across participants. However, we 557 were able to partially analyze the contributions of vari-558 ous brain regions to the encoding of temporal decision-559 making. Decision-related time information resided pri-560 marily in the temporal lobe—both medial and lateral re-561 gions. Time-keeping in the medial temporal lobe fits 562 into conventional views about time encoding in this area. 563 However, we speculate that time-related computations 564 are actually more widespread, but that representations 565 in other regions (e.g., sensory cortices) were hidden due 566 to limited spatial coverage in our recordings. 567 Finally, we were able to recover high-resolution 568 outcome-aligned time information, but only leading 569 up to a planned decision. As expected, the timing 570 of unanticipated pop events could not be predicted: 571 on pop trials, any plans regarding an upcoming 572 response would be interrupted by the pop, and neural 573 signals tracking time would be aligned to some future, 574 undetermined point. 575 Taken together, our results reveal that neuronal pop-576 ulations in the human brain track behaviorally-relevant 577 time flow and encode temporally-structured information 578 that predicts upcoming decisions made under risk. 579 9
