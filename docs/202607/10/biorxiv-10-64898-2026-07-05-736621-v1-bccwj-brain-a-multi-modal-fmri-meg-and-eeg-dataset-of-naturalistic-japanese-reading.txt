Abstract 6

We present the BCCWJ-Brain dataset, a multi-modal neuroimaging resource comprising functional 7

magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), magnetoencephalography (MEG), and electroencephalogra-8

phy (EEG) data recorded from native Japanese speakers reading newspaper articles from the 9

Balanced Corpus of Contemporary Written Japanese (BCCWJ). Neural data were collected 10 

from 112 participants (36 fMRI, 35 MEG, and 41 EEG) as they read twenty newspaper articles 11 

presented in a Rapid Serial Visual Presentation (RSVP) paradigm. By providing three com-12 

plementary neuroimaging modalities collected under identical naturalistic reading stimuli, this 13 

dataset provides a cognitive benchmark for computational models such as large language models. 14 

The dataset is publicly available on the OpenNeuro platform, offering a valuable resource for 15 

neuroscience, natural language processing, and related research fields. 16 

## Background & Summary 17 

The past decade has seen rapid growth in the use of neuroimaging data as a benchmark for evaluating 18 

computational models of language, particularly large language models, LLMs, (cf. Caucheteux et al., 19 

2023, Gao et al., 2025, Goldstein et al., 2022, Schrimpf et al., 2021, among others). By comparing the 20 

internal representations derived from such models with brain activity recorded during naturalistic 21 

language comprehension, researchers have probed both the cognitive plausibility of these models 22 

and the computational principles underlying language processing in the human brain (cf. Chen and 23 

Sivakumar, 2026, Kumar et al., 2024, Lamarre et al., 2022). This comparison between human brain 24

A critical limitation of most existing datasets, however, is that they record only a single 28 

neuroimaging modality, which restricts the research questions that can be addressed. Functional 29 

MRI (fMRI) measures blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) signals with high spatial resolution but 30 

poor temporal resolution, making it well-suited to identifying where in the brain language processes 31 

occur. Electroencephalography (EEG) measures scalp electrical potentials with high temporal 32 

resolution but limited spatial precision, and is ideally suited to characterizing the temporal dynamics 33 

of language processing, such as the N400 and P600 event-related potential (ERP) components. 34 

Magnetoencephalography (MEG) has advantages in both resolutions: its high temporal resolution 35 

matches that of EEG, and its relatively high spatial resolution. Because the information that can be 36 

captured depends critically on the modality employed, a comprehensive evaluation of a computational 37 

model’s cognitive validity requires data from multiple modalities, a standard that single-modality 38 

datasets cannot meet. 39 

Several recent multimodal datasets have begun to address this gap, including the Mother of 40 

Unification Studies (MoUS) dataset (Schoffelen et al., 2019) for Dutch, the SMN4Lang dataset 41 

(Wang et al., 2022) for Mandarin Chinese, the Alice Dataset (Bhattasali et al., 2020) for English, 42 

and the Le Petit Prince Hong Kong dataset (Momenian et al., 2024) for Cantonese (Table 1). 43 

However, these datasets predominantly target spoken language comprehension, cover a restricted set 44 

of languages, and none simultaneously provides fMRI, MEG, and EEG from the same stimuli in a 45 

reading paradigm. Moreover, while existing resources span several languages including Chinese and 46 

English, Japanese remains notably absent, despite its typologically distinct properties such as its 47 

agglutinative morphology and flexible word order, which make it a particularly valuable target for 48 

neurolinguistic investigation. 49 

Here we introduce BCCWJ-Brain , a new multi-modal neuroimaging dataset designed to address 50 

these gaps. The dataset comprises fMRI, MEG, and EEG recordings from 112 native Japanese 51 

speakers who read twenty newspaper articles drawn from the BCCWJ (Maekawa et al., 2014), 52 

a widely used and linguistically well-annotated corpus. All three modalities were recorded using 53 

identical text stimuli, enabling direct cross-modal comparisons. 54 

The dataset is publicly available at OpenNeuro platform, separated in three dataset, BCCWJ-55 

fMRI (ds007752), BCCWJ-MEG (ds007763), and BCCWJ-EEG (ds007753). These datasets include 56 

112 participants’ data in total, in which each participant read twenty newspaper articles segment by 57 

segment, spanning 1,642 phrasal units (or ‘bunsetsu’ ) and 229 sentences across approximately 30 58 

minutes of stimuli. 59

Participants 61 

A total of 124 participants read twenty Japanese newspaper articles from The Balanced Corpus of 62 

Contemporary Written Japanese (BCCWJ) (Maekawa et al., 2014) during neuroimaging acquisition. 63 

Following the exclusion of participants with unavailable or unusable data, the final dataset comprises 64 

112 participants. 65 

Separate participant groups were recruited for fMRI, MEG, and EEG data collection. For 66 

fMRI, 42 participants were initially recruited; however, six were subsequently excluded; four due to 67 

incomplete data acquisition (sub-00, sub-01, sub-02, and sub-28), one due to stimulus mispresentation 68 

(sub-35), and one due to excessive head movement (sub-03), which resulted in a final fMRI sample of 69 

36 young adults (15 females; mean age = 21.28, SD = 1.68). For MEG, 41 participants were initially 70 

recruited. Six were excluded; four due to a data splitting issue (sub-05, sub-12, sub-15, and sub-26) 71 

and two due to BEM file issues (sub-13 and sub-29), resulting in a final MEG sample of 35 young 72 

adults (13 females; mean age = 21.71, SD = 2.75). For EEG, all 41 recruited participants were 73 

retained in the final sample (21 females; mean age = 20.45, SD = 2.88). Across all three modalities, 74 

participants were right-handed and self-identified as native Japanese speakers. All participants 75 

provided informed consent prior to the experiment and were compensated for their time. See Tables 76 

2 and 3 for a summary. 77 

Procedure 78 

Stimuli and Procedure 79 

BCCWJ (Maekawa et al., 2014) were used for stimuli. Each newspaper articles were segmented 80 

into phrasal units (‘ bunsetsu ’) prescribed by the National Institute for Japanese Language and 81 

Linguistics. The stimuli consist of 1642 phrasal units, 229 sentences. The duration of the experiment 82 

is around 30 minutes long for reading excluding comprehension questions sessions. Each phrasal unit 83 

was presented for 500ms in Rapid Serial Visual Presentation (RSVP) using PsychoPy (Peirce, 2007, 84 

2009), followed by a 500ms blank screen. The presentation order of the articles was randomized for 85 

each participants, for each article, one comprehension question was presented and the participants 86 

answered the yes-no questions regarding the content of the article by pressing the button on the left 87 

index finger if the answer is ‘yes’, or on the left middle finger if the answer is ‘no’. 88 

Before MEG session, we digitized the shape of participant’s faces with a FastSCAN laser scanner 89 

(Polhemus), and the five head-position coils. T1-weighted anatomical scans were collected for each 90 

participants before or after the MEG session. Likewise, T1-weighted images were collected before or 91 

after the fMRI session. 92

Preprocessing 112 

fMRI MRI files were converted from DICOM to NIfTI formats using dcm2niix. 1 The preprocessing 113 

for functional scans were done using MATLAB (MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA) and Statistical 114 

Parametric Mapping (SPM12) software. The preprocessing pipeline included motion realignment, 115 

slice timing correction, coregistration to structural images, tissue segmentation, normalization to 116 

MNI space, and smoothing with an 8 mm FWHM Gaussian kernel. 117 

MEG The raw MEG data was collected with 1,000Hz online. Continuously Adjusted least Square 118 

Method (CALM) filter (Adachi et al., 2001) were applied. Then the continuous MEG data were 119 

combined with digitized files and converted into raw.fif files for further analysis. In order to remove 120 

ocular artifacts such as eye blink, Independent Component Analysis (ICA) was applied. Then a 121 

bandpass filter was applied to 0.1-40 Hz. Data were epoched from -0.1-1,000 ms relative to word onset 122 

and baseline correction was applied using the pre-stimulus window of -0.1 to 0ms. For each subject, 123 

any channel exceeds a peak-to-peak amplitude threshold of 2e-12 Tesla is rejected automatically. 124 

Data were downsampled to 200 Hz for the further analysis to reduce computational load. 125

for removing artifacts. A 0.1-40Hz bandpass filter was applied. Then, the average of all sensors 127 

was used for re-references. Epoching time window was -0.1 to 1,000 ms, with applying baseline 128

## Data Records 133 

The BCCWJ-Brain dataset is publicly available on OpenNeuro. Data are organized according to the 134 

BIDS specification (Gorgolewski et al., 2016), including the BIDS extensions for MEG (Niso et al., 135 

2018) and EEG (Pernet et al., 2019). 136

Behavioral Results 148 

Participants answered 20 yes-no comprehension questions regarding the content of each newspaper 149 

article. For fMRI experiment participants, a mean accuracy for these comprehension questions was 150 

68% (SD = 3.6). For MEG and EEG experiments, the mean accuracies were 81% (SD =7.54) and 151 

85% (SD=6.33) respectively. 152

## Usage Notes 189 

Although we made the BCCWJ-Brain dataset publicly available, BCCWJ texts are not open source. 190 

To access the texts, please obtain a BCCWJ license; specifically, a contract for the Paid edition of 191 

the BCCWJ is required, as described at https://clrd.ninjal.ac.jp/bccwj/en/subscription.html. The 192 

script to reconstruct the text in the event files is provided on the BCCWJ platform (https://bccwj-193
