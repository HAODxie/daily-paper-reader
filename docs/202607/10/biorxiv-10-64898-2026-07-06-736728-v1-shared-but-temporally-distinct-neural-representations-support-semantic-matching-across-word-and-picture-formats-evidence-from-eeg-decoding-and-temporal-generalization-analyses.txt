## Abstract 29 

Audiovisual semantic matching can be achieved using either written words or pictures, 30 

yet whether these formats engage shared semantic matching representations with 31 

similar temporal dynamics remains unclear. We recorded electroencephalography 32 

from 27 participants while they performed audiovisual semantic matching tasks in 33 

which spoken words were paired with either written words or pictures. Stimuli 34 

included both natural and man-made objects. Time-resolved multivariate pattern 35 

analyses (MVPA or decoding), cross-decoding, and temporal generalization analyses 36 

were used to characterize the temporal dynamics of semantic processing. Reliable 37 

decoding of matching versus mismatching judgments emerged in both word and 38 

picture conditions. Decoding onset that significant above chance level occurred earlier 39 

for written words than for pictures and cross-decoding analyses revealed successful 40 

generalization between word and picture formats. Temporal generalization analyses 41 

further demonstrated distinct representational dynamics across formats, with word 42 

processing characterized by predominantly time-specific neural representations and 43 

picture processing showing more sustained and temporally stable representations. In 44 

addition, matching-related discrimination emerged earlier for natural objects than for 45 

man-made objects across both formats. The results suggest that speech-word 46 

matching shows earlier neural evidence of audiovisual alignment than speech-picture 47 

matching, potentially reflecting differences in how auditory linguistic input is 48 

integrated with visual information across representational formats. 49

## 1. Introduction 52 

In everyday life, people frequently match spoken language with information 53 

presented in other visual formats. For example, after hearing the word “apple,” one 54 

can quickly determine whether a subsequently presented written word or picture 55 

refers to the same object. This ability relies on the integration of auditory input with 56 

visual information, ultimately supporting semantic matching and object recognition 57 

(Chen & Spence, 2013; Ghuman et al., 2026; Potter & Faulconer, 1975; Theios & 58 

Amrhein, 1989). Although both written words and pictures convey conceptual 59 

information (Dirani & Pylkkänen, 2024; Ganis et al., 1996; Kutas & Federmeier, 60 

2011), they differ in representational format and may therefore engage distinct neural 61 

processing pathways. 62 

One potential source of this difference lies in how auditory and visual information 63 

are aligned across representational formats. Written words share linguistic codes with 64 

spoken words and may therefore be more readily aligned with auditory word 65 

representations. In contrast, pictures require the extraction and organization of visual 66 

object information before this visual information can be integrated with the auditory 67 

word (Dirani & Pylkkänen, 2024; Gao et al., 2026; Kutas & Federmeier, 2011). 68 

Consequently, speech–word matching may show earlier neural evidence of 69 

audiovisual alignment than speech–picture matching (Dirani & Pylkkänen, 2023; 70 

Ghazaryan et al., 2023; Rahimi et al., 2022). Importantly, such differences should not 71 

be interpreted as evidence that written words necessarily provide faster semantic 72 

access than pictures, but rather as reflecting differences in the integration of auditory 73 

and visual information across formats. 74 

Recent advances in multivariate pattern analysis (MVPA; decoding) provide a 75 

powerful framework for addressing this question. Compared with traditional 76 

event-related potential (ERP) analyses, MVPA is more sensitive to distributed patterns 77 

of neural activity and allows the characterization of how neural representations 78 

emerge, evolve, and generalize over time (Carrasco et al., 2024; Grootswagers et al., 79 

2017; Li Calzi et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). For example, some previous studies 80

In the present study, we conducted time-resolved decoding and temporal 96 

generalization analyses to investigate the temporal dynamics of semantic matching 97 

across different representational formats. Specifically, we recorded EEG signals while 98 

participants performed two cross-modal semantic matching tasks. That is, a99 

speech-word matching task and a speech-picture matching task. In each task, 100 

participants heard a spoken word and judged whether it matched a simultaneously 101 

presented written word or picture (e.g., the spoken word “apple” paired with the 102 

written word or picture “apple” or “orange”). In addition to decoding congruency 103 

semantic, we also examined whether neural activity patterns retained information 104 

about representational format (word vs. picture) even when semantic matching status 105 

was held constant. Furthermore, previous theories of semantic cognition suggest that 106 

natural and man-made objects differ in their underlying feature structure. Natural 107 

objects tend to be characterized by highly shared perceptual features, whereas 108 

man-made objects rely relatively more on functional, contextual, and associative 109 

information for conceptual representation (Hoffman et al., 2018; Ralph et al., 2017; 110

We hypothesized that (1) speech-word and speech-picture matching would both 116 

elicit reliable decoding of matching versus mismatching conditions, reflecting the 117 

emergence of shared semantic matching representations across modalities; (2) 118 

decoding onset for speech–word matching would occur earlier than speech–picture 119 

matching, indicating earlier emergence of semantic matching representations in the 120 

speech–word condition. Such differences may reflect more efficient alignment 121 

between spoken and written linguistic representations rather than differences in 122 

semantic access alone; (3) cross-decoding analyses would reveal shared neural 123 

matching representations between tasks, and we expected picture processing to 124 

exhibit broader temporal generalization than word processing; and (4) natural objects 125 

would show earlier decoding onset than artificial objects across both word and picture 126 

conditions, reflecting differences in perceptual and conceptual processing demands 127 

between object categories. 128

## 2.3 Procedure 168 

On each trial, one of four stimulus types (matching word, mismatching word, 169 

matching picture, or mismatching picture) was randomly presented at the center of the 170 

screen. Participants were instructed to place their hands on a standard QWERTY 171 

keyboard, with the left index finger resting on the "F" key and the right index finger 172 

on the "J" key. For half of the participants, the "F" key was assigned to matching 173 

stimuli and the "J" key to mismatching stimuli. For the remaining participants, the 174 

response mapping was reversed. Word in Chinese and picture stimuli were presented 175 

with equal probability (1:1), as were matching and mismatching trials. In total, 208 176 

trials were included in the experiment. 177 

Before the formal experiment, participants completed a practice session 178 

consisting of 20 trials to familiarize themselves with the task. During the formal 179 

experiment, each of the four conditions was presented 52 times, yielding 208 trials 180 

that were randomly distributed across four blocks. 181 

Each trial began with a fixation cross presented for a randomly jittered duration 182 

of 200 –500 ms, followed by the presentation of the visual stimulus (Figure 1). The 183 

auditory stimulus was presented 150 ms before the onset of the visual stimulus, 184 

following previous studies showing that this stimulus onset asynchrony helps align 185 

the timing of auditory and visual semantic activation (Chen & Spence, 2011), thereby 186 

facilitating audiovisual semantic matching. The auditory stimulus lasted 187 

approximately 550 ms. The visual stimulus remained on the screen for a maximum of 188 

1500 ms or until a response was made. Participants were instructed to determine 189 

whether the auditory and visual stimuli were semantically congruent and to respond 190 

within 1500 ms. This was followed by a blank screen with a jittered duration of 800 –191 

1000 ms. 192

To assess the temporal stability of the neural representations identified by the 256

is reinstated or maintained at later stages of processing. Generalization extending 262 

beyond the diagonal is typically interpreted as evidence for stable neural 263 

representations that persist over time, whereas decoding confined mainly to the 264 

diagonal reflects transient neural states characterized by continuously changing 265 

representational formats (King & Dehaene, 2014). 266 

## 2.6 Statistical analysis for decoding performance 267 

For statistical evaluation of decoding performance and TGM, we tested whether 268 

decoding accuracy exceeded the theoretical chance level of 50%, as commonly 269 

performed in previous ERP decoding studies (Bae & Chen, 2024; Bae & Luck, 2018). 270 

Statistical significance was assessed using a nonparametric cluster-based permutation 271 

approach similar to cluster-level mass univariate analyses widely adopted in EEG 272 

research (Maris & Oostenveld, 2007). Specifically, one-sample t-tests were conducted 273 

at each post-stimulus time point to determine whether decoding accuracy was 274 

significantly above chance for the first three decoding analyses. Because 275 

below-chance decoding is generally not interpretable for SVM classifiers, one-tailed 276 

tests were used. Adjacent time points reaching p < 0.05 were combined into temporal 277 

clusters, and the summed t-values within each cluster were used as the cluster-level 278 

statistic (t-mass). The observed cluster statistics were then compared with a null 279 

distribution generated through permutation testing to control for multiple comparisons 280 

across time. Statistical significance was determined using a family-wise alpha 281 

threshold of 0.05. 282

## 4. Discussion 360 

In the present study, we investigated the temporal dynamics of semantic 361 

matching between spoken words and visual information (i.e., written word or picture) 362 

using time-resolved decoding and temporal generalization methods. Several main 363 

findings emerged. First, reliable decoding of matching vs. mismatching trials was 364 

observed in both speech-word and speech-picture tasks. Second, matching-related 365 

decoding emerged earlier for written words than for pictures, suggesting earlier 366 

alignment between auditory linguistic input and visual word information than between 367

4.1 Behavioral performance mirrors neural decoding dynamics 377 

The behavioral findings generally converged with the decoding results (see the 378 

discussion from the following sections). Participants responded more accurately and 379 

faster in the word condition than in the picture condition, suggesting that semantic 380 

judgments were easier when information was presented as written words. This finding 381 

is broadly consistent with evidence that word and picture processing engage partially 382 

distinct routes to conceptual knowledge, with semantic information becoming 383 

available at different stages across modalities (Dirani & Pylkkänen, 2023). Whereas 384 

written words share linguistic representational codes with spoken words, pictures 385 

require mapping between auditory linguistic input and visual object representations 386 

before semantic congruency can be evaluated. Consequently, the longer response 387 

times observed for pictures likely reflect additional visual object processing and 388 

audiovisual integration demands, rather than slower semantic access per se (Ghuman 389 

et al., 2026). 390 

Reaction times were also shorter for matching than mismatching trials, indicating 391 

a behavioral advantage for semantically congruent information. Such congruency 392 

facilitation effects are consistent with findings showing that semantically consistent 393 

information is integrated more efficiently, whereas incongruent information requires 394 

prolonged semantic evaluation and integration processes (Krugliak et al., 2024). The 395 

absence of a significant Format×Matching interaction indicates that the magnitude of 396 

the semantic congruency benefit was statistically comparable across word and picture 397

in their processing routes and temporal dynamics (Dirani & Pylkkänen, 2023, 2024). 400 

Thus, semantic congruency may exert its facilitative influence on a common 401 

conceptual system regardless of input format, even though the neural processes 402 

supporting auditory–visual alignment and integration differ across modalities. 403 

4.2 Earlier semantic matching in the speech-word condition: the role of 404 

cross-modal representational alignment 405 

A key finding of the present study is that matching-related information became 406 

decodable earlier in the speech-word condition than in the speech-picture condition. 407 

We interpret this temporal advantage as reflecting more efficient alignment between 408 

auditory word representations and visual word information, rather than faster 409 

semantic access for written words per se (Ghuman et al., 2026; Ralph et al., 2017). 410 

Written words share linguistic representational codes with spoken words, whereas 411 

pictures require visual object information to be extracted and organized before it can 412 

be integrated with auditory linguistic input (Grill-Spector & Weiner, 2014; Martin, 413 

2016). Thus, the delayed onset in the picture condition may reflect additional 414 

visual-to-auditory integration demands during semantic matching. 415 

This interpretation aligns with dual coding theory (Bi, 2021), which proposes 416 

that words and pictures are processed via partially distinct representational systems 417 

(verbal vs. imagery-based systems). Recent extensions and empirical work further 418 

support this distinction, showing differential contributions of verbal and non-verbal 419 

codes to memory and semantic integration (Monzel et al., 2022). According to this 420 

framework, pictures typically engage richer perceptual and imaginal representations, 421 

which may require additional processing before they can be aligned with auditory 422 

linguistic input during semantic matching. In contrast, written words share 423 

representational characteristics with spoken language and may therefore facilitate 424 

more rapid audiovisual alignment. 425 

Complementary support comes from hub-and-spoke models of semantic 426 

cognition, in which modality-specific perceptual systems (“spokes”) interact with 427

4.3 Cross-decoding and partially shared semantic representations 434 

The cross-decoding results demonstrated successful generalization across word 435 

and picture formats (see Figure 3b), indicating the presence of shared neural 436 

representations underlying semantic matching (Bezsudnova et al., 2024; Giari et al., 437 

2020). Specifically, classifiers trained on one modality were able to decode match 438 

versus mismatch in the other modality, albeit with slightly shifted temporal profiles. 439 

This finding provides evidence that partially shared neural information contributes to 440 

semantic matching across both linguistic and visual input streams. Importantly, the 441 

successful cross-decoding observed between speech-word and speech-picture 442 

conditions suggests that both tasks ultimately converged on partially shared semantic 443 

representations, even though the timing of representational alignment differed across 444 

formats. 445 

Such results are consistent with distributed theories of conceptual knowledge, 446 

which propose that semantic matching representations are not tied to a single input 447 

modality but instead emerge from interactions among multiple modality-specific 448 

systems. Successful cross-decoding therefore suggests that word and picture 449 

processing recruit partially overlapping neural codes during semantic matching. 450

representational similarity analysis have similarly demonstrated that semantic 452 

categories and object identity can be decoded across visual and linguistic formats, 453 

supporting the idea of partially shared conceptual coding across modalities (e.g., 454

al., 2019; Li Calzi et al., 2025). Importantly, the present results extend these findings 456 

by showing that such shared representations are not only modality-independent but 457

4.4 Temporal generalization patterns: dynamic vs. stable representations 474 

Temporal generalization analysis further revealed striking differences between 475 

word and picture processing. Word decoding was primarily closed to the diagonal of 476 

the TGM matrices, indicating that neural representations were highly time-specific 477 

and rapidly changing over time. In contrast, picture decoding exhibited broad 478 

off-diagonal generalization, suggesting more temporally stable and sustained neural 479 

representations (King & Dehaene, 2014). Such rapidly evolving representations are 480 

consistent with a sequence of processing stages that may include orthographic, lexical, 481 

and semantic operations. 482 

This dissociation suggests that word processing may rely on rapid transitions 483 

among orthographic and linguistic representations that can be efficiently aligned with 484 

auditory input, resulting in neural patterns that evolve quickly over time (Hauk et al., 485 

2006). In contrast, picture processing likely involves the gradual accumulation and 486 

integration of visual object information before stable auditory–visual matching 487

4.5 Natural vs. artificial objects: category-dependent temporal dynamics 493 

We further found that natural objects elicited earlier decoding onset than artificial 494 

objects across both word and picture formats. These findings suggest that object 495 

category may modulate the speed with which visual information can be integrated 496 

with auditory linguistic input during semantic matching. One possible explanation is 497 

that natural objects (e.g., fruits and animals) possess more perceptually coherent and 498 

highly shared feature structures, whereas artificial objects are more strongly defined 499 

by functional and associative properties (Hebart et al., 2020; Ralph et al., 2017). Such 500 

differences may contribute to the later emergence of semantic discrimination observed 501 

for artificial objects. 502 

This interpretation is consistent with contemporary semantic cognition 503 

frameworks, which propose that conceptual representations emerge from interactions 504 

between modality-specific perceptual systems and transmodal semantic hubs, with 505 

natural categories relying relatively more on perceptual feature statistics and artifact 506 

categories relying more heavily on functional knowledge and contextual associations 507 

(Ralph et al., 2017). Furthermore, recent EEG decoding and representational 508 

similarity studies have shown that object-category information emerges rapidly during 509 

visual processing and that neural representational geometry is systematically shaped 510 

by semantic category structure (Hebart et al., 2023; Iamshchinina et al., 2022; 511 

Wamain et al., 2023; Weber et al., 2024). These representational properties may 512 

contribute to the earlier decoding onset observed for natural objects in the present 513 

study. 514

First, the study focused on healthy young adults using a controlled EEG paradigm, 517

cognitive neuroscience, it may not capture potentially nonlinear dynamics of semantic 535 

processing (Hosseini et al., 2021). Future work could explore deep learning-based 536 

decoding models or nonlinear representational analyses to further characterize 537 

semantic dynamics (Craik et al., 2019). 538

experimental control but may not fully capture the continuous and dynamic nature of 542 

real-world language processing (Alday, 2019; Brodbeck & Simon, 2020). Moreover, 543 

explicit matching judgments likely recruit additional attentional, response-selection, 544 

and decision-making mechanisms that can contribute to late positive neural activity 545 

beyond semantic processing itself (S. Luck, 2014; Polich, 2007). Future studies 546 

should therefore examine semantic matching under more naturalistic conditions, such 547
