Abstract 13 Large-scale functional MRI datasets provide resources to understand inter-individual variation in 14 human brain function and relate this variation to behaviour and health. However, most existing 15 approaches fail to bridge the gap between population-average and individual-specific modelling, 16 limiting the identification of structured subgroup heterogeneity across individuals. Here we 17 develop a scalable framework for unsupervised subgroup discovery in population-scale resting-18 state fMRI data from 19,993 UK Biobank participants. Using stochastic Probabilistic Functional 19 Modes, we estimate population-informed individualised spatial topographies of resting-state 20 networks and derive high-dimensional functional fingerprints for each participant. We then 21 identify latent subgroups by applying Gaussian mixture modelling independently to each 22 fingerprint feature, yielding hundreds of reproducible subgroup definitions across 1,000 23 functional dimensions. We report approximately 5,700 significant differences between 24 subgroups in a range of non-imaging phenotypes related to cognition, lifestyle, physical and 25 mental health. Spatial organisation of the brain networks reveals distinct subgroup differences 26 in sensory-motor and higher-order cognitive systems, in addition to correspondence with 27 regional patterns of genetic variability across the brain. Together, these results demonstrate that 28 large-scale functional neuroimaging contains rich latent subgroup structure linked to behavioural 29 and biological variation. Our framework provides an interpretable and scalable basis for stratified 30 models of human brain function and population neuroscience. 31

# 1 Introduction 32 

Functional MRI provides non-invasive recordings of brain activity and connectivity. It has been 33 instrumental in characterising various modes of human brain function, e.g., attention, language, 34 sensorimotor, and the default mode. Each mode is a system of interconnected voxels and/or 35 regions that work in synchrony, such that a single time course can be used to summarise their 36 activity. Functional modes have been characterised during cognitive tasks as well as the intrinsic 37 “resting state” brain activity (a.k.a. resting state networks, RSNs), and structured differences in 38 brain disorders have been reported (Biswal et al., 1995; Buckner and Vincent, 2007; Calhoun et 39 al., 2008; Damoiseaux et al., 2006; Raichle et al., 2001). With growing large-scale brain imaging 40 datasets, e.g., Human Connectome Project (Smith et al., 2013), UK Biobank (Miller et al., 2016) 41 and ABCD (Casey et al., 2018), neuroimaging research has been given access to open resources 42 to examine the brain at population scales. This is fundamentally different from conventional 43 neuroimaging datasets, where small homogeneous cohorts are available (Bijsterbosch et al., 44 2018; Button et al., 2013; Szucs and Ioannidis, 2020). Here, we exploit the power of big data, and 45 the new generation of individualised brain function mapping techniques, to characterise axes of 46 population variations and emergent subgroups based on 19,993 subjects in UK Biobank. 47 Brain function, and its variability, have been conventionally characterised for population 48 averages, to understand “an average brain” (Samanez-Larkin and D’Esposito, 2008; Woolrich et 49 al., 2025), and to compare the average brains across various cohorts, e.g., dementia patients vs 50 age-matched healthy adults (Verdi et al., 2021). Therefore, while these studies have been 51 important in understanding the mechanistic and consensus patterns of changes in various 52 disorders, cross-individual variability of the brain function in health and diseases remain poorly 53 understood. This has been partly due to the limitations of the neuroimaging datasets, where only 54 small cohorts of < 50 people were available (Szucs and Ioannidis, 2020), and partly due to the 55 limitation of the conventional brain connectivity modelling techniques being built on models of 56 group averages (Calhoun et al., 2001; Yeo et al., 2011). 57

Using these new tools, growing evidence has shown that functional modes vary across 60 individuals, and that they can be predictive of traits and diseases. However, these studies have 61 predominantly focussed on “functional connectivity” between the modes. More specifically, 62 after defining functional modes as either distributed systems (e.g., Default Mode or Dorsal 63 attention), or localised parcels in atlases (e.g., Schaefer (Schaefer et al., 2018) or HCP’s 64 Multimodal (Glasser et al., 2016) Parcellations), it has often been of interest to find temporal 65 correlations between the timecourses of these modes, and to use these functional connectivity 66 features as personalised fingerprints, and in phenotype prediction pipelines (Bijsterbosch et al., 67 2018; Finn et al., 2015; He et al., 2022). 68 Importantly, however, cross-individual variability in brain function is not limited to temporal 69 features – it can also be reflected in the spatial organisation of the functional modes (Bijsterbosch 70 et al., 2018; Harrison et al., 2020). When modelled accurately for every individual person, the 71 spatial organisation of the modes has been shown to be associated with, and to be predictive of, 72 cognition, emotion, lifestyle, and health. Recent studies have provided convincing evidence for 73 this: these include hierarchical parcellation schemes that yield individualised atlases of the brain 74 (i.e., “hard parcellations”), as well as hierarchical matrix factorisation schemes that yield 75 individualised RSNs (i.e., “soft parcellations”) (Farahibozorg et al., 2025; Kong et al., 2019). 76 In this study, we build on these recent advances ( Figure 1 ), in particular using stochastic 77 Probabilistic Functional Modes (sPROFUMO) (Farahibozorg et al., 2021) on 19,993 subjects from 78 UK Biobank to: a) characterise continuous fingerprints, i.e., axes of population variations in the 79 spatial organisation of the brain’s functional modes; b) identify reproducible latent subgroups 80 based on non-Gaussianities in these fingerprint features, and assess the differences between 81 these subgroups. # 2 Materials and Methods 110 

## 2.1 Data 111 

2.1.1 UK Biobank (UKB) 112 

Twenty thousand (20,000) subjects were randomly drawn from the May 2019 release of the UK 113 Biobank (UKB) data (application number 8107, all participants are over 45 years old). North West 114 Multi-centre Research Ethics Committee (MREC) has provided ethical approval to UKB for data 115 collection and sharing (http://www.ukbiobank.ac.uk/ethics/), and by extension, these ethical 116 regulations cover our study. All participants completed written informed consent prior to being 117 scanned. Resting state fMRI data in UKB consists of 1 recording session with TR = 0.735s, 490 (or 118 530) time points per session, providing ~6 minutes of recording per participant. The data used 119 here was in volumetric space and it was preprocessed using UKB’s standard preprocessing 120 pipeline (Alfaro-Almagro et al., 2018). The preprocessing steps consisted of quality control (QC), 121 brain extraction, motion correction, artefact rejection using FSL-FIX, and high-pass temporal 122 filtering (Gaussian-weighted least-squares straight line fitting with sigma of 50.0s). Subsequently, 123 data were registered to standard 2mm MNI space. According to our previous investigations into 124 subject-specific modelling of functional modes (Farahibozorg et al., 2021), and to improve the 125 SNR of the data, we applied an additional spatial smoothing to obtain a final 5mm FWHM 126 smoothness (versus with the original 2mm in UKB pipeline). 127

2.2.1.2 Inference and application details 158 

Stochastic Variational Bayes (sVB) is used as the inference technique in sPROFUMO. Specifically, 159 during the inference, subjects from big data are divided into small batches, and model 160 parameters are optimised to obtain an approximate distribution, 𝑞 , that is as close as possible to 161 the true posterior. The group model parameters, as Bayesian priors, are used for top-down 162 regularisation of subject model parameters within each batch, and posterior distributions are 163 summed and fed back to obtain an updated group model. Importantly, group-level parameters 164 are treated as “global” parameters that are continuously updated over batches, and the model 165 iterates between group and subject updates until convergence. Default parameters, as described 166 in (Farahibozorg et al., 2021) were used for application to UKB: forget rate ( 𝛽 ) = 0.6, to specify 167 global parameter updates relying on the current compared with previous batches. Delay 168 parameter ( 𝜏 ) = 5, to specify the contribution of the initial batches to the overall inference. Batch 169 size of 50 subjects was used, and each subject was normally picked in 2 to 3 batches (2.5 on 170 average), and the overall group model was updated 5,000 times. 171 sPROFUMO’s internal initialisation applies a few preprocessing steps to the UKB-processed rfMRI 172 data, including voxel-wise de-meaning and variance normalisation. The first step of Bayesian 173 model initialisation involves the estimation of a set of initial maps to start the inference from a 174 realistic set of group-level parameters (i.e., to start the model with reasonable priors). These 175 initial maps are estimated using MELODIC’s Incremental Group-PCA (MIGP) (Smith et al., 2014) 176 followed by variational ICA, both of which are implemented internally within s/PROFUMO 177 software. Subsequently, the group model is initialised using hyperpriors and the initial maps, and 178 used for regularisation of the matrix factorisation that is conducted at subject level, as elaborated 179 earlier, to start the full stochastic VB inference. 180

In the Results section, we use Kurtosis and Skewness to characterise non-Gaussianities 260 in each fingerprint feature. Kurtosis = 0 indicates a Gaussian distribution while Kurtosis > 0 and < 261 0 would indicate super-Gaussian and sub-Gaussian distributions, respectively. Skewness = 0 262 indicates a Gaussian distribution while Skewness > 0 and < 0 represent long tails on the right-263 hand and left-hand side, respectively. 264

We 583 estimated shared variances for each pairwise comparison by computing correlations between 584 the corresponding canonical covariates within the held-out test sets, and averaging those 585 correlations across folds. These results are shown in Figure 3 b. 586 The correlation values for the top-matched CCA components of the features-only CCA, 587 subgroups-only CCA and both concatenated CCA, respectively, were as follows: 1) cognitive 588 nIDPs: 0.178, 0.196 and 0.172; 2) mental health nIDPs: 0.096, 0.132 and 0.111; 3) alcohol nIDPs: 589 0.099, 0.108 and 0.091; 4) tobacco nIDPs: 0.122, 0.121 and 0.107; 5) cardiovascular nIDPs: 0.188, 590 0.303 and 0.248; 6) bone health nIDPs: 0.130, 0.199 and 0.189. 591 Therefore, for five of the six phenotype categories – cognitive, alcohol, cardiovascular, bone and 592 mental health – subgroup modelling provided stronger brain-phenotype associations than 593 continuous fingerprint features alone and combining the two did not further improve 594 performance. This finding suggests that the discrete subgroup representation captures 595 behaviourally relevant population structure more effectively than continuous fingerprints, and 596 potentially reduces noise and redundant variability. 597

## 3.4 Genetic relevance of fingerprints and subgroups 651 

Having established the link between subgroups and the individualised spatial organisation of 652 brain networks, as well as their link to phenotypes, we next examined the relationship between 653 subgroups and genetics. Demonstrating such correspondence would provide an independent 654 biological validation of the latent subgroup structure identified using unsupervised methods from 655 fMRI. To address this, we first identified genetic variants associated with the continuous 656 fingerprint features (using Genome Wide Association Studies (GWAS)) and then tested whether 657 spatial patterns of subgroup variability corresponded to regional patterns of genetic variation 658 across functional brain networks. # 4 Discussion 711 

Using individualised functional brain topographies from 19,993 UK Biobank participants, we 712 demonstrate that large-scale functional neuroimaging contains reproducible latent subgroup 713 structure that bridges population-average and individual-specific models of the brain’s functional 714 organisation. To reveal this, we developed a scalable framework that builds on stochastic 715 Probabilistic Functional Modes (sPROFUMO) (Farahibozorg et al., 2021), combined with two 716 unsupervised fingerprint extraction approaches (bigFLICA and subjectICA) and feature-wise (1D) 717 Gaussian Mixture Modelling to identify multiple subgroup definitions across distinct axes of 718 functional variation. Importantly, unlike conventional approaches based on population-average 719 models or temporal functional connectivity, our framework uses individualised spatial 720 

topographies of functional brain networks to characterise latent population structure. Across 721 19,993 participants, we identified 1,000 fingerprint features and hundreds of reproducible 722 subgroup definitions that were associated with ~5,700 significant phenotype differences, 723 exhibited distinct organisation across sensory-motor and higher-order cognitive systems, and 724 showed correspondence with regional patterns of genetic variation across the brain. 725 Subgroup modelling based on large-scale population imaging can take a significant step towards 726 a core aim of fMRI research: providing biomarkers of traits and disease while helping bridge the 727 gap between population neuroimaging and precision medicine (Demeter and Greene, 2025; 728 Parkes et al., 2020). Specifically, conventional neuroimaging datasets with small sample sizes 729 typically included homogeneous cohorts (Button et al., 2013; Szucs and Ioannidis, 2020). As a 730 result, conventional fMRI modelling techniques were designed to analyse such datasets, and 731 therefore relied on population average models to identify consensus patterns within groups 732 (Calhoun et al., 2001; Yeo et al., 2011), rather than the structured heterogeneity that becomes 733 apparent in large-scale datasets. 734 However, compelling evidence accumulated over recent years has established structured and 735 meaningful patterns of individual variability in brain function and connectivity (Gordon et al., 736

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted July 19, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.07.17.739129 doi: bioRxiv preprint

37 2017; Kong et al., 2019). This has led to extensive research into fMRI-based fingerprinting (Finn 737 et al., 2015). However, to fully exploit the potential of large heterogeneous populations in 738 modelling various sources of subject variability, it is important to bridge the gap between 739 population-average and individual-specific modelling – this can be achieved using stratified 740 methods. Subgroup modelling, as proposed in the current paper, takes a step towards bridging 741 this gap. Specifically, using multiple phenotype categories – cognition, mental health, alcohol and 742 tobacco use, bone and cardiovascular health – we identified thousands of highly significant 743 differences between the data-driven subgroups. This provides a proof-of-principle that 744 incorporating latent subgroup structure into models of brain function captures biologically 745 meaningful population heterogeneity and may provide a richer foundation for future 746 individualised fMRI biomarkers. 747 Subgroup modelling has been used in previous fMRI literature for applications such as brain 748 disorder classification (Du et al., 2018) and disease subtyping (Brucar et al., 2023; Miranda et al., 749 2021). However, several challenges hinder the generalisability and reproducibility of such 750 approaches, including limited sample sizes or the lower quality of clinical datasets. Large-scale 751 high-quality datasets of thousands of healthy participants will, by definition, include hundreds of 752 potential clinical cohorts in pre-diagnosis stages. For example, by 2027, in the imaged cohort of 753 the UK Biobank, 6,000, 4,000 and 2,800 participants are expected to develop Alzheimer’s disease, 754 stroke and Parkinson’s, respectively (Miller et al., 2016). Therefore, these datasets are not only 755 useful for characterising healthy populations at scale but also provide a solid foundation for 756 modelling clinical data. Specifically, for Bayesian methods such as sPROFUMO, UK Biobank can 757 provide informative “priors” for subsequent disease classification and subtyping, whereby the 758 state-of-the-art transfer learning methods can be used to inform disease classification and 759 subtyping in any new clinical data, based on similarities to the subpopulations that were 760 identified in the current study. 761 The approach presented here is based on individualised spatial topographies of the brain’s 762 functional networks. Compared with functional connectivity, spatial maps remain a much less 763

Functional 766 connectivity analysis computes temporal correlations between the brain networks (or parcels), 767 and as such, it cannot be used to “localise” the network anomalies. This hinders biological 768 interpretability of the functional connectivity-based biomarkers. In contrast, in this study we used 769 fully individualised spatial maps to obtain voxel-wise maps of subgroup differences (MSD), as 770 shown in Figure 4 . Interestingly, we found distinct MSDs for sensory-motor versus cognitive 771 networks, whereby MSDs related to visual, auditory and motor networks were highly similar to 772 each other, and distinct from MSDs related to the default mode, fronto-parietal, ventral attention 773 and language networks, and vice versa. This block structure in the spatial correlations between 774 MSDs suggests that subgroup variability is organised at the level of meta-networks rather than 775 individual functional networks. 776 This meta-network organisation aligns with previous investigations into amplitudes of the brain 777 networks (Bijsterbosch et al., 2017) as well as in the dynamic states (referred to as meta-states 778 in (Vidaurre et al., 2017)). This finding can be interpreted in the context of the growing evidence 779 that inter-individual variability in functional brain organisation is anatomically-informed (rather 780 than random or uniform), and thus differs between sensory-motor and associative systems (Finn 781 et al., 2015; Mueller et al., 2013). For sensory-motor networks, the areas that we found in this 782 study, including subcortical, cerebellar, and parieto-occipital regions, reflect the relatively hard-783 wired developmental trajectories and structural-functional connectivity that characterise the 784 unimodal brain regions (Baum et al., 2020; Guell et al., 2018). In contrast, we found frontal and 785 parietal association cortices highlighted in the MSDs related to the cognitive networks -786 consistent with these regions maturing later during development, showing higher synaptic 787 plasticity, and greater inter-individual variability in functional connectivity (Mueller et al., 2013; 788 Sydnor et al., 2021). This is broadly consistent with the cortical hierarchy framework, where 789 transmodal association areas are thought to be less constrained by genetics and more sensitive 790

Together, these results 791 suggest that subgroup difference maps identified by our unsupervised techniques reflect 792 biologically meaningful axes of population variability, where the spatial organisation of these 793 MSDs is broadly aligned with the well-known functional organisation of the cortex. 794 The genetic analysis provides independent evidence supporting the biological relevance of these 795 subgroup differences. As well as identifying genetic loci associated with continuous fingerprint 796 features, we found clear spatial correspondence between maps of subgroup differences and 797 regional patterns of genetic variation across the same functional networks. Although these 798 spatial associations do not imply causal mechanisms, they suggest that the spatial organisation 799 of subgroup variability is partly aligned with the genetic architecture underlying functional brain 800 organisation, complementing the behavioural and spatial evidence presented here. 801 Beyond characterising normative population variability, the subgroup organisation identified 802 here may also have implications for understanding disease vulnerability. The prefrontal and 803 parietal association cortex, subcortex, and cerebellum that were found here as the main zones 804 of cross-subgroup differences, have all been implicated in various neuropsychiatric conditions 805 (Goodkind et al., 2015; Sha et al., 2019), raising the possibility that the same pathways that 806 accommodate subgroup diversity in healthy populations may also constitute points of 807 vulnerability in diseases. More broadly, these findings provide a framework for investigating 808 heterogeneity in both healthy populations and clinical cohorts using latent subgroup structure 809 derived from individualised functional brain organisation. 810
