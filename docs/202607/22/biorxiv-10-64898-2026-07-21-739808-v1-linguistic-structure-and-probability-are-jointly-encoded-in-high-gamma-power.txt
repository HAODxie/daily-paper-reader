Abstract 1 During speech comprehension, the brain dynamically infers a hierarchy of increasingly abstract representations 2 from the sensory input. An important step in the inferential hierarchy is the combination of words to form phrases 3 and sentences. Whether this process is driven primarily by statistical patterns in the linguistic input, or by a 4 mechanism that combines words into hierarchical representations, is a subject of considerable debate that has 5 regained importance with the arrival of large language models. This study investigates whether local cortical 6 activity (high gamma power; 70 -150 Hz) from intracranial recordings is jointly modulated by lexical probability 7 and syntactic structure; and whether lexical probability affects the inference of syntactic structure. To this end, an 8 open dataset of electroco rticography recordings is analyzed with multivariate temporal response functions and a 9 model comparison approach. The results indicate that high gamma power is sensitive to multi -word estimates of 10  constituency structure and lexical probability estimates, both in isolation and jointly. The temporal response 11  functions suggest that syntactic structure building depends on interregional communication between regions 12  connected through dorsal - and ventral streams. Furthermore, the study provides evidence that bottom -up syntactic 13  information is less likely to be encoded by neural populations that strongly code for lexical probability measures, 14  while top -down syntactic information shares neural resources with lexical uncertainty. We suggest that lexical 15  uncertainty modulates the weighting of anticipatory structural information. With this, the current study supports 16  models that suggest that cues are leveraged flexibly in a feed -forward and feed -back fashion during speech 17  comprehension .18

1.  Introduction 22  During language comprehension, the brain infers a hierarchy of increasingly abstract representations from the 23  sensory input , from phonemes to the meaning of the message . This is viewed as a dynamical process in which 24  representations can cue each other both in a feed -forward and feed -back manner (1,2) . An important step in the 25  inferential hierarchy is the combination of words to create interpretable phrases and sentences . High frequency 26  neural activity in the gamma band (70 -150 Hz) has been suggested to play a critical role in this process (2 –9) .27  Whether it is driven primarily by the distributional statistics of tokens in linguistic inpu t, or by a mechanism that 28  combines words into abstract hierarchical representation s is a subject of considerable debate (10 –15) . In this study ,29  therefore, we explore whether these sources of information are jointly represented within high gamma power (70 -30  150 Hz) during spoken language comprehension .31 

# 32  1.1.  Lexical probability and syntactic structure in neural dynamics 33  There is much evidence for the separate and joint use and modulation of neural activity by statistical and structural 34  information (for example, 15 –20) . In a seminal study, Ding and colleagues (21) showed that neural activity reflects 35  linguistic structure by displaying a power -increase that the rate of occurrence of that unit. In that study, results 36  revealed a 1 Hz power increase as a response to the occurrence of four -word sentences and a 2 Hz increase for 37  two -word phrases – with the (monosyllabic) words presented at a 4 Hz rate. Subsequently, Batterink and Paller 38  (22) showed that similar signatures of representations can be elicited on the basis of transitional probability alone. 39  In a statistical learning paradigm, the authors presented participants with two conditions of syllables; a random 40  condition, and a condition in which syllables could form trisyllabic ‘non -words’ based on the transitional 41  probability between syllables. The results revealed that power at 1.1 Hz – the rate of occurrence of the non -words 42  – increased as the participants learned the transitional probabilities in the input .43  These sets of finding s together indicate that the brain creates latent representations using both statistical 44  patterns in the input (probability) and knowledge of the structure of language (syntax) . The studies show that 45  statistical patterns and linguistic information can exist and be leveraged in isolation. In naturalistic contexts, 46  however, linguistic structure and statistical patterns (probability) are related : the presence of linguistic structure 47  drives specific statistical regularities in language (14,23) , and these statistical regularities can help the perceiver 48  to find structure in the input (24 –28) . Indeed, MEG studies have shown that slow neural dynamics jointly encode 49  structural and statistical properties (15,29) and that these two types of information can interact: lexical probability 50  affects the time -course of structure building processes captured by delta -band activity , suggesting that probability 51  plays a role in structure building (30) ; and the representation of lexical probability in the neural signal is affected 52  by the syntactic status of a word (31) . This set of findings indicates that probabilistic and syntactic information 53  may be exploited dynamically during speech comprehension (14) .54  Besides low -frequency activity, the high gamma band (70 -150 Hz) has been proposed to play an important role in 55  speech processing (32) and structure building in particular (2 –9) . Activity in this frequency band is commonly 56  assumed to reflect the average firing rates and synchrony of the local neural population (33 –35) . Concretely , high 57  gamma power (HGP) has been suggested to reflect the active process of binding (6) , the semantic composition of 58  language -specific concepts (3,9) , and the application of grammatical rules or stored lexical knowledge to infer a 59  higher -level structure (2,36) . Any of these processes may depend on or elicit modulations of patterns of inhibition ,60  a process to which gamma activity broadly has been related (2,37,38) .61  Indeed, HGP can reflect syntactic structure. Ding and colleagues (21) also analysed power in the high 62  gamma band (70 -200 Hz) recorded with electrocorticography (ECoG). This analysis revealed that HGP responded 63  similarly to the sentences, phrases, and word lists as low -frequency activity, namely: by an increase in the 64  amplitude of the HGP signal at the frequency corresponding to the rate of occurrence of the linguistic unit in 65  electrodes in the middle and posterior superior temporal gyrus (STG) and left inferior frontal gyrus (IFG). And 66  similarly to the effects shown with M/EEG , subsequent studies revealed that power increases in HGP at the 67  corresponding rate of occurrence can be elicited using a classical, syllable -based statistical learning paradigm , too 68  (39,40) . This suggests that HGP plays a role (either causal or consequential) in both syntactic structure building 69  and in segmentation using transitional probability information. 70

# 112  1.3.  Surpris al and entropy in high gamma power 113  Besides  linguistic structure,  Nelson et al.  (43) also considered effects of trigram lexical probability measures on 114  HGP , namely surprisal and entropy . Surprisal is the (log) probability of a word given the context (two words in 115  the case of a trigram model). If surprisal is high, the word was unexpected (either unpredictable or unpredicted) .116  Entropy is the weighted average of all surprisal values of the words that can appear in the target position and 117  captures uncertainty. High entropy means high uncertainty. Nelson and colleagues showed that entropy was 118  inversely related to HGP in superior and middle temporal areas; in other words, HGP decreased for increased 119  uncertainty. In the same areas , there was a positive effect of surprisal, which means that HGP increased as word 120  probability decreases. Adding surprisal and entropy as cofactors did not change the effects for the syntactic 121  predictors, providing the first evidence that these factors are jointly modulating local , high frequency neural 122  activity. 123  There have since been analyses of linguistic encoding in high gamma band activity using surprisal and 124  entropy (and other measures ) obtained from large language models (LLMs) as the features of interest (49 –52) .125  These estimates are frequently taken to reflect probabilistic processing in the human brain (53) . By analyzing a 126  dataset of naturalistic language comprehension, Goldstein and colleagues (50) showed that HGP (70 -200 Hz )127  during language comprehension can be modeled by a combination of entropy and surprisal derived from LLMs 128  such as GPT2 . The correlations between the probability values and cortical activity were largest just before word 129  onset for entropy , and at 400 milliseconds post word onset for surprisal . These effects were found in superior 130  temporal and inferior frontal regions. Importantly, u sing the same dataset , Zou and colleagues (31) showed that 131  probability and syntax are not encoded independently. By dividing words based on the position in a phrase – either 132  ph rase -initial or not –, the authors showed that the correlation between surprisal and high gamma activity 133  interacted with the syntactic status of a word. Specifically, the correlation between GPT2 -derived surprisal and 134  high gamma activity was lower for phrase -initial words than for phrase -internal words. These effects existed in 135  STG and IFG. 136

# 226  2.4.  Temporal Response Functions 227  To estimate how HGP relates to syntactic and probabilistic processes , we used (multivariate) temporal response 228  functions (TRFs) to model the impact of a set of syntactic and lexical properties on the signal recorded at each 229  electrode. In essence, TRFs are a multivariate linear regression approach. Using annotations of multiple aspects 230  of the linguistic stimulus as features (see below), the TRF allows us to estimate how various aspects of language 231  affect neural activity. The multivariate approach enables the estimation of effects for each feature separately . By 232  lagging the features in time relative to the continuous neural signal, the TRF estimates modulation of these features 233  at different points in time. The model returns a time -course of coefficients reflecting the encoding of a feature in 234  the neural signal across time , in the present case, at the level of individual electrodes . In other words, an increase 235  at a given time -lag for a feature reflects an increase in the associated brain response to this feature, at that electrode ,236  and at the given time -lag after stimulus onset. Each feature has its own associated time -course of coefficients. 237  The equation (1) of the linear model is as follows: 238

# 269  2.5.  TRF feature design 270  To distinguish several sources of variance in the signal stemming from language comprehension, we constructed 271  seven experimental features from the linguistic stimulus in three categories: base features, which were present in 272  every model and are used to account for variance that could affect the results; and two sets of experimental 273  features, namely lexical probability and syntactic features. Prior to the construction of the features, the provided 274  transcription of the Dutch text was checked and corrected where necessary. This led to a slight reduction in the 275  total number of words in the stimulus (N = 442 instead of N = 447). The word onset time -points provided with 276  the dataset were adjusted accordingly. 277 

# 278  2.5.1.  Base features 279  We constructed three base features: speech envelope , word onset , and word frequency .280  The speech envelope feature was added to capture variance from early auditory processing to separate it 281  from processing at the lexical and syntactic level. The speech envelope was computed by taking the absolute value 282  of the Hilbert transform for each speech segment, and resampling this to 128 Hz to match the sampling rate of the 283  (preprocessed) ECoG data. 284  The word onset feature was added to capture any response time -locked to word onset that was not 285  accounted for by any of the other (more informative) features. To create the word onset feature, we used word 286  onset time -points to create a time -course of zeros with values of one inserted at the sample that corresponded to 287  the onset of a word. These time -series were convolved with a Gaussian kernel with a standard deviation of 15ms ,288  for two reasons. Firstly, this accounts for any temporal inaccuracy of or uncertainty about the exact word onset 289  time -points. Secondly, this process ensures that the feature requires a degree of regularization similar to the 290  continuous speech envelope feature by increasing its autocorrelation. 291  The word frequency feature was used to capture decontextualized lexical processing. Like the word onset 292  feature, this feature consisted of a time -series of zeros with values inserted at word onset. The values in this case 293  were Zipf word frequency values obtained from the wordfreq package (63, version 3.1.1) with the zipf_frequency 294  function for Dutch (see Van Heuven et al. (64) for the use of the Zipf metric). The resulting feature was equally 295  convolved with a Gaussian kernel with a standard deviation of 15ms. Figure 2 shows an example of the resulting 296  features. 297

For entropy, we set the value of the first word to 0. Conversely , the last word of a 320  segment had a context of all the preceding words that contributed to surprisal and entropy estimates. 321

2.5.3.  Syntactic features 334  To create syntactic features, we required a syntactic representation of the stimulus. All sentences were manually 335  parsed for constituency structure using a simplified minimalist framework. This entailed drawing a full X -bar 336  structure for noun phrases (NP) and verb phrases (VP), but not for other phrases unless the intermediate 337  projections were filled. Using the full X -bar structure for NPs and VPs ensured proper distinction between 338  arguments and adjuncts, with arguments being attached as a sister of the head of a phrase, and adjuncts occupying 339  the intermediate projection. See Figure 2 for the parse of an example sentence. 340  From the resulting structures we extracted node counts. Node counts provide an indication of the number 341  of syntactic operations that are performed at the position of a given word. These estimates have been shown to 342  explain variance in several types of neural recordings, such as fMRI and MEG (18,30,67,68) . To count the nodes 343  that are built for a word, we must assume a parsing algorithm. We use d two algorithms : a top -down algorithm 344  (hereafter : “top -down ”) and a bottom -up algorithm ( hereafter : “bottom -up ”). The top -down parsing algorithm is 345  predictive. It enumerates nodes left -to -right, building all possible nodes upward and to the right in the tree 346  structure . This is comparable to ‘open bracket’ estimates. In other words, the top -down feature specifies the 347  number of nodes that need to be built to incorporate future words. The bottom -up parsing algorithm, by contrast, 348  only counts a node whenever it has seen all evidence for this node. This is comparable to ‘closing bracket’ 349  estimates. We assumed that the parser was a ‘perfect oracle’ : it only builds nodes for the correct structure when 350  faced with ambiguity. 351  As can be observed in Figure 2 , the syntactic analysis of a sentence may contain ‘t’. This is a trace , a 352  syntactic marker that results from constituents moving to different projections in the tree structure as part of the 353  spell -out (linearization) of the sentence. Even though they make up part of the structure, t hese traces have no 354  (clear) acoustic correlate . In other words, they do not belong to any word , but they do have nodes associated with 355  them. To solve this issue, we summed the node counts that belong to trace elements to the preceding word. As an 356  example, consider the word ‘zeggen’ ( to say ) from the example sentence in Figure 2 . The bottom -up node count 357  for this word is 5 (upward to the left: V, VP, VP, VP VP). The bottom -up node count for the following trace is also 358  5 (I, IP, IP, CP, CP), leading to an assigned word count of 5+5=10 for the word ‘zeggen’. 359

# 388  Interaction . The interaction analysis was conducted to study the influence of the probability of a word on 389  the HGP correlates of syntactic structure building. Since interaction terms in TRFs do not yield interpretable 390  waveforms and can obscure latency effects (30,70) , we used a split -feature approach. This meant that we divided 391  the syntactic node counts over two features based on the median of word probability values, namely: (Feature 1) 392  node counts for high surprisal (or entropy), and (Feature 2) node counts for low surprisal (or entropy). This split 393  was done for all four combinations of probability and syntactic features: bottom -up split by surprisal, bottom -up 394  split by entropy, top -down split by surprisal, and top -down split by entropy. The evaluation of the effect of this 395  division on reconstruction accuracy was done by fitting 1000 models with random divisions of the syntactic 396  feature. The effect was considered significant if the ‘systematic’ split (i.e., using either surprisal or entropy) 397  resulted in a R 2-value that was higher than 95% (or more) of the ‘random’ split. 398  Like in the joint encoding analysis and to account for variation between electrode s, we fit each division 399  with various combinations of additional features. All feature combinations are summarized in Table 2 below. We 400  used the results of the ‘main effects’ analysis to determine which model to evaluate and interpret for each electrode .401  Specifically, we checked for each electrode which features had positive coefficients for their contribution to the 402  signal (irrespective of their significance). We then selected the model that contained all these features and one of 403  the splits. For example, if a n electrode had non -negative effects for surprisal, entropy, and bottom -up, we would 404  evaluate the models that split bottom -up by either surprisal or entropy, and also contain surprisal and entropy as 405  main effects (i.e., rows 11 and 12 from Table 2 below) against their random counterparts. 406  If the interaction resulted significant from the permutation test on the R 2-values, we evaluated the 407  difference between the TRF waveforms. # 685  3.1.3.  Joint encoding - summary 686  In summary, the results from the joint encoding analysis suggest that surprisal, entropy, bottom -up node counts 687  and top -down node counts are all encoded by HGP, both in isolation and jointly. Surprisal was encoded in most 688  of the recorded signals and had the largest contribution in terms of coefficients. The effects are largest in the 689  posterior STG and pars triangularis and opercularis of the IFG. Entropy was encoded in the STG and IFG as well, 690  though its coefficients were smaller than for surprisal and the positive coefficients had a more anterior distribution. 691  Bottom -up node counts had positive effects in pars triangularis, the anterior portion of the STG and MTG, and 692  electrodes in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. The positive contributions of top -down node counts were 693  distributed, among others in the SMG and inferior parietal cortex, a few electrodes in pars orbitalis and the MTG. 694  While all possible combinations of features exist in the data, correlations between feature coefficients further 695  indicated that the contribution from bottom -up node counts is smaller when entropy and surprisal explain more 696  variance, but that top -down is more likely to have a greater impact when entropy does, too. This suggests that top -697  down syntactic representations might share neural resources with entropy, while bottom -up syntactic 698  representations are more likely to be encoded by different neural populations than lexical probability. 699  A clustering approach of the TRFs showed that overall, HGP globally increases as a function of both 700  increasing uncertainty (entropy) and surprise (surprisal). The peaks are around 400 -500 milliseconds in the 701  STG/IFG, though generally the effect of entropy appears slightly later and slightly more anterior than the effect 702  of surprisal. The TRFs for structure building suggest multi -stage approaches in which HGP increases and 703  decreases alternatingly, which recruit IFG and the anterior temporal lobe (bottom -up) or parietal areas (top -down). 704  These results suggest that structure building is not an instantaneous process; rather, it suggests that structure 705  building is a process that requires intermittent communication between two or more (distant) areas. 706 

# 707  3.2.  Interaction analysis 708  3.2.1.  Reconstruction accuracy 709  Having shown that lexical and syntactic features may be encoded within the same, or neighbouring populations, 710  as reflected in the patterns of results described above, we sought evidence for interaction between these . In line 711  with suggestions from the statistical learning l iterature (27,28) , we examined whether lexical probability affected 712  cortical activity related to structure building . To this end , we tested four models in which node counts were divided 713  according to the value of the lexical probabilistic features : top -down node counts divided by the median of 714  surprisal or entropy; and bottom -up node counts divided by surprisal or entropy. In other words, there were two 715  syntactic features in each model: one with the node counts of words for which the surprisal or entropy value was 716  above the median, and with the node counts of the words for which the surprisal or entropy value was below the 717  median. The reconstruction accuracy of these models was compared against a distribution of 1000 models with 718  node counts randomly divided over two features. If splitting according to the probability values results in a better 719  fit than splitting randomly, we can conclude that there is some effect of the probability feature on the encoding of 720  the syntactic feature : an interaction. 721

# 799  4.  Discussion 800  In this study, we investigated whether high gamma power (HGP) jointly encodes lexical probability and syntactic 801  structure; and whether lexical probability affects HGP signatures of structure building. We analyzed ECoG 802  recordings of naturalistic speech processing using temporal response functions (TRFs) in two separate analyses ,803  testing the independent and dependent encoding of lexical and syntactic features respectively . We demonstrate 804  that lexical probability and syntactic structure are encoded by HGP, in both spatially separate (one feature per 805  electrode) and spatially intersecting (multiple features per electrode) fashion . In addition, a correlation analysis 806  suggested that features may (or may not) co -occur systematically . This means that HGP indeed jointly represents 807  probability and syntactic structure. We also find evidence for interactions between the encoding of probabilistic 808  and syntactic information , indicating an impact of lexical probability on processes associated with structure 809  building . With this, the current study contributes to a growing literature that suggests that lexical probabilistic and 810  syntactic cues are exploited flexibly during speech comprehension (1,2,71 –73) .811 

# 812  4.1.  High gamma power encodes constituency structure 813  Both bottom -up and top -down node counts contributed positively to the reconstruction accuracy (R 2) of TRF 814  models of HGP, over and above any effects of speech envelope, word onset, word frequency, surprisal, and 815  entropy. This suggests that HGP reflects the processing of constituency structure. Previous studies have reported 816  mixed results. While encoding of multi -unit syntactic node opening and closing measures in HGP has been found 817  (43) , other authors have suggested that the effect can be accounted for by including word frequency as a predictor 818  (44) . Consequently, Murphy (9) proposed that HGP captures basic semantic composition (3) , but is not sensitive 819  to multi -unit syntactic node opening and closure. Because the present study shows effects of top -down and bottom -820  up node counts derived from constituency structure over and above word frequency , t he results reported here are 821  not consistent with this interpretation . This may be due to differences in parsing algorithms used in the present 822  and previous studies: Woolnough and colleagues obtained their measure of open nodes and closing nodes from a 823  left -corner parsing model , which has properties of both bottom -up and top -down parsing algorithms. The p resent 824  study used node counts obtained from top -down and bottom -up parsing algorithms; measures closely related to 825  the number of parsing operations as used by Nelson and colleagues (43) , a study that reported a positive 826  relationship between open node count and HGP . W hile w e agree with Woolnough and colleagues (44) that the 827  choice of covariates has a nontrivial impact on uncovering neural variance related to syntactic processing, our 828  results indicate a further dependency on the parsing algorithm used to formalize the structure of the input 829  (18,67,68) .830

# 884  4.3.  A relationship between lexical probability and global disinhibition 885  The ‘joint encoding’ analysis revealed that surprisal was encoded in most of the recorded signals and explained 886  the most variance, as indicated by the number of electrodes and coefficient size. The effects are largest in the 887  posterior STG and pars triangularis and opercularis of the IFG. Entropy was encoded in the STG and IFG as well, 888  though its coefficients were smaller than for surprisal and the positive coefficients had a more anterior distribution. 889  The contributions of surprisal and entropy are positively correlated, suggesting that when surprisal values explain 890  variance in the neural signal, it is more likely for entropy to do so as well. These findings suggest that lexical 891  uncertainty and surprise tend to be represented by similar neural populations. 892  HGP globally increases as a function of both increasing uncertainty and surprise. The peaks are around 893  400 -500 milliseconds in the STG/IFG, though generally the effect of entropy appears slightly later and slightly 894  more anterior than the effect of surprisal. These effects are partially in accord with previous findings; positive 895  coefficients for surprisal have been reported before, including the peak at 400 milliseconds (31,50) , though trigram 896  entropy has elicited negative coefficients for HGP in the 200 -500ms time -window (43) . This study (43) reported 897  that syntactic features had a larger impact on model fit than the probabilistic features. This difference between 898  studies may be at least partially attributed to the different surprisal estimates – trigram models versus GPT2 -899  derived surprisal used here – in line with previous reports of larger contributions for probability estimates from 900  transformer -based architectures versus n -gram -derived estimates in models of neural activity during speech 901  comprehension (30,97) .902

That surprisal explained the most variance is compatible with the view that surprisal is a sum -statistic 903  pooled over many possible sources of linguistic information (14,98) , particularly when obtained from large 904  language models , which use a large contextual window for next -word prediction. Such sources of linguistic 905  information include syntactic representations and semantic relatedness, as well as pragmatic cues such as register ,906  but also pure probabilistic information ; i.e., how frequently linguistic units (co -)occur. This information is 907  theoretically separable from other sources of information, as is exemplified in studies of statistical learning 908  (22,40,99,100) , but it is inherently correlated with linguistic information in speech and language. W hile all these 909  sources of information can affect our response to incoming input (e.g., 101) , it is to date unknown whether this 910  change in response hinges on probabili ty . In other words, because of the nature of probabilistic features, it is 911  unclear whether all effects associated with them are reflections of probabilistic processing or representation of 912  probability . Given the evidence for statistical learning and the use of statistical cues in situations such as ambiguity 913  (102) , we assume that at least some part of the variance captured with surprisal and entropy is probabilistic 914  processing in its purest sense; the rest of the variance may reflect constraint from other available cues (31) . The 915  current study cannot disentangle those sources of variance. 916  We interpret the time courses of the effects of surprisal and entropy on HGP as follows . The state of 917  neural activation at any time point is affected by previous input, constituting a structuring context for the 918  processing of incoming stimuli. Here, we propose that the effects observed are reflections of global, transient 919  changes in the neural state that are provoked by the processing of new input , inhibiting some neural populations 920  more than others to minimize competition between related - and previously inferred representations (2) . The 921  processing of new input changes this state. Being sum -statistics over many potential linguistic representations ,922  surprisal and entropy are predictors that are apt to capture such global changes. In contrast to low -surprisal words, 923  high -surprisal words are unexpected given previous input, and neural populations coding for these words were 924  inhibited ; more excitatory (or disinhibitory) activity will be necessary to reach word recognition . Consequentially, 925  the recognition of unexpected words will produce a larger change in the sensitization pattern of neural populations. 926  For entropy, a similar mechanism might be at play. Recognizing words in positions of high uncertainty about the 927  continuation – i.e., a relatively unconstrained path – will lead to a larger change in the current state of the brain 928  than recognizing words in constrained condition, provided that the word matches the expectations (low surprisal). 929 

# 930  4.4.  Speech comprehension is a flexible weighting of cues 931  Besides the encoding of individual features, we also set out to examine how they were jointly encoded, and 932  whether they affect each other. To do so, we evaluated whether the contributions to model fit were correlated 933  between features , and whether the neural encoding of syntactic structure is affected by the probability of a word. 934  Correlations between feature coefficients from the joint encoding analysis indicated that the contribution from 935  bottom -up node counts is smaller when entropy and surprisal explain more variance . Bottom -up node count 936  coefficients correlate negatively with both probabilistic features. This suggests that bottom -up structural 937  processing draws more on neural populations that do not strongly enc ode contextual lexical probability ; a finding 938  that is in line with previous studies reporting spatial distinctions between lexical and syntactic conditions (46,47) .939  At the same time, this analysis revealed that top -down syntactic information is more likely to have a greater impact 940  when entropy does, too. This suggests that structural predictive information and lexical uncertainty are more likely 941  to be represented by the same (or minimally very closely situated ) neural populations. While this could simply 942  indicate a joint representation, their co -occurrence could also signal a process in which (A) lexical uncertainty 943  determines the weighting of predictive structural information or (B) predictive structural information determines 944  the relative importance of lexical uncertainty. 945  We find interactions between probability and syntax in several electrodes. In the electrodes that display 946  an interaction between entropy and top -down , the time -course of HGP modulation by top -down node counts 947  appeared to be shaped by low -entropy words more than by high -entropy words. This suggests that lexical 948  uncertainty affects the encoding of top -down structural information. Considering also the positive correlation 949  between the coefficients of these features, we suggest that lexical uncertainty modulates the weighting of 950  anticipatory structural information such that it plays a larger role in shaping the comprehension process when 951  lexical uncertainty is low; a trade -off in which predictive cues receive stronger weighting in the comprehension 952  process in low -uncertainty states. Evidence for a trade -off at this level adds to previous work that has shown that 953  high word entropy leads to increased low -frequency tracking of lower -level features, such as acoustic and 954  phonemic features (103,104) .955  In the electrodes in which bottom -up structure building interacts with surprisal, the TRFs revealed that 956  these neural populations are more sensitive to bottom -up structure building when surprisal is high. # 972  5.  Conclusion 973  This study provides evidence for an interactive, dynamic process of speech comprehension that continuously 974  integrates syntactic and lexical probabilistic information. The results show that local, high -frequency (70 -150 Hz) 975  cortical activity jointly represents lexical probabilistic information and syntactic structure, and that these features 976  are actively integrated during spoken language comprehension. The current results indicate that high gamma 977  power is sensitive to multi -word estimates of constituency structure and suggest that syntactic structure building 978  depends on communication between interconnected regions of the dorsal - and ventral streams of speech 979  processing . Furthermore, we provide evidence that bottom -up syntactic information is less likely to be encoded 980  by neural populations that strongly code for lexical probability measures, while top -down syntactic information 981  shares neural resources with lexical uncertainty. We propose that lexical uncertainty modulates the weighting of 982  anticipatory structural information, with these cues being interactively resolved in a distributed and often 983  overlapping network of perisylvian cortical patches .984
