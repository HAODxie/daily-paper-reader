Abstract 18 

Understanding the neural basis of human cognition requires measuring not only localized 19 

brain activity but also how functional interactions between brain regions change in response to 20 

different cognitive demands. Event -related fMRI is an efficient design for linking trial -wise 21 

behavioral and computational variables to brain activity, but comparable methods for examining 22 

their effects on functional connectivity remain limited. Here, we develop beta -PPI (beta -series 23 

psychophysiological interaction) , a method that leverages single -trial response estimates from 24 

even -related fMRI to quantify how trial -wise variables modulate functional connectivity . This 25 

task -based functional connectivity method provides a flexible approach for studying functional 26 

connectivity for event -related fMRI designs . We evaluated beta -PPI using comprehensive 27 

simulations across several experimental conditions and signal qualities. Beta -PPI can sensitively 28 

detect ground -truth effects and exhibited good parameter recovery. Compared with generalized 29 

psychophysiological interaction, beta -PPI achieved comparable performance across most 30 

conditions while demonstrating improved statistical power under lower signal -to -noise 31 

conditions. We further validated beta -PPI using empirical event -related fMRI data. Distinct trial -32 

wise cognitive variables selectively modulated functional connectivity during their corresponding 33 

trial epochs, demonstrating the temporal specificity and flexibility of the approach . By testing 34 

how trial -wise variables modulate functional connectivity, beta -PPI extends task -based 35 

connectivity analysis to model -based fMRI and provides a common single -trial framework that 36 

could facilitate the integration of connectivity, activation, and representational analyses in event -37 

related fMRI. 38

1. Introduction 40 

Human cognition emerges from interactions among brain regions rather than isolated 41 

activity (Goldman -Rakic, 1988; Mesulam, 1990) . Distributed yet c oordinated neural activity 42 

supports diverse cognitive functions, including perception, decision making, learning, and 43 

cognitive control (Cole et al., 2013; Hipp et al., 2011; van den Brink et al., 2022) . Understanding 44 

how brain network connectivity is configured in response to different cognitive demands is 45 

therefore a major goal of cognitive neuroscience. Functional connectivity analyses provide a 46 

useful framework for addressing this question. By quantifying statistical dependencies between 47 

neural signals (Park and Friston, 2013) , functional connectivity assesses the extent activity in 48 

different brain regions covaries over time. These statistical relationships provide a network level 49 

description of how distributed brain systems are organized. Unlike localization analyses, which 50 

identify where task -evoked responses occur, functional connectivity characterizes how activity is 51 

coordinated across distributed brain regions. Importantly, these interactions have been shown to 52 

change across behavioral contexts (Cocuzza et al., 2020; Hearne et al., 2017) . As task demands 53 

change, the strength of coupling between regions may increase or decrease, reflecting dynamic 54 

reconfiguration of region -wise interactions . This approach naturally emphasizes that brain 55 

networks are not static, but instead dynamically reconfigure in response to different task demands. 56 

Although the task -dependent and dynamic nature of brain network interactions have long 57 

been recognized (Mill et al., 2017; Shine et al., 2016) , much of the work has focused on task -free ,58 

resting -state connectivity (Buckner et al., 2013; Deco et al., 2011) . These studies have provided 59 

insights into the organization of large -scale functional brain networks (Ladwig et al., 2026; 60 

Schaefer et al., 2018) , and its alterations across neurological and psychiatric disorders (Greicius et 61 

al., 2004; Yan et al., 2019) . However, resting -state connectivity does not directly address how 62 

functional connectivity are reconfigured in response to changing cognitive demands. For this 63 

purpose, methods that quantify connectivity during task performance are needed. 64

Several methods have been developed to study task -dependent changes in functional 65 

connectivity. For example, Psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analysis tests whether 66 

connectivity between brain regions varies as a function of an experimental manipulation (Friston 67 

et al., 1997) . Generalized PPI (gPPI) extends this framework to accommodate multiple task 68 

conditions in event -related fMRI (McLaren et al., 2012) . A different approach is beta -series 69 

correlation (Rissman et al., 2004) , which estimates trial -wise response amplitudes and measures 70 

connectivity by correlating beta estimates across brain regions . Both PPI and beta -series explicitly 71 

model task -evoked responses to quantify task -dependent changes in connectivity. In contrast, 72 

residual -based functional connectivity approaches estimate task connectivity after removing trial -73 

evoked responses from continuous BOLD time series (Al -Aidroos et al., 2012; Hwang et al., 74 

2019) . Extensive work has been conducted to compare and evaluate different task connectivity 75 

methods (Cisler et al., 2014; Cole et al., 2019; Di et al., 2021; Masharipov et al., 2024; O’Reilly 76 

et al., 2012) .77 

Task -based connectivity methods aim to characterize how interactions between brain 78 

regions support different cognitive processes. Cognitive processes are inherently dynamic and 79 

flexible , and event -related fMRI provides a n efficient experimental design approach for isolating 80 

these dynamics (Buckner, 1998) . It enables trial -by -trial manipulations of cognitive process es , as 81 

well as temporally separating distinct cognitive operations between ep oc hs within a trial (Ollinger 82 

et al., 2001) , such as stimulus input , decision making, response selection, and feedback 83 

processing. Event -related fMRI design thus allows trial -level variation in cognition to be 84 

quantified at the trial and epoch level . A major challenge is therefore how to characterize this 85 

trial - and epoch -specific cognitive process and its variability . One useful approach is 86 

computational modeling, which estimates latent variables that evolve throughout a task to provide 87 

mechanistic inferences of underlying cognitive processes. Examples include prediction errors 88 

(Gläscher et al., 2010) , expected value (Daw et al., 2011) , uncertainty (Nassar et al., 2019) , latent -89

whether these variables also influence interactions between distributed brain regions. 93 

Addressing this question requires methods capable of testing whether functional 94

require continuous time series after removal of task -evoked responses . Consequently, they are 97

2. Methods 131 

2.1 Overview 132 

The beta -PPI method quantifies whether functional connectivity between two brain 133 

regions is modulated by a trial -wise variable. Similar to conventional seed -based functional 134 

connectivity analyses, one brain region is designated as the seed region, and functional 135 

connectivity is modeled as the statistical relationship between trial -wise response amplitudes in 136 

the seed and target brain regions. The method then tests whether this relationship changes 137 

systematically as a function of a trial -wise moderating variable. 138

Beta -PPI separates three source s that contribute to task -related functional connectivity 139 

estimates . First, baseline coupling represents the relationship between source and target activity 140 

that is independent of the computational variable and task -evoked responses . This is akin to task -141 

free, intrinsic functional connectivity (Cole et al., 2014; Fox et al., 2006) . Second, task -evoked 142 

responses capture changes in local activity magnitudes that are evoked by task inputs (e.g., 143 

computational variable or stimulus conditions) and thus do not reflect changes in functional 144 

connectivity (O’Reilly et al., 2012) . Third, connectivity modulation represents the extent to which 145 

the computational variable modulates functional connectivity between the source and target 146 

regions. Th is serves as the primary measurement of interest. The following sections describe the 147 

mathematical formulation of the beta -PPI model, the simulation framework we used to evaluate 148 

its statistical properties, and how we validated it with an empirical event -related fMRI dataset. 149

generate functional connectivity. 183

Single trial response amplitudes were estimated using the LSS regression procedure 260 

(Mumford et al., 2012) . For each trial j, a design matrix was constructed containing three 261 

regressors: (1) a regressor modeling the trial of interest, (2) a regressor modeling all remaining 262 

trials combined, and (3) an intercept term. The coefficient associated with the trial -of -interest 263 

regressor was retained as the estimated response amplitude for that trial. This procedure will yield 264 

one response estimate per trial for both the source and target regions. The resulting beta series 265 

therefore provide s an estimation of trial -by -trial fluctuations in task -evoked activity while 266 

reducing contamination from overlapping responses. Because the beta -PPI framework operates on 267 

trial -wise response amplitudes rather than continuous BOLD signals, these beta estimates 268 

constitute the inputs to all subsequent connectivity analyses. 269 

2.3.4 beta -PPI Connectivity Estimation 270 

Task -modulated connectivity was estimated using the beta -PPI framework described 271 

above. For each simulated participant, source and target region ’s beta series obtained from the 272 

LSS procedure were standardized to have zero mean and unit variance. Then, f or each simulated 273 

subject , target region ’s beta estimates were modeled as a function of source region activity, 274 

moderator values, and their interaction. The interaction coefficient served as the subject level 275 

estimate of task modulated connectivity. Because source activity and moderator related activation 276 

were included explicitly in the regression model, the interaction coefficient reflects variance 277 

uniquely attributable to modu lation in functional connectivity rather than shared co -activation. 278 

The subject level interaction coefficients were subsequently entered into group -level analyses to 279 

assess parameter recovery, statistical power, and false positive rates. 280

2.3.5 Simulation parametrization 290 

Parameters set for the latent generative model were varied to explore a range of 291 

conditions , including task -evoked source activation (α = 0.1 to 0.75), baseline source -target 292 

coupling (γ = 0 to 0.60), connectivity modulation (δ = 0 to 0.40), latent neural signal -to -noise 293 

ratio (SNR = 0.1 to 1.0), and BOLD signal -to -noise ratio (SNR = 0.50 to 2.0). Because the true 294 

magnitudes of latent neural coupling and task -modulated connectivity are not directly observable 295 

in empirical data, these ranges were chosen to span weak through relatively strong effects while 296 

avoiding unrealistic ceiling conditions. 297 

2.3. 6 gPPI 298 

Although both gPPI and beta -PPI test whether functional connectivity is modulated by 299 

task variables, they differ in several important aspects . gPPI estimates interactions in the 300 

continuous fMRI time series by first deconvolving the seed -region BOLD signal to approximate 301 

latent neural activity, multiplying this neural estimate by the psychological regressor, and 302 

reconvolving the interaction term to form a PPI regressor (McLaren et al., 2012) . In contrast, 303 

beta -PPI operates directly on trial -specific response estimates obtained from event -related GLMs, 304 

testing whether trial -to -trial variation in a moderator variable explains variability in functional 305 

connectivity across trials. Consequently, the primary distinction between the two methods lies in 306 

whether interactions are estimated from deconvolved estimated time series or from single -trial 307 

response estimates. To benchmark the performance of beta -PPI against a conventional task -based 308 

connectivity approach, we simulated a gPPI analysis using the same simulated datasets and 309

4. Discussion 659 

Event -related fMRI combined with computational modeling has become a powerful 660 

approach for investigating the latent cognitive processes underlying human behavior. Although 661 

model -based fMRI is widely used to relate regional brain activity to trial -wise computational 662 

variables, comparatively few methods can test how these variables influence interactions between 663 

distributed brain regions. In addition, most existing task -based connectivity approaches are not 664 

naturally suited for the single -trial structure of event -related fMRI. The present study addresses 665 

these two methodological gaps by introducing beta -PPI, a task -based functional connectivity 666 

framework that leverages single -trial response estimates to quantify how trial -wise behavioral and 667 

computational variables modulate functional connectivity in event -related fMRI. 668 

4.1 Beta -PPI is an effective task -based functional connectivity method for event -related 669 

fMRI 670 

We showed that a cross a comprehensive set of simulations, beta -PPI exhibited several 671 

desirable properties. The method can sensitively detect simulated interaction effects, maintained 672 

appropriate false -positive rates, and robust to changes in intrinsic coupling and task -evoked 673 

coactivation. Furthermore , these statistical properties translated to empirical data. Using 674 

computational variables derived from a Bayesian model, beta -PPI identified patterns of 675 

connectivity modulation associated with both task belief and error, feedback processes . Together, 676 

the simulation and empirical results demonstrate that beta -PPI provides a practical method for 677 

investigating how continuously varying computational processes influence functional 678

4.2 Limitations 741 

An important limitation of our study concerns the level we implement the latent neural 742 

response simulations . Our simulations modeled latent neural events that generated BOLD 743 

responses without simulat ing the detailed biophysical processes , such as neuronal firing rate, local 744 

field potentials, synaptic coupling , that generate fMRI signals. Several previous validation studies 745 

of functional connectivity methods have employed more detailed neural -mass or biophysical 746 

models that explicitly represent synaptic dynamics, potentials, or circuit interactions (Cole et al., 747 

2019; Masharipov et al., 2024) . The goal of our simulation was to control the magnitude and 748 

interactions between events that give rise to measurable BOLD responses rather than modeling 749 

the precise neuronal mechanisms that generated those events. We are therefore agonistic to the 750 

exact circuit level physiology that can generate the significant interaction effects we simulated .751 

Because the relationship between microscopic neural activity and the BOLD signal remains 752 

indirect (and controversial), they depend on assumptions regarding neurovascular coupling 753 

(Logothetis, 2008) . We therefore chose a simpler latent generative framework that minimizes 754 

assumptions while retaining necessary control over important properties that beta -PPI is designed 755 

to estimate. Nevertheless, a more definitive evaluation of beta -PPI will likely come from 756

4. 3 Implementation suggestion and conclusion 769 

Model -based fMRI, which uses computational models to analyzing fMRI data , is shifting 770 

fMRI analyses from comparisons between experimental conditions toward trial -by -trial 771 

characterization of latent cognitive processes (O’Doherty et al., 2007) . Beta -PPI is well aligned 772 

with this approach by providing a flexible method for testing how continuously varying 773 

computational variables influence functional connectivity between distributed brain regions. 774 

In practice, beta -PPI can be implemented using a variety of connectivity analysis 775 

strategies. Similar to conventional seed -based functional connectivity analyses, investigators can 776 

define one or more seed regions based on hypotheses or independent ROIs , and test how trial -777

regions. This is the approach we demonstrated with our empirical validation. Alternatively, the 779 

same framework can be extended to whole -brain or network -level analyses by estimating trial -780
