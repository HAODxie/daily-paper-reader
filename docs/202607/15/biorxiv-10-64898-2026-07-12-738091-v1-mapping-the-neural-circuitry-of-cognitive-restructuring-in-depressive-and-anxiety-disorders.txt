Abstract 34 

Background: Cognitive restructuring, the process of identifying and challenging negative 35 

thoughts, is a key technique for treating depressive and anxiety disorders. Although 36 

neuroimaging studies have characterised the brain systems supporting cognitive restructuring 37 

in healthy individuals, it remains unclear how these systems are altered in depression and 38 

anxiety, or whether each disorder is associated with distinct neural dysfunction. 39 

Methods: Seventy-three clinical participants with depressive or anxiety disorders and 70 40 

healthy controls completed a cognitive restructuring paradigm during 7 Tesla functional 41 

magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). The task required participants to either repeat a series of 42 

negative statements or challenge them using Socratic questioning. Group-level fMRI analyses 43 

examined the effects of depressive and anxiety symptom severity on brain activation, while 44 

dynamic causal modelling characterized the directional neural influences between implicated 45 

regions. 46 

Results: During challenging compared to repeating statements, greater depressive symptoms 47 

were associated with reduced dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) activation. Conversely, 48 

greater anxiety symptoms were associated with greater dlPFC activation. Effective 49 

connectivity results revealed that depressive symptoms were associated with greater 50 

inhibition from the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC) to the ventromedial prefrontal 51 

cortex, whereas anxiety symptoms were associated with greater excitation from the dlPFC to 52 

amygdala and greater inhibition from the vlPFC to amygdala. 53 

Conclusions: While clinical participants modified negative beliefs as effectively as healthy 54 

controls, depressive and anxiety symptoms were associated with dissociable neural signatures 55 

during restructuring. This suggests that cognitive behavioral therapy may engage partially 56 

distinct mechanisms depending on symptom profile, a possibility that warrants longitudinal 57 

investigation of treatment response. 58

Cognitive models of depression and anxiety posit that deeply ingrained negative beliefs are 60 

core features in the development and maintenance of these disorders (1). Once activated by 61 

subsequent stressors, these beliefs act to bias the processing of information (2-4). As a key 62 

component of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), cognitive restructuring represents a 63 

process that assists patients in identifying, evaluating, and modifying maladaptive automatic 64 

thought patterns (5). Through this, cognitive restructuring represents a key therapeutic 65 

mechanism underlying symptom improvement in depression and anxiety disorders. Although 66 

often used interchangeably, cognitive restructuring and cognitive reappraisal differ in their 67 

primary targets. Reappraisal explicitly regulates emotion by reinterpreting a stimulus (6), 68 

whereas cognitive restructuring modifies underlying automatic thought patterns, with changes 69 

in affect emerging as a downstream consequence. Consequently, while cognitive restructuring 70 

likely shares neural systems with reappraisal, its heavier reliance on self-referential 71 

processing implicates circuitry that remains poorly characterised, thereby limiting the clinical 72 

utility of current mechanistic models 73 

Growing evidence indicates that cognitive restructuring involves complex interactions 74 

between brain systems implicated in self-belief processing and affect regulation (7-10). The 75 

ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), as a core hub of the default mode network, is 76 

central to these functions, including supporting cognitive representations of the self (11). This 77 

role in self-referential processing is thought to reflect the vmPFC’s broader function in 78 

encoding the subjective and affective value of stimuli (12-14). Affect regulation also appears 79 

to be important for successful restructuring, as heightened negative affect limits the 80 

integration of novel positive information needed to revise negative beliefs (15, 16), thereby 81 

constraining belief updating. This regulation is supported by the cognitive control network, 82 

which contributes to the metacognitive process that allows self-representations to become the 83 

objects of attention (17). Within this network, the presupplementary motor area (preSMA) is 84 

hypothesized to aid in strategy selection by modulating lateral prefrontal regions through its 85 

roles in directing internal attention (18) and inhibitory control (19). The dorsolateral 86 

prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) is subsequently suggested to maintain and manipulate strategy-87 

relevant representations and regulatory goals in working memory (20), while the ventrolateral 88 

prefrontal cortex (vlPFC) is more directly implicated in suppressing affective responses 89 

during reappraisal (21). The regulatory influence of lateral prefrontal regions is, in turn, 90 

thought to act on the amygdala (6). The relatively sparse anatomical connectivity between the 91 

lateral prefrontal cortex and the amygdala (22-24), however, indicates that intermediary 92 

regions including the vmPFC also play an important role in mediating this regulation (25-27). 93

Given the central role of cognitive restructuring in the treatment of anxiety and 94 

depressive disorders, growing research has sought to characterize dysfunction within this 95 

circuitry and its relationship with symptom expression. Accordingly, widespread prefrontal 96 

alterations have been shown across depressive and anxiety disorder studies investigating 97 

representations of the self (28). Specifically, depression appears to be more strongly 98 

associated with increased anterior cingulate cortex activation, whereas anxiety is associated 99 

with increased insula and dlPFC activation (28). The clinical relevance of these activation 100 

patterns is highlighted by the fact that CBT has been shown to normalize hyperactivation 101 

across the dorsal anterior cingulate, dlPFC, and insula in anxiety disorders (29). Conversely, 102 

the cognitive reappraisal and emotion regulation literature has reported reduced activity in the 103 

dlPFC, vlPFC, and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex across both anxiety and depressive 104 

disorders (30, 31), although more recent work has also yielded inconsistent findings (32, 33). 105 

These inconsistencies may reflect the opposing effects of depressive and anxiety symptoms 106 

being masked at the disorder level due to the high rates of comorbidity between these 107 

conditions (34-36). Such symptom divergence has previously been observed during 108 

interoceptive processing, where anxiety and depressive symptoms demonstrated opposing 109 

neural associations (37), highlighting the need for dimensional approaches to resolve 110 

apparent neural heterogeneity. 111 

To address this, we examined the neurocircuitry of cognitive restructuring in a 112 

transdiagnostic clinical sample, predominantly with anxiety and depressive disorders, using 7-113 

Tesla (7T) fMRI and a novel cognitive restructuring task (38). We assessed both task-evoked 114 

activation and dynamic causal modelling (DCM; 39) to characterize how depressive and 115 

anxiety symptoms were differentially associated with regional activity and directional 116 

influences among prefrontal-subcortical regions. We hypothesized that depressive symptoms 117 

would be associated with hypoactivation of the dlPFC and vlPFC (30, 31) and reduced 118 

inhibitory connectivity from lateral prefrontal regions to the amygdala (40), with the vmPFC 119 

serving as the principal mediating node. Conversely, we hypothesized that anxiety symptoms 120 

would be associated with greater activity in the insula, amygdala, and dlPFC (28), alongside 121 

greater inhibitory prefrontal-to-amygdala connectivity (41), also routed through the vmPFC. 122

Cognitive restructuring paradigm 151 

All participants completed a previously detailed cognitive restructuring paradigm (10, 38). 152 

Prior to scanning, participants were trained in cognitive restructuring techniques including 153 

active rebuttal, reinterpretation, and perspective shifting, which use Socratic questioning to 154 

challenge negative self-perceptions, akin to those employed in cognitive psychotherapy (47). 155 

Once participants' proficiency was determined by the research staff, the paradigm was 156 

administered during concurrent fMRI scanning. The cognitive restructuring task consisted of 157 

a single run consisting of 16 blocks ( Fig. 1 ). Each block began with the on-screen 158 

presentation of a negative self-cognition statement drawn from CBT and psychopathology 159 

literature (Supplementary Table S1) (48, 49). Participants then indicated via button press 160 

whether they would repeat or restructure the given statement using their pre-learned 161

General linear modelling 190 

Following preprocessing of the fMRI data (see Supplementary Methods), first-level general 191 

linear model (GLM) analyses were conducted for each participant using SPM12. Regressors 192 

for the challenge, repeat, and decide conditions were specified using the onsets and durations 193 

of each block, convolved with a canonical hemodynamic response function. Physiological 194 

noise regressors were then added to the GLM, and a high-pass filter (1/128 seconds) was 195 

applied to account for low-frequency drifts. The FAST method was used to estimate temporal 196 

autocorrelations due to its enhanced performance at short repetition times (52). Primary 197 

contrast images from each participant (Challenge > Repeat and Challenge < Repeat) were 198 

carried forward to second-level analyses using the summary statistics approach to random-199 

effects analyses. At the second level, we modelled the three DASS subscales as separate 200 

regressors within a single GLM to isolate the effects of each symptom domain, adjusting for 201 

age and sex as covariates of no interest. To ensure that collinearity between regressors did not 202 

bias the estimates, we examined the variance inflation factor (VIF) of the three DASS 203 

subscales. This revealed a VIF of 2.1 for the depression subscale, 2.6 for the anxiety subscale, 204 

and 2.9 for the stress subscale. As these values are under 5, this indicates an acceptable level 205 

of collinearity between factors (53). 206 

207 

Dynamic causal modelling 208 

DCM estimates the rate of change in the activity of a region as a function of activity in 209 

another (i.e., effective connectivity, measured in Hertz; Hz). In task-based DCM, connectivity 210 

is decomposed into a baseline component (intrinsic connectivity) and a component modulated 211 

by the experimental condition of interest (54). Positive values are indicative of a putative 212 

excitatory influence between regions, while negative values represent a putative inhibitory 213 

influence. Self or within-region connections are conversely represented by unitless log 214 

scaling parameters that modify the default inhibitory connectivity of -0.5 Hz. For these self-215 

connections, positive values for these parameters are indicative of greater inhibition, whereas 216 

negative values represent reduced inhibition. 217

Our model space for DCM consisted of five regions of interest: the preSMA, dlPFC, vlPFC, 220 

vmPFC, and amygdala ( Supplementary Fig. S1 ). These regions were chosen based on prior 221 

evidence implicating them in cognitive restructuring for healthy individuals (38, 55). 222 

Representative timeseries were extracted from each region at the subject-level following 223 

published guidelines (54). For full details of the extraction methodology, see Supplementary 224 

Methods. The candidate model was specified using DCM version 12.5 in SPM12. Intrinsic 225 

connectivity was modelled as bidirectional between all regions, except between the preSMA 226 

and amygdala, which was assumed to be mediated via other prefrontal areas, given their lack 227 

of anatomical connection. Modulation by the Challenge condition was also specified across 228 

these connections. The ‘Task’ condition (onset of all Challenge and Repeat blocks) was 229 

defined as the driving input into the preSMA, given this region drove the largest input effect 230 

in prior investigations (38). This input was additionally mean-centered, so that the intrinsic 231 

connectivity represented the average connectivity. Parametric empirical Bayes (PEB) was 232 

then used to estimate symptom-related effects on effective connectivity (for additional 233 

estimation and inference details see Supplementary Methods). Leave-one-out cross-validation 234 

(LOOCV) in the PEB framework was finally used to evaluate the ability of connectivity 235 

parameters to predict individual differences in symptoms (Supplementary Methods and 236 

Results). 237

Discussion 363 

This study examined the neurocircuitry of cognitive restructuring and its links to depressive 364 

and anxiety symptoms. Challenging negative statements engaged the dlPFC differently by 365 

symptom type: anxiety was associated with increased activation, depression with reduced 366 

activation. Heart rate data also supported this divergence. Depressive symptoms were also 367 

linked to altered vlPFC–vmPFC connectivity, while anxiety showed broader connectivity 368 

changes, including with the amygdala. Together, these findings show that although clinical 369 

participants restructured negative statements as efficiently as healthy controls, depression and 370 

anxiety were marked by dissociable patterns of prefrontal-subcortical circuitry and autonomic 371 

regulation. 372 

373 

Anxiety and depressive symptom associations with the posterior dlPFC 374 

We observed that dlPFC activation associated with depression and anxiety symptoms during 375 

restructuring was localized to the posterior portion of this region. The dlPFC is involved in 376 

monitoring and manipulation of working memory contents (56) and exhibits a posterior-to-377 

anterior functional gradient (57). Within this gradient, the posterior dlPFC tracks dynamic 378 

changes in the environment and individual, and the anterior dlPFC supports more abstract 379 

representations (58, 59). The posterior dlPFC is therefore thought to support the integrative 380 

process underpinning cognitive control (60). Evidence indicates that posterior dlPFC activity 381 

reflects the cognitive effort required to overcome a heightened affective load and implement 382 

cognitive control, with reappraisal of high-intensity emotions eliciting greater activation than 383 

low-intensity emotions (61). Consistent with this role, transcranial magnetic stimulation 384 

(TMS) targeting peak dlPFC activation during cognitive restructuring accelerated heart rate 385 

regulation and increased subsequent use of cognitive restructuring, despite not reducing 386 

overall distress (62). Together, these findings implicate the posterior dlPFC as a key locus for 387 

the efficient instantiation of cognitive control, particularly while under affective load. 388 

Expanding on this work, we observed that posterior dlPFC activation during 389 

restructuring tracked anxiety and depressive symptoms in opposite directions. Anxiety and 390

anxiety have been inconsistent (78). This inconsistency likely reflects multiple factors, 421 

including the amygdala’s complex functional architecture (78) as well as compensatory 422 

mechanisms within that downregulate altered amygdala responses. Although our activation 423

Network associations with depressive symptoms 435 

Depressive symptoms were also associated with greater inhibitory influence from the vlPFC 436

been shown to predict treatment response (84-86). During task-based studies, these vmPFC 442

predicts treatment response at rest (88), whether it generalizes to contexts like cognitive 445 

restructuring, where vmPFC is engaged rather than suppressed, remains an open question. 446

Limitations 448 

While participants were trained in cognitive restructuring techniques, including active 449 

rebuttal, reinterpretation, and perspective shifting, we did not record which strategy was 450 

deployed on each trial. This information could provide clearer insight into the observed 451 

associations, given that different strategies engage distinct prefrontal regions (21). The 452 

strategies may also have varied systematically across groups and symptom profiles. 453 

Moreover, the sample comprised predominantly young female participants. Although this 454 

demographic profile is consistent with the epidemiology of depressive and anxiety disorders, 455 

and age and sex were adjusted for in all analyses, replication in more age- and sex-balanced 456 

samples is needed to establish the generalizability of these findings. While we use the terms 457 

"bottom-up" and "top-down" to describe interactions between the prefrontal cortex and 458

Conclusions 466 

Our findings illustrated that cognitive restructuring reduced the endorsement of negative 467 

statements in clinical participants, yet the underlying neurobiology diverged by symptom 468 

profile. Depressive and anxiety symptoms demonstrated differential associations with dlPFC 469 

function, downstream prefrontal–subcortical circuitry, as well as heart rate during cognitive 470 

restructuring, suggesting processes central to CBT may engage partially distinct mechanisms 471 

across symptom presentations. Longitudinal studies are needed to determine whether these 472 

neural signatures prospectively predict treatment response and inform the targeting of 473 

cognitive interventions. 474
