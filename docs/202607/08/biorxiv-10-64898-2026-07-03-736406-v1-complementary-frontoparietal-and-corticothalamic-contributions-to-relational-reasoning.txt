Abstract 35 

Complex reasoning depends on flexible coordination among frontal, parietal, and thalamic 36 

systems, but the circuit mechanisms that support increasing relational demands remain unclear. 37 

We combined EEG with biologically grounded corticothalamic neural field modelling while 38 

participants solved relational problems of graded complexity. Successful reasoning was 39 

associated with dissociable frontoparietal dynamics. Frontal regions showed increased theta-band 40 

power, whereas parietal regions showed reduced alpha- and beta-band power. Theta-band phase 41 

synchronisation across frontoparietal-network nodes increased with problem complexity but was 42 

not associated with performance. By contrast, stronger beta-band synchronisation across the 43 

same network was associated with slower and less accurate responses as demands approached 44 

the highest complexity, suggesting that stronger coordination is not uniformly beneficial. Neural 45 

field modelling indicated that these regional spectral dynamics reflected specific complexity-46 

dependent circuit adaptations. Parietal regions showed modulation of intracortical and 47 

corticothalamic gains, intrathalamic inhibition, prolonged loop delays, and faster synaptic 48 

filtering, whereas frontal regions primarily adjusted intracortical gains to maintain local 49 

excitatory–inhibitory balance and supported longer temporal integration windows. Together, 50 

these empirical and model-derived findings reveal complementary frontoparietal and 51 

corticothalamic mechanisms for relational reasoning. 52 

53 

Significance statement 54 

A central challenge in cognitive neuroscience is understanding how frontoparietal-thalamic 55 

interactions support the flexible integration of relations during complex reasoning. Using 56 

electroencephalography and biophysical modelling during sudoku-like tasks with varying 57 

relational complexity, we show that frontal and parietal cortices adopt distinct but coordinated 58 

strategies. As complexity increases, frontal regions stabilise processing by tuning excitation– 59 

inhibition balance, whereas parietal regions additionally reorganise corticothalamic and 60 

intrathalamic circuitry. These changes co-occur with band- and region-specific power shifts and 61 

increased frontoparietal synchronisation, indicating that effective reasoning depends not simply 62 

on scaling neural activity, but on coordinated, region-specific circuit reconfigurations. 63

Introduction 66 

The frontoparietal network (FPN) serves as a central hub for integrating information required for 67 

complex problem-solving and reasoning (1, 2). Across a wide range of cognitively demanding 68 

tasks, increasing task difficulty is accompanied by heightened activity and reconfiguration of 69 

functional connectivity within frontal and parietal cortices (3-7). While these findings establish 70 

the central role of the FPN in reasoning, we still have limited insight into the temporal, spectral, 71 

and circuit-level mechanisms by which relations are integrated, particularly as relational 72 

demands increase. 73 

74 

Relational complexity (RC) provides a principled framework for quantifying reasoning demands, 75 

defined as the maximum number of relations that must be integrated within a single cognitive 76 

operation (8). Across diverse reasoning domains, including non-verbal visuospatial puzzles, 77 

propositional logic, transitive inference, and sentence comprehension tasks, RC functions as a 78 

domain-general marker of processing capacity limits (8-12). Accordingly, increases in relational 79 

complexity reliably impair performance and recruit frontoparietal regions and networks, linking 80 

graded behavioural costs to the neural engagement required for relational integration (4, 5). 81 

82 

The Latin Square Task (LST) is a validated paradigm for manipulating RC (11, 13, 14). In the 83 

LST, participants solve single-constraint visuospatial puzzles in which relational demands are 84 

systematically varied. A prior fMRI–EEG study using the LST identified a 2–4.2 s post-stimulus 85 

window in which the FPN supports relational-complexity integration (15). These delayed 86 

representations are distinct from early stimulus-locked responses and aligned with high-order 87 

computational strategies learned by artificial neural networks trained on the same task (16, 17). 88 

Oscillatory activity in the theta, alpha, and beta bands has been linked to both local processing 89 

and long-range coordination across frontoparietal systems during higher-order cognition (18, 19) 90 

and recent work has begun to characterise the role of frequency-specific neural dynamics in 91 

supporting reasoning under increasing cognitive demands (20-22). However, the mechanisms 92 

coordinating integration within this temporal window remain unresolved. 93 

94 

Corticothalamic interactions provide a plausible mechanism through which modulation of FPN 95 

activity can be achieved to facilitate the processing of RC (23-25). The thalamus is anatomically 96

in mean thalamic activation (1). This functional dissociation suggests that complexity-related 100 

effects may be mediated by distinct changes in thalamo-FPN coordination or gain, rather than by 101 

overall increases in thalamic activity. Increasing relational demands may thus be supported by 102 

thalamic-induced selective amplification of neural activity in frontal or parietal cortices, or by 103 

distinct reconfigurations of corticothalamic loops that shape frontoparietal dynamics. Neural 104 

field theory (NFT) models provide a mechanistic framework for addressing these questions by 105 

linking macroscopic EEG spectra to biologically grounded circuit parameters governing cortico– 106 

cortical, corticothalamic, and intrathalamic interactions (28-35). 107 

108 

Here, we used EEG and corticothalamic NFT to investigate how frontal, parietal, and thalamic 109 

circuits support relational integration as reasoning complexity increases. Focusing on the 110 

established LST RC integration window (15) we characterise complexity-dependent changes in 111 

local spectral power and frequency-specific frontoparietal synchronisation, and their 112 

relationships with behaviour. We then apply NFT modelling to dissociate cortical and 113 

corticothalamic contributions to these effects. Our findings reveal a functional dissociation 114 

between frontal and parietal processing as complexity increases, characterised by region-specific 115 

adjustments in the excitation–inhibition balance and corticothalamic gain. Together, these results 116 

advance mechanistic insight into how the frontoparietal–thalamic system accommodates 117 

increasingly complex relational processes supporting human reasoning. 118

We started by assessing participants’ behavioural performance. We modified the puzzle format 121 

to accommodate shorter presentation and response times, while keeping stimuli within the 122 

participant’s visual field (Fig. 1A). Each trial presented a 4×4 grid partially filled with four 123 

differently coloured circles. Participants were asked to identify the missing-coloured circle 124 

marked with a red question mark. The relationship between coloured items adhered to a single 125 

rule: each item could appear only once per row and once per column . This rule allowed us to 126 

manipulate relational demands by selectively removing items from the grid (Fig. 1B). 127

Our task design included four distinct levels of complexity, classified by the minimum number 129 

of chunked relations required to deduce the missing item (Fig. 1B). Control problems (“unary” 130 

condition) presented unsolvable puzzles that violated the abstraction rule by containing duplicate 131 

items within the same row or column. These problems served as a baseline (“null” complexity) 132 

for rule comprehension. Binary problems required participants to combine items within a single 133 

row or column, using an internal representation of the complete set, to determine which of the 134 

four possible items could occupy that position. Increasing the requirement to coordinate 135 

information across both a row and a column yielded ternary problems. Finally, problems 136 

requiring participants to combine information across multiple rows and columns simultaneously 137 

were classified as quaternary , the highest level of relational complexity in the task. 138 

139 

As in previous studies that adopted the LST and related RC tasks, participants’ performance on 140 

our task declined as relational integration demands increased. Across the four complexity levels, 141 

performance accuracy declined as RC increased (Fig. 1D; F(1.43, 62.99) = 111.54, p < 0.001, ηp²142 

= 0.717). Conversely, response times (Fig. 1E) showed a progressive increase with complexity 143 

(Fig. 1E; F(2.20, 96.64) = 144.74, p < 0.001, ηp² = 0.767). 144

Band-specific frontoparietal activity and phase synchronisation support behavioural 241 

performance across relational demands 242 

Building on the region- and band-specific power effects described above, we next asked whether 243 

reasoning behaviour was linked to changes in FPN synchronisation, as observed in fMRI studies 244 

(4, 5, 44). For each participant, we calculated change scores from lower to higher complexity and 245 

tested whether changes in FPN synchronisation statistically mediated the relationship between 246 

changes in nodal power and changes in performance (Fig. 3). We modelled changes relative to 247 

the highest level of complexity (Quaternary), contrasting Quaternary with Binary (Model 1) and 248 

Quaternary with Ternary (Model 2). This approach isolates neural and behavioural changes 249 

associated with increasing relational demands as task complexity approaches its upper bound. 250 

We found no evidence that changes in FPN synchronisation mediated the relationship between 251 

nodal power and behavioural performance (Fig. 3; SI Appendix, Fig. S6). However, examination 252 

of the individual pathways suggested that the relationships between FPN activity and behavioural 253 

performance differed according to both relational complexity and frequency band. 254

Distinct complexity-related corticothalamic gain modulation in frontal and parietal cortices 286 

Given the observed changes in frontal and parietal activity and cross-regional synchronisation 287 

with increasing relational complexity, we next examined potential circuit-level mechanisms 288 

using a corticothalamic neural field model (CTM) applied to the source-reconstructed EEG 289 

spectra (SI Appendix, Methods, Fig. 4A). Within this framework, scalp EEG activity reflects the 290 

balance between the activity of cortical excitatory pyramidal neurons and inhibitory 291 

interneurons, while thalamic contributions arise from interactions between excitatory activity in 292 

the relay nuclei and inhibitory activity in the reticular nucleus (29, 45). We interpret the fitted 293 

parameters in three families: local cortical gains, corticothalamic loop gains, and temporal 294 

filtering parameters. Local cortical gains index the balance of recurrent excitation and inhibition 295 

within each cortical region. Corticothalamic and intrathalamic gain index estimated feedback 296 

through relay and reticular thalamic populations. Synaptodendritic parameters index the duration 297 

of postsynaptic integration. Because we did not observe significant lateralisation effects in the 298 

parameter estimates (p FDR > 0.05), we averaged the frontal and parietal model parameters across 299 

both hemispheres for subsequent analyses. 300

Discussion 382 

Humans can integrate multiple relations, enabling reasoning beyond simple associations (46). 383 

Here, we showed that frontal and parietal regions may support this process through distinct 384 

neural mechanisms. As complexity increased, frontal and parietal regions exhibited distinct load-385 

dependent changes in oscillatory power and synchronisation. Biophysical modelling extended 386 

these results by revealing a circuit-level division. The frontal cortex supported longer-sustained 387 

neural integration by modulating the local excitatory-inhibitory balance, while parietal regions 388 

reorganised thalamocortical loops to accommodate relational integration demands. Together, 389 

these findings reframe frontoparietal interactions not as a uniform recruitment of shared 390 

resources, but as regionally differentiated computational strategies in which synchronisation is 391 

necessary but not sufficient for problem-solving, and in which excitatory–inhibitory rebalancing, 392 

rather than gain amplification, is likely key to supporting relational reasoning. 393 

394 

At the regional level, increasing relational complexity was associated with opposing shifts in 395 

broadband power at the frontal and parietal cortices. Spectral parameterisation indicated that 396 

these effects were driven by region-specific changes in aperiodic activity, alongside selective 397 

recruitment of periodic oscillations. Frontal regions showed an upward shift in aperiodic activity 398 

with increasing demand, whereas parietal regions showed a downward shift, indicating divergent 399 

adaptations across the frontoparietal system. Aperiodic spectral features have recently emerged 400 

as a sensitive, domain-general index of cognitive demand across tasks, including serial 401 

subtraction, working memory, task switching, and interference paradigms (20, 22). Extending 402 

this literature, the present findings demonstrate that aperiodic adaptations during relational 403 

integration are not uniform but regionally differentiated, suggesting distinct computational roles 404 

within the frontoparietal system. 405 

406 

Superimposed on these broadband shifts were dissociable band-limited effects. Increased 407 

relational complexity was characterised in the frontal cortex by enhanced theta power and 408 

attenuated beta power, whereas the parietal cortex showed reductions in both alpha and beta 409 

power. This spectral profile differs from prior representational analyses of the same time 410

primarily encoded in frontoparietal alpha and beta bands, consistent with dynamic redistribution 412 

of representational weight as demands increase (15, 16). Together, these findings suggest that 413 

stable encoding and online construction of relations rely on partly separable mechanisms: alpha 414 

and beta support representational stability, while frontal theta increases with demand, reflecting 415

accounts linking lateral frontal theta to top-down control and adaptive network coordination 417 

during cognitively demanding operations (47-50). Accordingly, Lu, Dermody, Duncan and 418 

Woolgar (22) demonstrated frontal theta recruitment across distinct cognitive control paradigms 419 

that did not explicitly manipulate relational integration. The present findings extend previous 420 

work by showing that lateral frontal theta recruitment is sensitive to the problem's complexity, 421 

suggesting that integrative binding demands may be a specific computational driver of control-422 

related theta dynamics. 423 

424 

Consistent with prior work, increasing relational demands were accompanied by reductions in 425 

parietal alpha and beta activity, often linked to higher cognitive load and active processing (21, 426 

41, 42). However, this pattern is not universal; some studies report increases in these bands with 427 

task difficulty (22). This discrepancy may reflect differences in task structure. Our multi-level 428 

complexity manipulation revealed nonlinear neural recruitment that simpler dual-level contrasts 429 

may have missed. More broadly, these findings suggest that spectral modulation depends on how 430 

information is used. Beta attenuation may reflect a shift from stable maintenance to flexible 431 

processing, indicating that relational integration preferentially engages dynamic updating rather 432 

than sustained representations. 433 

434 

Our mediation analysis identified that covarying frontoparietal theta and beta power and inter-435 

regional synchronisation differentiate discrete levels in relational processing demands, building 436 

on previous fMRI research showing increased integration during relational tasks (2, 4, 5, 7, 51, 437 

52). Beta activity was behaviourally relevant, but greater synchrony was not always beneficial. 438 

Excessive network engagement predicted poorer performance at higher complexity. This may 439 

indicate that efficient coordination, rather than maximal activation, supports reasoning. Beta 440 

effects were linked to transitions to the highest complexity, consistent with a role in large-scale 441

synchronisation for higher-order relational processing, with disruptions in such coordination 443 

likely constraining the formation of complex relational abstractions (44, 53, 54). 444 

445 

Complementing the empirical results, the CTM modelling results suggest that increasing 446 

complexity was associated with reduced excitatory and inhibitory cortical gains, maintaining 447 

balance. In addition, relational encoding demands induced regionally dissociable adjustments in 448 

synaptodendritic temporal dynamics. Parietal cortex exhibited faster synaptic decay as 449 

complexity increased, consistent with a narrowing of the temporal integration window. 450 

Conversely, the frontal cortex showed concurrent slowing of both rise and decay kinetics, 451 

supporting more sustained integration (29, 30, 55). These opposing adjustments suggest 452 

complementary temporal regimes, in which faster parietal decay may enhance temporal precision 453 

and rapid updating of relational representations, while prolonged frontal integration may support 454 

sustained maintenance and manipulation over extended time scales. 455 

456 

Cortico-cortical and corticothalamic net gain remained largely unchanged across complexity, 457 

indicating that stability is maintained through compensatory local adjustments rather than large-458 

scale reconfiguration (30). The exception was a selective reduction in intra-thalamic gain in 459 

parietal circuits, suggesting downregulation of thalamic feedback specific to higher-order 460 

processing. Together, these findings suggest that relational complexity is supported by targeted 461 

local modulation in local gain, synaptic timing, and thalamic feedback while preserving global 462 

circuit stability; a pattern that may generalise across other cognitive demands and domains (20, 463 

22, 42, 56, 57). 464 

465 

In summary, the current work offers a novel multiscale perspective on how fronto-parietal-466 

thalamic neural dynamics at regional, inter-regional, and circuit levels adapt to increasing 467 

relational complexity. The results show that relational complexity is resolved by region-specific 468 

modulations in neural activity that maintain the global system’s stability. 469

Materials and Methods 473 

Participants 474 

Forty-seven participants (mean age = 25; age range = 19 - 33; 62% female) were recruited from 475 

the Brisbane Metropolitan area. Ethics approval was granted by the QIMR Berghofer Research 476 

Ethics Committee (P3644). Participants provided written informed consent and were eligible if 477 

they were aged between 18 and 35 years and had no previous reported history of mental health or 478 

neurological disorder. Of the 47 recruited participants, 2 were excluded due to poor data 479 

acquisition, leaving 45 participants for behavioural analyses; a further 11 were excluded from 480 

EEG analyses for failing to meet the minimum correct trial threshold ( ≥20 trials per condition), 481 

yielding a final EEG analysis sample of 34 participants. 482 

483 

Latin Square Task 484 

The LST is a nonverbal relational reasoning paradigm that systematically varies processing 485 

demands by manipulating the number of relations that must be interrelated simultaneously (10). 486 

Participants are presented with a four-by-four grid containing coloured circles and a target 487 

question mark (Fig. 1A). Participants must solve for the question mark by applying the rule that 488 

an item (in this case, one of four colours) can appear only once in a row or column. Task 489 

complexity is manipulated across four levels based on the number of relations that must be 490 

processed simultaneously to determine the solution. Unary relations (control puzzles) 491 

intentionally violate the task's underlying logic and are unsolvable, as indicated by the 'x' 492 

response option. Binary relations require processing along a single dimension (either a row or a 493 

column). Ternary relations require integrating information across a single row and a single 494 

column. Quaternary relations demand simultaneous processing across multiple rows and 495 

columns. 496 

497

positioned according to the international 10-20 system. EEG data were preprocessed offline 500 

using custom scripts adopting EEGLAB functions (58) in MATLAB (v2019b). Only correct 501 

trials were included in the final analysis, excluding incorrect or missing responses. Source 502 

modelling of scalp-level EEG sensor recordings was implemented using the M/EEG toolbox 503

Temporal and spectral decomposition of the frontal and parietal time series was performed using 508

Statistical Analysis 519 

Task accuracy, response time, and regional power were analysed using a one-way within-520 

subjects ANOVA to assess a main RC effect, implemented in JASP. Where a Mauchly’s test 521 

indicated that the assumption of sphericity was violated (p < 0.05), a Greenhouse-Geisser 522 

correction was applied. Normality was initially assessed using a Shapiro-Wilk test and inspection 523 

of the distribution. A log transformation was applied to the raw power values as the power 524 

spectra follow a log-normal distribution. The main effects of complexity from the power analysis 525 

were false discovery rate (FDR) corrected for multiple comparisons using the Benjamini-526 

Hochberg method implemented in MATLAB to correct for the four ROIs, power components, 527 

and frequency bands (66). For the CTM modelling analysis, FDR correction was applied across 528 

ROIs and the number of fitted parameters. 529
