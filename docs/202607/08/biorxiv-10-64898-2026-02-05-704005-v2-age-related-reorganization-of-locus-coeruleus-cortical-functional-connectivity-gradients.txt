Abstract 32 

The locus coeruleus (LC) regulates attention, arousal , and adaptive behavior via 33 

widespread noradrenergic projections to cortex, yet its large -scale cortical 34 

organization and age -related vulnerability remain poorly understood. Using 7T MRI 35 

during naturalistic movie -viewing with neutral and negative emotional valence, study -36 

specific LC delineation, and PET -derived receptor –transporter maps, we 37 

characterized LC –linked cortical or ganization in younger and older adults. Functional 38 

gradient analysis revealed two dominant axes: a stable primary gradient anchored by 39 

catecholaminergic receptor –transporter distributions, and a context -sensitive 40 

secondary gradient aligning with the visual –somatomotor (V –S) axis in neutral movie -41 

viewing and the sensorimotor –association (S –A) axis in negative movie -viewing .42 

During negative movie -viewing, older adults exhibited higher global and within -43 

frontoparietal control network dispersion, indicating reduced functional differentiation ,44 

a pattern further associated with poorer emotional wellbeing . By revealing 45 

chemoarchitectural and context -sensitive LC –linked cortical organization, current work 46 

identifies mechanisms underlying age -related LC –linked cortical reorganization with 47 

consequences for mental health. 48

# Introduction 59 

The locus coeruleus (LC) is the brain ’s principal source of noradrenaline (NA). 60 

Through widespread projections to cortical and subcortical regions, the LC –NA system 61 

regulates attention, arousal, stress responses, and adaptive behavior by modulating 62 

large -scale brain dynamics 1–5. LC neurons primarily operate in two firing modes : brief 63 

phasic bursts that enhance tas k-relevant processing and sustained tonic activity that 64 

maintains baseline arousal and behavioral flexibility 5,6 . By modulating neural gain, the 65 

LC –NA facilitates transitions between focused and exploratory behavioral states 5–9.66 

Convergent theoretical accounts, including the adaptive -gain 5, network -reset 4,10 , and 67 

integration –segregation frameworks 11,12 , propose that the LC –NA system shapes 68 

large -scale functional organization to balance global integration and segregation in the 69 

cortex. Anim al studies further demonstrate that elevated tonic LC activity can reshape 70 

long -range communication, supporting the LC ’s role as a neuromodulatory hub for 71 

large -scale cortical dynamics 13 .72 

Given the LC –NA system ’s capacity to influence cortex -wide patterns of activity 4,8,13 –73 

> 15

, a fundamental question arises: how is LC influence organized across the cortex? 74 

Specifically, do cortical regions couple to the LC in systematic patterns that reflect 75 

underlying biologically meaningful functional architecture? This question matters 76 

because the organization of LC –cortex connectivity has direct implications for 77 

cognitive and emotional function 16 –20 . Prior LC connectivity work has largely treated 78 

cortical areas as discrete units or relied on static seed -to -voxel connectivity 20 –22 ,79 

approaches that may not capture the broad organization of the cortex through the lens 80 

of the LC . Gradient -based mapping overcomes this limitation, positioning cortical 81 

regions according to their patterns of LC connectivity to reveal continuous transitions 82 

in LC –cortical functional organization 23 –25 . Crucially, gradients are not merely 83

Aging provides a critical window during which cortical organization changes 90 

substantially. For instance, late adulthood is associated with reduced cortical 91 

differentiation, whereby functional networks become less distinct and less selectively 92 

organized 25,28 –31 . This reduced differentiation is linked to poorer cognitive 93 

performance 25,28,30 and greater vulnerability to mental health difficulties in later life 32 .94 

According to theoretical account developed by Li and colleagues across a sustained 95 

line of work 33 –35 , these age -related changes reflect reduced neuromodulatory 96 

precision, which weakens neural gain control and reduces the selectivity of cortical 97 

responses. The LC is thus a strong mechanistic candidate , given its role as the primary 98 

source of cortical NA and its extensive projections to the cortex 1–7,10 . However, it 99 

remains un known whether aging is associated with a reorganization of LC -cortical 100 

connectivity, and whether this reflects a general loss of cortical differentiation or 101 

network -specific alterations. Answering this question will provide important insight into 102 

the role of neuromodulatory systems in brain aging and inform prevailing theories of 103 

age -related neural reorga nization. 104 

Here, we address the gaps in three critical ways . First, we map the gradients of LC –105 

cortical connectivity to characterize how the LC ’s influence is embedded within large -106 

scale cortical organization . Second, we assess age -related differences by quantifying 107 

dispersion within the LC –cortical functional connectivity gradient space and testing 108

To address these objectives, we leveraged ultra -high field 7 Tesla Magnetic 112

## LC functional organization reflects rostro -caudal differentiation 236 

To validate whether our gradient approach detect s meaningful spatial structure within 237 

the LC, we transposed , for each subject, the LC -voxel × cortical -parcel matrix and 238 

computed normalized an gular (cosine) similarity between LC voxels based on their 239

## Dominance analysis 821 

To assess how LC –linked cortical gradient maps are related to neurotransmitter 822 

receptor and transporter distributions, we applied dominance analysis 47 . This method 823 

quantifies the relative contribution of each receptor and transporter density map to the 824 

fit ( adjusted R² ) of a multiple linear regression model. It does so by fit ting models 825 

across all possible combinations of predictors (2 ᵖ − 1 submodels for p predictors). For 826 

each variable, total dominance is defined as the average increase in explained 827 

variance (R²) when that variable is added to each submodel, such that the sum of all 828 

predictors ’ dominance equals the adjusted R ² of the full model. This approach 829 

accounts for collinearity among predictors and provides interpretable estimates of 830 

relative importance. We also computed interactional dominance, defined as the 831 

increment al variance explained when adding a predictor to the model containing all 832 

other predictors, indexing unique variance in the presence of shared effects for 833 

negative ( Supplementary Figure 24) and neutral movie -viewing ( Supplementary 834 

Figure 25). Total and interactional dominance values were normalized by the full 835 

model fit (R² adj ) to facilitate comparisons across analyses. 836
