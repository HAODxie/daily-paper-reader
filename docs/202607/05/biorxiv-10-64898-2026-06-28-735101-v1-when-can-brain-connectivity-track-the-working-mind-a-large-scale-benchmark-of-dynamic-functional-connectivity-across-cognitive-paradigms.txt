# When Can Brain Connectivity Track the Working Mind? A Large-Scale Benchmark of Dynamic Functional Connectivity Across Cognitive Paradigms 

Mohammad Torabi 1, , Jean-Baptiste Poline 2, , and Georgios D. Mitsis 3, 

> 1Graduate Program in Biological and Biomedical Engineering, McGill University, Montreal, Canada

Dynamic functional connectivity (dFC) — the time-varying re-1

configuration of brain network interactions — has become a 2

widely adopted method for studying how neural dynamics re-3

flect ongoing cognition. Yet a fundamental question remains 4

unresolved: can dFC reliably track when a person is cognitively 5

engaged, and if not, why does it fail? Here, we address this ques-6

tion through a large-scale benchmark of seven widely used dFC 7

methods, evaluating how well each predicts task presence across 8

16 fMRI datasets encompassing over 1,500 participants and 28 9

distinct experimental settings, complemented by realistic simu-10 

lated data. 11 

Across experimental data, dFC-based tracking of cogni-12 

tive engagement was unreliable in many cases: most 13 

method–experiment combinations performed near chance, and 14 

no single method succeeded across all contexts. This failure, 15 

however, was not uniform. Both experimental and simulated 16 

data showed that decoding performance varied systematically 17 

with three interacting factors — experimental design, data qual-18 

ity, and the choice of dFC method — rather than depending on 19 

dFC features alone. 20 

Critically, we identify specific experimental design conditions 21 

associated with more reliable tracking: paradigms with longer, 22 

more regular task blocks and fewer task–rest transitions were 23 

substantially more decodable, while data quality independently 24 

influenced performance across methods. These findings offer 25 

actionable principles for when dFC can — and cannot — be 26 

expected to serve as a reliable marker of underlying cognitive 27 

states. 28

Introduction 33 

A central goal of cognitive neuroscience is to understand 34 

how the brain dynamically encodes and transitions between 35 

mental states. Dynamic functional connectivity (dFC) — the 36 

time-varying pattern of coordinated activity across brain net-37 

works — has emerged as a promising window into this pro-38 

cess [1–5]. Unlike static measures of connectivity, dFC aims 39 

to track the reconfiguration of neural networks as cognition 40 

unfolds in real time, offering potential as both a scientific 41 

tool for studying cognition and biomarker for neurological 42 

and psychiatric conditions. 43 

Despite widespread adoption, the validity of dFC as a mea-44 

sure of ongoing cognitive states remains poorly established 45 

[6–8]. Because most dFC studies use resting-state data — 46 

where no ground truth exists for what the brain "should" be 47 

doing — it has proven difficult to determine whether ob-48 

served fluctuations in connectivity reflect genuine cognitive 49 

dynamics or merely motion/physiological noise and mea-50 

surement artefacts [6, 9]. This ambiguity is compounded 51 

by the substantial "analytical flexibility" related to how dFC 52 

is computed: different methodologies applied to the same 53 

dataset may yield dramatically variable results, with inter-54 

method variability often comparable in magnitude to the tem-55 

poral fluctuations being studied [10]. The practical implica-56 

tion is alarming — a large body of published findings may 57 

critically depend on - sometimes arbitrary- methodological 58 

choices, with no principled basis for knowing which meth-59 

ods, if any, are reliably tracking mental state. 60 

This problem cannot be resolved by studying resting-state 61 

data alone. Pioneering work established that task-based 62 

paradigms can be used to this end: Gonzalez-Castillo et al. 63 

[11] showed that dFC states can track distinct mental tasks 64 

(e.g., math vs. memory) at the block level, and Xie et al. 65 

[8] used multitask datasets to compare methods in terms of 66 

their ability to segment time series into cognitively homo-67 

geneous periods. These contributions validated the logic of 68 

using tasks as ground truth — but 1) they were implemented 69 

at the timescale of sustained, minute-long task blocks, where 70 

cognitive states are stable and contrasts between conditions 71 

are large, and 2) validated on a single dataset. A more de-72 

manding and ultimately more informative test is whether dFC 73 

features, assigned to individual time points, can track cog-74 

nitive engagement at a finer temporal scale than sustained 75 

block-level contrasts — regardless of the temporal resolution 76 

intrinsic to each method. This requires detecting not only the 77 

broad contrast between two sustained cognitive states — as in 78 

block-level comparisons — but the moment-to-moment shift 79 

between being actively engaged in a task and being at rest. 80

Our results deliver a clear and consequential message. In 100 

experimental data, dFC-based tracking of cognitive engage-101

across broader contexts remains an open question. 121

neuroscience uses connectivity dynamics as a window into 123 

mental states. They provide a framework for addressing the 124 

uncertainty about whether dFC fluctuations reflect genuine 125 

cognitive dynamics or merely noise and measurement arte-126 

facts, examine whether widely adopted dFC methods are ac-127 

tually valid for tracking cognitive engagement in real time, 128 

and offer researchers actionable design principles that may 129 

increase the likelihood of reliable dFC-based cognitive state 130 

tracking. More fundamentally, this work provides a multi 131 

datasets and time resolved predictive validation framework to 132 

evaluate dFC methods and their interactions with paradigms 133 

across 28 experiments. 134 

Results 135 

To determine whether dFC can reliably track cognitive en-136 

gagement as it unfolds, and identify when and why it fails, 137 

we benchmarked seven dFC methodologies across 28 task-138 

fMRI experiments ( EXP.1 –EXP.28 ), each treated as an in-139 

dependent evaluation setting. We used the binary transition 140 

between task and rest as a ground-truth validity criterion, as-141 

sessing each method’s ability to decode cognitive state at the 142 

resolution of individual fMRI time points (TRs). Decoding 143 

performance was quantified using balanced accuracy (chance 144 

level = 50%), and intrinsic task-rest separability was assessed 145 

using the Silhouette Index (SI). Together, these metrics reveal 146 

both the reliability of dFC as a cognitive state tracker and the 147 

structure of the underlying feature space. For an illustration 148 

of the full prediction pipeline, see Figure 1. 149 

DFC Patterns Do not Always Track Cognitive Engage-150 

ment in Experimental Data. In experimental fMRI data, 151 

dFC-based decoding of ongoing cognitive engagement was 152 

unreliable across the majority of method–experiment com-153 

binations (Figure 2). Crucially, this near-chance decoding 154 

cannot be attributed to an absence of task-evoked signal in 155 

the underlying data: across experiments, the task-rest BOLD 156 

contrast (Cohen’s d, computed per region and taken as the 157 

maximum across regions) ranged from 0.19 to 2.06 (median 158 

0.46), with nearly all experiments (27 of 28, 96%) exceed-159 

ing 0.25 and about half (13 of 28, 46%) exceeding 0.5 (Sup-160 

plementary Figure 9). A measurable task-evoked response 161 

was therefore present in essentially every experiment, yet this 162 

did not translate into reliable single-TR decoding from dFC 163 

— and a strong contrast was not sufficient on its own (e.g., 164 

EXP.2 , with the largest contrast, was decoded near chance). 165 

Most combinations yielded accuracies near chance (~50%), 166 

with only 3 out of 196 combinations exceeding 70% and 18 167 

out of 196 significantly above chance. This near-chance per-168 

formance is not a marginal effect: it reflects a systematic 169 

failure of the examined dFC methods to detect the cognitive 170 

transition between engagement and rest in experimental data, 171 

even when a task-evoked signal is demonstrably present. 172 

A small cluster of experiments (EXP.12 , EXP.13 ,173 

EXP.14 ), all drawn from the same dataset ( ds003465 ), 174 

achieved peak accuracies of 70–79% when using the Sliding 175 

Window and Time-Frequency methods. Even these relatively 176 

favorable cases, however, were not robust across methods: 177 

performance dropped to near chance for Clustering and Dis-178 

crete HMM applied to the same experiments. This indicates 179 

that high performance is method-dependent rather than a sta-180 

ble property of cognitive engagement itself, and may arise 181 

from a specific alignment between experimental structure and 182 

methodological assumptions. 183 

These favorable cases, however, were the exception. The ma-184 

jority of experiments (18 out of 28) were not reliably de-185 

coded by any method, with accuracies below 60% and in 186 

many cases close to chance. Reliable single-TR decoding 187 

thus emerged only for specific combinations of experiment 188 

and method, and was rare across the benchmark as a whole. 189

Three Factors Predict When dFC Successfully Tracks 295 

Cognitive Engagement. Having established that dFC 296 

sometimes succeeds and often fails, we next address the fol-297 

lowing question: what specifically determines success? We 298 

conducted a top-vs-bottom profile analysis, stratifying runs 299 

of each experiment into the top and bottom 20% of decoding 300 

performance and computing standardized effect sizes (Co-301 

hen’s d) for a set of interpretable task design, signal-strength, 302 

and data-quality factors (Figures 5 and 6). This analysis 303 

transforms the benchmarking results into a predictive frame-304 

work: a set of empirically grounded conditions under which 305 

dFC can be expected to reliably track cognitive state. 306 

Three factors emerged as consistent and interpretable predic-307 

tors of performance. First, task-rest transition frequency 308 

was the strongest and most consistent negative predictor 309 

across methods, particularly for Sliding Window and Time-310 

Frequency (Cohen’s d ¥ ≠ 1.5 in experimental data, d < ≠2311 

in simulation). Experiments in which participants frequently 312 

switched between task and rest were systematically harder 313 

to decode, likely because rapid transitions demand temporal 314 

resolution that these dFC methods cannot achieve. This ef-315 

fect was present even under simulated, noise-free conditions, 316 

confirming that transition frequency imposes a design-level 317 

ceiling on cognitive state tracking that cannot be overcome 318 

by methodological refinement alone. 319 

Second, task-rest signal contrast (Cohen’s d of BOLD dif-320 

ferences between task and rest averaged across regions) was a 321 

consistent positive predictor: experiments with larger neural 322 

response differences were more decodable. This effect was 323 

stronger for state-based methods (CAP, Continuous HMM) 324 

than for state-free approaches, suggesting that methods that 325 

model discrete brain states may be especially sensitive to the 326 

magnitude of task-evoked responses. 327 

Third, task block structure — specifically the length and 328 

regularity of task epochs — was associated with performance 329 

in a method-dependent manner. Longer median task du-330 

rations facilitated decoding for Sliding Window and Time-331 

Frequency, while greater variability in block durations im-332 

paired performance for Time-Frequency, CAP, and Contin-333 

uous HMM. These findings indicate that the temporal regu-334 

larity of cognitive demands, not just their presence, shapes 335 

whether dFC can faithfully decode them. 336 

Crucially, the same factors predicted performance in both ex-337 

perimental and simulated data, but their effects were stronger 338 

in simulation. Transition frequency remained the dominant 339 

negative predictor, and the task-rest signal contrast effects 340 

became more pronounced — particularly for CAP — in the 341 

absence of real-world artefacts such as motion and physiolog-342 

ical noise. This consistency across conditions supports these 343 

factors as robust design-level determinants of cognitive state 344 

trackability, rather than features specific to either the noisy 345 

experimental data or the controlled simulated setting. 346 

Methods that remained near chance across all conditions 347 

(Clustering, Discrete HMM, Windowless) showed weak and 348 

inconsistent factor associations, consistent with the interpre-349 

tation that their limitations are methodological rather than 350 

driven by task design or signal properties. 351 

Together, these three factors — transition frequency, task-rest 352 

signal contrast, and block regularity — constitute a practi-353 

cal and empirically validated framework for predicting when 354 

dFC will more likely reliably track ongoing cognition. 355 

Although not explicitly captured by these factors, the cog-356 

nitive domain of an experiment may modulate performance 357 

indirectly through its influence on task structure or signal am-358 

plitude. A preliminary analysis across cognitive domains is 359 

provided in Supplementary materials Section, but does not 360 

support strong domain-specific conclusions at this stage. 361

A central aspiration of cognitive neuroscience is to decode 403 

the dynamics of cognition using brain dynamics. DFC has 404 

attracted wide interest as a tool for achieving this goal by 405 

tracking the moment-to-moment reconfiguration of brain net-406 

works as cognitive states unfold in real time. However, the 407 

empirical foundation for this ambition is stil not sufficiently 408 

elucidated. Our results provide the most systematic evalua-409 

tion to date of whether dFC can achieve this overall goal, and 410 

under which conditions. 411 

Overall, our results strongly suggest that dFC-based tracking 412 

of cognitive engagement is unreliable in many experimen-413 

tal contexts — but this failure does not appear to be solely 414 

a limitation of the dFC approach itself. Across both exper-415 

imental and simulated data, we observed that decoding per-416 

formance varied systematically with the experimental design, 417 

the data quality, and the dFC method used: some combina-418 

tions yielded reliable task decoding, while many others did 419 

not. This suggests that the unreliability seen in experimen-420 

tal data also depends on the conditions under which dFC is 421 

applied. The key question is therefore not simply whether 422 

dFC can track cognition, but under what conditions it is more 423 

likely to do so — and our results provide a principled, em-424 

pirically grounded answer structured around three interact-425 

ing factors that shape success: experimental design , data 426 

quality , and methodological choice . Within experimental 427 

design, three specific features are particularly consequential: 428 

task-rest transition frequency, task-rest signal contrast, and 429 

task block regularity. 430 

The Gap Between Simulated and Experimental Perfor-431 

mance. One of the most striking findings of the present study 432 

is the contrast between simulation and experimental perfor-433 

mance. When simulation data were used, several methods 434 

achieved near-perfect single-TR decoding of task engage-435 

ment; in experimental data, many experiment–method com-436 

binations did not exceed chance levels. This gap helps iden-437 

tify where the real difficulty lies: even if the information 438 

needed to track cognitive engagement is captured by con-439 

nectivity dynamics, our results suggest it can be systemati-440 

cally obscured in experimental data by the complex, aggre-441 

gate nature of the BOLD fMRI signal (motion and physio-442 

logical artefacts, neurovascular coupling) and by suboptimal 443 

experimental design. 444 

This distinction has implications regarding how we may in-445 

terpret the results of dFC studies. If the drivers identified 446 

here generalize, they would predict that published findings of 447 

meaningful dFC dynamics during cognition are more likely 448 

to emerge from studies with favorable experimental designs 449 

and high data quality, while null results may disproportion-450 

ately reflect unfavorable conditions rather than the absence 451 

of real cognitive dynamics. We have not verified this directly 452 

across the existing literature as it is outside the scope of the 453 

current study, and doing so would require the kind of system-454 

atic meta-analytic coding we identify as a future direction. 455 

Nevertheless, our framework provides a principled basis for 456 

adjudicating between these possibilities post hoc when evalu-457 

ating individual studies, and for designing future studies that 458 

optimize the conditions under which dFC can reliably detect 459 

cognitive dynamics. 460 

Experimental Design as the Primary Determinant. Of 461 

the three main drivers, experimental design exerts the most 462 

fundamental influence on dFC performance — fundamental 463 

in the sense that its effects persist even under noise-free sim-464 

ulated conditions, beyond the reach of methodological refine-465 

ment or data-quality improvements. Our factor analysis iden-466 

tifies three specific design features that jointly determine how 467 

detectable task engagement will be. 468 

Task-rest transition frequency is the dominant limiting fac-469 

tor. Frequent transitions between cognitive engagement and 470 

rest reduce decoding performance strongly and consistently 471 

across methods, and this effect persists even under noise-free 472 

simulated conditions. This indicates a fundamental temporal 473 

resolution constraint: current dFC methods — even the best-474 

performing ones — cannot faithfully track cognitive state 475 

when the brain is asked to switch rapidly between engage-476 

ment and rest. This constraint is design-level, not method-477 

ological, and cannot be overcome by choosing a better algo-478 

rithm. It implies that event-related and rapid mixed designs, 479 

which are standard in much of cognitive neuroscience, are 480 Torabi et al. | A Large-Scale Benchmark of dFC Methods bioR ‰iv | 11

Data Quality as a Modulating Factor. Beyond experimen-519 

tal design, the quality of the data plays an independent and 520 

substantial role. By data quality, we mean the extent to which 521 

the measured BOLD signal faithfully reflects the underlying 522 

task-related neural dynamics — which is degraded by mo-523 

tion, physiological fluctuations, and the indirect, aggregate 524 

nature of the BOLD signal. The large performance gap be-525 

tween simulated and experimental data — present even for 526 

experiments with favorable designs — indicates that these 527 

factors attenuate task-related dynamics in ways that experi-528 

mental design alone cannot compensate for. Many of these 529 

factors are intrinsic to fMRI and only partly controllable, 530 

which sets a practical ceiling on what design optimization 531 

alone can achieve. 532 

Importantly, the contributions of experimental design and 533 

data quality are separable in our framework. Simulations that 534 

preserve task timings of experimental data, but eliminate the 535 

influence of factors such as motion and physiological arte-536 

facts, allow us to isolate what design alone constraints; com-537 

parisons between simulated and experimental performance 538 

for the same experiment reveal the additional attenuation at-539 

tributable to data quality. This decomposition suggests that 540 

for some experiments, design is the primary bottleneck; for 541 

others, data quality is the dominant limitation. Both must be 542 

considered when interpreting why a given study succeeded or 543 

failed in terms of using dFC to track cognitive engagement. 544 

Methodological Choice Determines What Can Be Ex-545 

tracted. Given favorable experimental design and sufficient 546 

data quality, methodological choice determines whether the 547 

available task-related signal can actually be detected. Across 548 

all conditions, methods fell into two clearly distinct groups. 549 

Sliding Window and Time-Frequency methods showed the 550 

most consistent sensitivity to task-related dynamics in exper-551 

imental data, with some state-based approaches (CAP, Con-552 

tinuous HMM) exhibiting strong performance under ideal-553

trast, Clustering, Discrete HMM, and Windowless methods 555 

achieved near chance performance across virtually all con-556

limitations are primarily methodological and cannot be at-558 

tributed to design or data quality. 559 

This methodological stratification has direct implications for 560 

the field. Encouragingly, Sliding Window — among the most 561 

widely used dFC methods [10] — was also among the better-562 

performing methods for decoding task engagement in our 563 

benchmark, suggesting that the field’s most popular choice 564 

performed comparatively well under the conditions tested 565 

here. This alignment was not consistent, however: Clus-566

framework, while Time-Frequency performed comparably to 568 

Sliding Window; yet, it remains substantially underrepre-569

An important nuance is that methods are not consistent in 574 

terms of which experiments are most tractable. This method– 575 

experiment interaction implies that different dFC approaches 576 

are sensitive to distinct properties of the underlying signal, 577

changeable. The choice of method is not merely a technical 579 

preference — it encodes assumptions about the temporal and 580 

spatial structure of cognitive dynamics that may or may not 581 

match the paradigm at hand. 582

Compact. The consistently low Silhouette Index values 625

A Framework for Contextualizing dFC Results. The 647 

three main drivers identified here — experimental design, 648 

data quality, and methodological choice — are rarely re-649 

ported systematically in published dFC studies, yet our data 650 

show they predict a substantial portion of the variance in 651 

decoding performance across 28 independent experiments. 652 

If these drivers generalize beyond our benchmark dataset, 653 

they would predict the following specific pattern: studies 654 

that combine favorable experimental designs (low transition 655 

frequency, high signal amplitude, regular block structure) 656 

with good data quality and sensitive dFC assessment methods 657 

would succeed in using dFC to track cognition, while those 658 

that do not fulfill these conditions would not. Whether this 659 

prediction holds across the broader literature remains to be 660 

tested — doing so would require a systematic meta-analysis 661 

that codes published studies along these dimensions and asks 662 

whether performance outcomes co-vary accordingly. We 663 

view this as an important direction for future work. 664 

In the meantime, our results provide a practical basis for con-665 

textualization: Rather than asking "does dFC track cogni-666 

tion?" as if it were a binary question with a universal an-667 

swer, studies should report the design and data-quality factors 668 

identified here to help answer the more tractable question of 669 

when dFC tracks cognition, allowing readers to situate results 670 

within the broader performance landscape that our bench-671

a comprehensive and reproducible framework for computing, 673 

comparing, and benchmarking dFC methods across datasets 674

as critical. 677

Methods and Materials 749 

We developed a unified framework to evaluate and interpret 750 

dFC methods for predicting task presence at the level of indi-751 

vidual time points. The pipeline operates on BIDS-formatted 752 

fMRI datasets with associated task timing information and 753 

supports multiple datasets and experimental paradigms, en-754 

abling large-scale benchmarking. Time-resolved dFC fea-755 

tures were extracted from preprocessed BOLD fMRI signals 756 

and used to classify task presence (task vs. rest) at each time 757 

point. Performance was assessed using balanced accuracy 758 

across methods and experiments. 759 

fMRI Datasets Selected from OpenNeuro. Functional 760 

MRI data were obtained from the OpenNeuro repository. To 761 

ensure suitable data quality and coverage for our study, we 762 

applied the following inclusion criteria: (i) the dataset con-763 

tains functional scans ( func datatype); (ii) includes at least 764 

50 participants; (iii) has at least one block-designed task 765 

paradigm with sufficiently long task and rest periods; (iv) is 766 

BIDS-compliant; and (v) includes events.tsv files with 767 

task/stimulus timing information. Out of the entire reposi-768 

tory, only 22 datasets met these criteria, yielding a total of ap-769 

proximately 50 experiments. After extracting task presence 770 

and rest/task labels, experiments with sub-optimal design -771 

those producing extreme class imbalances or unreasonable 772 

task presence time courses - were excluded. The final se-773

detailed overview of these datasets, including number of sub-775

All datasets were processed starting from raw fMRI NIfTI 777 

images. First, we applied fMRIprep through nipoppy ,778 

a lightweight framework that streamlines preprocessing for 779

Deriving Task Presence Time Courses. For each exper-865 

iment, task presence was determined separately for each in-866 

dividual scan (run), ensuring subject- and run-specific label-867 

ing. Initial labels were assigned using the onset and dura-868 

tion values provided in the events.tsv files: time points 869 

were marked as task-present (1) or rest (0). To account for 870 

the hemodynamic delay in fMRI signals due to neurovascular 871 

coupling mechanisms, the binary time course was convolved 872 

with a canonical HRF implemented using SPM’s double-873 

gamma function implemented in the nilearn toolbox. The 874 

resulting HRF-convolved time course, sometimes termed a 875 

block regressor [7] or expected task response, was then down-876 

sampled to the MRI TR and binarized again to produce the 877 

final task presence time course. 878 

Binarization was performed using a Gaussian Mixture Model 879 

(GMM), which assigns probabilities to each time point be-880 

longing to each cluster, i.e., rest or task. This probabilistic 881 

framework allows identification and removal of ambiguous 882 

“gray zone” time points corresponding to transitions between 883 

rest and task. Excluding these uncertain samples improves 884 

classifier performance and avoids confusing the model with 885 

unclear labels. 886 

The GMM-based procedure was implemented as follows: 887 

1. Initial clustering: Fit a two-component GMM to all 888 

time points. 889 

2. Downsampling: Cluster probabilities were downsam-890 

pled to MRI TR resolution. For sliding window dFC 891 

methods, probabilities were further aligned to the time 892 

points of the resulting dFC matrices. 893 

3. Discarding ambiguous points: Time points with 894 

probabilities < 0.99 for both clusters were removed. 895 

This threshold worked well for most experiments. In 896 

cases where a class became empty, the threshold was it-897 

eratively increased until both classes contained at least 898 

one sample. For a small number of experiments, even 899 

this adjustment did not resolve the issue; visual inspec-900 

tion revealed sub-optimal or unclear block designs that 901 

made reliable task/rest assignment impossible. These 902 

experiments ( N = 6 ) were excluded at this stage and 903 

are not among the 28 experiments reported in this 904 

study. 905 

4. Handling transitional clusters: If the two clusters 906 

did not clearly correspond to rest and task (e.g., the 907 

higher-mean cluster < 0.75 or the lower-mean cluster > 908 

0.25), the GMM was refitted with three clusters, with 909 

the middle cluster representing transition periods. 910 

Finally, all task presence time courses were visually in-911 

spected. Experiments with unclear rest/task assignments or 912 

extreme class imbalance were discarded from further anal-913 

yses. Illustrations of the GMM-based clustering and label 914 

assignment are shown in the Task Timing stage of Figure1, 915 

as well as in Figures 14 and 15 (Supplementary materials), 916 

including examples of experiments with optimal and sub-917 

optimal design. 918

Feature Extraction and Dimensionality Reduction. Af-942 

ter computing dFC, features were extracted for classification 943 

of task presence, with state-free and state-based methods han-944 

dled separately. 945 

State-free methods. Time-resolved dFC matrices (time ◊946 

region ◊ region) were vectorized into edge-wise feature vec-947 

tors (time ◊ connection, with 100 · (100 ≠ 1) /2 = 4950 con-948 

nections). 949 

Samples corresponding to transition periods identified by 950 

the task presence procedure were excluded. To reduce the 951 

effects of inter-subject variability, features were then cen-952 

tered for each subject by subtracting the subject-specific 953 

mean. This step removes subject-specific offsets while pre-954 

serving within-subject dynamics. Following subject center-955 

ing, features were standardized using StandardScaler 956 

(zero mean, unit variance), fitted on training data and applied 957 

to validation and test sets when performing cross-validation. 958 

Dimensionality reduction was then performed using super-959 

vised Partial Least Squares (PLS), which learns latent com-960 

ponents that maximize covariance between dFC features and 961 

task labels [45]. PLS was applied within a cross-validated 962 

pipeline, where the number of components was treated as 963 

a hyperparameter and selected using group-aware cross-964 

validation on the training data. Candidate component num-965 

bers ranged from low-dimensional (e.g., 2-5) to moderately 966 

high-dimensional (e.g., up to 50), and selection was based on 967 

balanced accuracy. All transformations were fitted on train-968 

ing folds only and applied to validation and test data to pre-969 

vent information leakage. 970 

As a comparison, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was 971 

also implemented, with the number of components selected 972 

via cross-validation; PCA results are reported in the Supple-973 

mentary materials. 974 

State-based methods. For state-based approaches (CAP, 975 

Clustering, Continuous/Discrete HMM, Windowless), fea-976 

tures were derived from the temporal expression of con-977 

nectivity states rather than full edge-wise matrices. This is 978 

because, in these methods, the connectivity matrices corre-979 

spond to a fixed set of states estimated at the group level 980 

and shared across time points; the time-resolved, subject-981 

specific information instead resides in how each time point 982 

expresses those states. Accordingly, we extracted state prob-983 

abilities (HMM-based methods), distances to state centroids 984 

(CAP/Clustering), or state weights (Windowless), resulting 985 

in feature matrices of shape (time , K ), where K is the num-986 

ber of states. 987 

Because these features are compositional (non-negative and 988 

summing to one), we applied an isometric log-ratio (ILR) 989 

transformation, mapping them to Euclidean space with di-990 

mensionality K ≠ 1. Samples corresponding to transition pe-991 

riods were removed prior to analysis. Subject centering and 992 

standard scaling were then applied as described above. The 993 

resulting normalized ILR-transformed features were used di-994 

rectly without additional PCA or PLS, as their dimensionality 995 

is already low. 996 

For each experiment and dFC method combination, features 997 

from all subjects were concatenated into a final matrix of 998 

shape (subject ◊ time , features ). These concatenated, nor-999 

malized, and transformed features serve as the inputs for task 1000 

presence prediction stage. 1001 

Remarks on manifold learning approaches. We also eval-1002 

uated Laplacian Eigenmaps (LE) with Procrustes alignment, 1003 

following prior work [46]. While LE can provide meaning-1004 

ful low-dimensional representations at the individual-subject 1005 

level, its embeddings are not uniquely defined and are sub-1006 

ject to arbitrary orthogonal transformations. Aligning these 1007 

embeddings across subjects using Procrustes analysis re-1008 

quires an implicit assumption of point-wise correspondence 1009 

between time points, which does not generally hold in dFC 1010 

data. 1011 

In experiments with identical task timing across subjects, 1012 

Procrustes alignment can enforce correspondence between 1013 

samples that share the same temporal position and label, in-1014 

troducing a form of information leakage and artificially in-1015 

flating classification performance. In more realistic settings 1016 

with variable timing across subjects, this assumption breaks 1017 

down, and alignment becomes unreliable or requires discard-1018 

ing large portions of the data. 1019 

Due to these limitations, and the risk of spurious alignment 1020 

driven by temporal structure rather than neural features, we 1021 

did not consider LE followed by Procrustes to be a reli-1022 

able approach for cross-subject representation learning in this 1023 

study. Notably, this approach yielded higher decoding per-1024 

formance than the methods reported here — but we interpret 1025 

this apparent advantage as a consequence of the information 1026 

leakage described above rather than as evidence of genuinely 1027 

better representation learning. 1028

Task-Rest Separability. In addition to supervised classifi-1070 

cation, we evaluated whether dFC features exhibit intrinsic 1071 

separability of rest and task-present states—that is, whether 1072 

task and rest time points form distinct structures in feature 1073 

space independent of any trained model. We refer to this 1074 

property as task-rest separability. 1075 

Following [46], task-rest separability was quantified using 1076 

the Silhouette Index (SI), which measures how well samples 1077 

with different labels are separated in the feature space based 1078 

on pairwise distances. For each dFC method, the SI was 1079 

computed using the true task labels (task vs. rest) and the 1080 

corresponding feature vectors. Higher SI values indicate that 1081 

rest and task-present samples are more distinctly organized 1082 

in feature space. 1083 

Importantly, SI does not involve training a classifier; instead, 1084 

it evaluates the geometric structure of the feature space with 1085 

respect to known labels. As such, it provides a complemen-1086 

tary, model-independent perspective on whether task-related 1087 

information is reflected as a globally separable structure, or 1088 

instead requires supervised methods to be detected. 1089
