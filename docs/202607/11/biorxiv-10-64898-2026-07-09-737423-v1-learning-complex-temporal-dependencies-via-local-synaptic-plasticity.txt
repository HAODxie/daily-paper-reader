# Abstract 

The ability to extract and exploit temporal structure across diverse tasks is central to human cognition. Neuroscientists have typically relied on recurrent neural networks (RNNs) trained with backpropagation through time (BPTT) when modelling neural and behavioural processes such as decision-making and motor control. However, this algorithm has limited biological plausibility, hence the computational principles underlying efficient learning of temporal dependencies remain unresolved. Here, we investigate temporal predictive coding (tPC), a recently proposed framework that extends predictive coding to the temporal domain while preserving local Hebbian update rules. We analyse and extend tPC to establish its relationship with several influential computational models of learning in RNNs, including BPTT, reservoir computing, and eligibility propagation (e-prop). We first demonstrate a functional equivalence between tPC and tBPTT1, a variant of BPTT in which gradients are propagated only one time step into the past. We then show that tPC can leverage reservoir dynamics to encode short-range temporal context, and simultaneously sculpt neural trajectories in state space to support downstream readout. We further demonstrate that hierarchical recurrent dynamics can facilitate learning of more complex temporal dependencies, while additionally conferring robustness to strong distractors. Finally, we show that tPC networks can be augmented with biologically inspired eligibility traces to solve temporally extended context-dependent tasks. Together, these results reveal that relatively simple recurrent networks governed by local plasticity can support temporal learning in more complex settings than previously appreciated. 

# Author summary 

To navigate the world, our brains must constantly track how events unfold over time, whether we are predicting the next word in a sentence or timing a tennis swing. Neuroscientists often use artificial neural networks to study how the brain learns these sequences. However, these models are often not biologically realistic, since they adjust neuronal connections using information that individual neurons would not have access to in the brain. Here, we examine an alternative framework called temporal predictive coding, which is designed to better reflect how brain networks may be organised. We show that these biologically inspired networks are surprisingly powerful: they can remember recent context and, when arranged in layers, can learn increasingly complex patterns. # Introduction 1

Humans successfully exploit the myriad regularities present in the external world to 2

adapt and generalise to novel environments. Such regularities are not only spatial but 3

also temporal in nature, unfolding across multiple timescales and often requiring the 4

integration of information over extended periods. From understanding speech and music 5

to predicting the behaviour of others and navigating dynamic environments, the ability 6

to extract and leverage temporal structure is fundamental to cognition. Recent work 7

has begun to uncover how temporal information may be represented across distributed 8

neural populations, including the discovery of sequential neural activity and temporal 9

context representations in cortical and hippocampal circuits [1вҖ“3]. Yet, computational 10 

accounts that explain how the brain might learn such temporal dependencies remain 11 

relatively underdeveloped. 12 

In computational neuroscience, a standard approach for modelling tasks with 13 

temporal dependencies, such as decision-making or motor control, involves recurrent 14 

neural networks (RNNs) trained using backpropagation through time (BPTT) [4, 5]. 15 

However, BPTT is generally considered biologically implausible and unlikely to be 16 

implemented by the brain [6]. In particular, BPTT requires the precise storage of past 17 

neural states and the backward propagation of error signals across time [7]. 18 

Nevertheless, owing to its effectiveness and versatility, BPTT is still the dominant 19 

training method for RNNs in computational neuroscience, despite the development of 20 

alternative approaches with varying degrees of biological plausibility. 21 

More generally, learning and inference in the brain remain subjects of considerable 22 

interest, and a variety of explanatory frameworks have been proposed [8вҖ“11]. The 23 

theory of predictive coding is one such well-established framework, widely used by 24 

neuroscientists to explain perceptual processes [11вҖ“14]. Consistent with the Bayesian 25 

Brain hypothesis [15], predictive coding posits that the cortex constructs internal 26 

models of the external world and uses them to generate predictions about incoming 27 

sensory input. A key appeal of predictive coding is that it not only provides a 28 

conceptual account of perception in terms of prediction error minimisation, but also 29 

offers a biologically plausible neural implementation based on local learning rules. To 30 

date, however, most predictive coding formulations have focused on static settings in 31 

which the temporal structure of stimuli is not explicitly modelled [16, 17]. 32 

Consequently, predictive coding networks have recently been augmented with 33 

recurrent dynamics to enable temporal prediction [18]. This framework, termed 34 

temporal predictive coding (tPC), has been shown to approximate Kalman filtering in 35 

both linear and non-linear dynamical systems, while retaining simple update rules based 36 

on local Hebbian plasticity [18]. Moreoever, tPC has been found to outperform Hopfield 37 

networks on sequential memory tasks [19], and to develop visual and spatial 38 

representations resembling those observed in biological neurons [18, 20]. A natural next 39 

step is therefore to assess whether tPC could serve as an alternative to BPTT-trained 40 

RNNs for modelling tasks involving more complex temporal dependencies. 41 

Here, we demonstrate how the tPC framework could be extended to solve several 42 

such tasks, without requiring explicit storage of past neural states or deviating from 43 

local plasticity rules. After establishing a functional equivalence between tPC and a 44 

truncated form of BPTT known as tBPTT1, we show that tPC can exploit its reservoir 45 

dynamics to encode temporal structure while simultaneously shaping neural trajectories 46

## Learning complex cross-temporal interactions via a hierarchical 252 

## architecture 253 

Although the standard tPC model can learn simple non-Markovian sequences, we 254 

reasoned that such a framework may become limiting when learning more complex 255 

temporal dependencies. In contrast to tBPTT, which explicitly propagates error signals 256 

backward through time (Fig 4a), tPC relies on local updates and therefore learning 257 

depends more critically on the instantaneous hidden state representation to encode 258 

temporally relevant context. In task settings involving non-trivial cross-temporal 259 

dependencies, temporal structure may not be easily learnable from the current state 260 

representation. 261 

We therefore hypothesised that more efficient learning of complex temporal 262 

dependencies could emerge from interactions between multiple recurrent reservoirs 263 

operating over partially distinct temporal representations. Intuitively, hierarchical 264 

recurrent dynamics may allow information to be integrated across multiple timescales 265

## Learning temporally extended context-dependent tasks via 318 

## eligibility traces 319 

Having shown that hierarchical recurrent dynamics enable efficient learning of complex 320 

temporal dependencies, we next investigate how tPC may be augmented with eligibility 321 

traces to support credit assignment across long temporal delays. Eligibility traces have 322 

been extensively studied in the context of three-factor learning rules and synaptic 323 

tagging and capture [31, 32]. Experimental evidence suggests that coincident pre- and 324 

post-synaptic activity does not necessarily induce immediate long-term potentiation 325 

(LTP). Instead, local co-activity can produce a transient biochemical stateвҖ”commonly 326 

referred to as a synaptic tag or eligibility traceвҖ”which temporarily marks a synapse as 327 

eligible for subsequent plasticity [33]. Lasting synaptic modification may then occur 328 

only upon the later arrival of a global modulatory signal, such as dopamine [32]. 329 

Recent computational work has demonstrated how biologically inspired eligibility 330 

traces can be formalised in RNNs to support online learning without requiring BPTT. 331 

In particular, the Random Feedback Local Online (RFLO) algorithm [34] and the 332 

eligibility propagation (e-prop) framework [35] showed that RNNs can maintain local 333 

eligibility traces that accumulate historical activity and thereby enable temporally 334 

delayed credit assignment. Here, we extend this formulation to a predictive coding 335 

framework, yielding an augmented architecture which we refer to as tPC-E. 336

# Discussion 376 

BPTT is generally acknowledged to be biologically implausible, but remains widely used 377 

in computational neuroscience. This is due to its effectiveness in learning complex 378 

temporal dependencies that are central to many behavioural and neural processes, 379 

including sequence generation, sensory prediction, working memory, and 380 

decision-making. Here, we presented a framework for extending predictive coding 381 

networks to learn such temporal dependencies while relying only on local synaptic 382 

plasticity. 383 

After first establishing a functional equivalence between tPC and tBPTT1, we 384 

showed that tPC can exploit reservoir dynamics to encode short-range temporal context, 385 

while simultaneously shaping neural trajectories in state space to support downstream 386 

readout. We next demonstrated that introducing a hierarchical architecture with delays 387 

facilitates learning of non-trivial temporal dependencies and enables robust maintenance 388 

of memory content even in the presence of strong distractors. Finally, we showed that 389 

tPC networks can be augmented with biologically inspired eligibility traces to solve 390 

tasks involving long delay durations. Together, these findings position tPC within a 391 

broader landscape of temporal learning frameworks (Fig. 7), highlighting important 392 

connections to reservoir computing, BPTT, and e-prop, in addition to the Kalman filter 393 

correspondence identified in previous work [17]. 394 

We aimed here to identify and investigate various computational ingredients that 395 

biological neural networks may exploit to support temporal learning across different 396 

settings, which we explored here using relatively simple tasks. Our work therefore 397 

invites several natural extensions. First, while our discrete-time implementation of 398 

tPC-H captures delayed interactions at a functional level, it does not provide a fully 399 

satisfactory treatment of delays from the perspective of predictive processing theory [30], 400 

which may instead call for a continuous-time formulation. In the case of tPC-E, we 401 

assumed a fixed inverse time constant of Оұ = 0 .5, consistent with previous work [36]. A 402 

more biologically principled approach would, however, consider a distribution of time 403 

constants, consistent with the substantial heterogeneity observed across neural 404 

systems [37, 38]. Eligibility traces could also be incorporated across multiple hierarchical 405 

levels, potentially with distinct temporal dynamics at each level. Finally, future work 406 

should test whether these mechanisms scale to richer behavioural settings involving 407 

higher-dimensional inputs and more complex temporal dependencies. Recent work on 408 

how dendritic morphology and neuronal dynamics may support temporal learning in 409 

deep networks provides one possible direction for such extensions [39]. 410 

While we focused our comparison here on BPTT, another influential algorithm, 411 

real-time recurrent learning (RTRL) [40], also computes exact gradients of the loss 412 

function with respect to recurrent weights by accounting for dependencies across all 413 

time steps. Unlike BPTT, RTRL performs these computations online, but at the cost of 414 

prohibitive computational and memory requirements, in addition to relying on non-local 415 

update rules. Nevertheless, the appeal of online gradient computation has motivated 416 

numerous efforts to derive tractable approximations to RTRL [36]. RFLO represents 417

support memory maintenance and flexible retrieval. The mechanisms studied here 439 

should therefore be viewed as addressing one part of the temporal learning problem, 440 

rather than as a complete account of long-timescale memory and credit assignment. 441 

Of note, although biological plausibility is framed here primarily in terms of locality 442 

in space and time, a deeper understanding of neural computation could substantially 443 

broaden the scope of biologically plausible temporal credit assignment. In the case of 444 

BPTT, for example, hippocampal reverse replay [51, 52] raises the intriguing possibility 445 

that biological systems may possess mechanisms for propagating information backward 446 

across experienced temporal sequences [6]. Moreover, recent studies have found evidence 447 

that different stages of a behavioural sequence may be represented by distinct but 448 

systematically related neural populations in prefrontal cortex [53, 54]вҖ”mapping 449 

temporal sequences onto a spatial pattern of activity. Such representations could, in 450 

principle, provide a substrate through which error-related signals are assigned across 451 

time, potentially enabling richer forms of temporal credit assignment than those 452 

captured by standard locality-based formulations. 453 

Despite recent advances in machine learning, state-of-the-art architectures such as 454 

transformers often rely on increasingly long context windows to capture long-range 455 

dependencies, incurring substantial memory and computational costs. Understanding 456 

how biological systems achieve efficient temporal credit assignment may therefore offer 457 

useful insights for both neuroscience and machine learning. Progress towards such an 458 

understanding will likely depend on close interaction between experimental and 459 

theoretical neuroscience, both to uncover novel biological mechanisms and to formalise 460 

their computational principles. 461
