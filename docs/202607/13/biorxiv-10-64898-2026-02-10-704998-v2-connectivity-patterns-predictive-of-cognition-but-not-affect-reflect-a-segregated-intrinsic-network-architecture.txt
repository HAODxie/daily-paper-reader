# Abstract 1

The brain’s intrinsic organization into resting-state networks has long been suggested to be 2fundamental for the offline support of mental processes. Extensive task-based evidence support 3the relevance of the crosstalk between network segregation, supporting systems specialization, 4and network integration, allowing to flexibly implement complex behavior. However, only 5scarce evidence focusing on few behavioral measures directly link changes in these network 6properties at rest with interindividual differences in behavior. 7In this work, using a comprehensive set of behavioral measures together with resting-state 8functional magnetic resonance imaging from the human connectome project, we assessed 9whether connectivity patterns predictive of behavior reflected segregation or integration based 10 on GINNA, a 33 resting-state-networks atlas with cognitive characterization. 11 We found that connectivity relevant for behavior organizes into 3 main latent dimensions, 12 summarizing Cognition , Positive Affect and Negative Affect . Crucially, we found that 13 connectivity predictive of Cognition , but not Affect , was associated with global network 14 segregation and reduced network integration. We further reveal differential resting-state-15 networks involvements, with Cognition associated with the segregation of higher-level resting-16 state-networks, and the integration of lower-level, visual networks. 17 All in all, the present results suggest that cognition may rest upon a segregated, modular 18 intrinsic brain architecture. 19 

# Author summary 20 

How psychological processes are maintained when the brain is not engaged in an explicit task 21 remains unclear. Resting-state networks offer a candidate intrinsic architecture, but whether 22 interindividual differences in diverse behavioral domains relate to this architecture in similar 23 ways is unknown. We combined Human Connectome Project resting-state fMRI with 58 24 behavioral measures to identify functional connectivity patterns predictive of behavior and 25 examine how they relate to network segregation and integration. These patterns organized into 26 three latent dimensions summarizing Cognition, Positive Affect, and Negative Affect. 27 Cognition-related patterns aligned with a segregated intrinsic architecture, showing stronger 28 within-network and weaker between-network connectivity, particularly among higher-order 29 resting-state networks, with complementary integration of visual networks. Affective patterns 30 showed less consistent network signatures, suggesting a more distributed or context-dependent 31 intrinsic organization. 32

# 1 Introduction 33 

The intrinsic organization of the brain into networks, and how it relates to mental processes, 34 has attracted much attention. Using resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging (rs-35 fMRI), it was shown that the brain is intrinsically organized into networks, i.e., groups of 36 regions with tightly coupled spontaneous blood oxygen-level dependent (BOLD) signal 37 fluctuations (Biswal et al., 1995; Fox et al., 2005; Fox & Raichle, 2007). These so-called 38 resting-state networks (RSNs), ever since their first observations (Biswal et al., 1995), have 39 been suggested to hold a functional relevance with respect to cognitive processes and behavior, 40 largely because their topology follows the boundaries of cognitive systems (Seitzman et al., 41 2019; Uddin et al., 2023). This strong assumption is nowadays reflected in the tendency for 42 researchers to refer to RSNs by their putative cognitive functions, rather than an anatomically 43 grounded taxonomy (Uddin et al., 2019, 2023). 44 Support for the notion that RSNs represent fundamental cognitive modules was initially 45 gathered from comparisons with tasks, with a first evidence of a similar network architecture 46 between rest and task (Smith et al., 2009). Additional evidence showed that task network 47 architecture is shaped by RSN architecture (Cole et al., 2014, 2016) and that RSNs display 48 modularity with respect to their task engagement (Bertolero et al., 2015; Yeo et al., 2015). 49 Motivated by these evidence, a 33 resting-state networks atlas grounded in meta-analytical 50 cognitive characterization was recently proposed, summarizing potential associations with 51 cognition in light of the neuroimaging literature (Gillig et al., 2025). Yet, while informative, 52 spatial comparison between RSNs and task activation maps provide evidence only of a coarse, 53 associative, but not an exploratory, relationship (Mill et al., 2017). 54 More direct evidence come from the study of resting-state functional connectivity (FC), 55 measuring the strength of the functional coupling between pairs of regions or networks 56 (typically, measured as the correlation between resting-state timeseries). FC is now widely used 57 to predict a wide range of behavior and latent cognitive processes, including, but not limited to, 58 intelligence (Thiele et al., 2024), general cognition (Ooi et al., 2022), working memory (Avery 59 et al., 2020), or personality (Nostro et al., 2018), indicating that individual differences are 60 reflected as long-term changes in the brain functional organization (Stevens & Spreng, 2014; 61 Wu et al., 2023). 62

3While studies linking variations in FC to behavior are now many, those that probe the relevance 63 of the intrinsic RSN architecture are scarce. Switching from a resting to a cognitive state implies 64 large-scale network reconfigurations, i.e. , regions change their network affiliations to flexibly 65 adapt to ongoing demand (Salehi et al., 2020). However, it has been shown that decreased task-66 related network reconfiguration was associated with better task performance across language, 67 reasoning and working memory (Schultz & Cole, 2016), suggesting that RSNs provide a 68 scaffold for efficient active cognitive processing. 69 Taken together, these results outline a framework in which resting-state FC may index the 70 offline maintenance of behavior through mechanisms of Hebbian plasticity, where repeated 71 task-related co-activation strengthens intrinsic functional couplings. In this framework, RSNs 72 scaffold cognition because behaviorally relevant couplings increasingly align with RSNs 73 delineations, therefore constraining and facilitating subsequent task-evoked activations. 74 To probe the relevance of the intrinsic RSN organization with respect to behavior, network 75 theory has proven to offer interesting tools. Of note, the brain constantly alternates between 76 integrated (increased cross-network communication) and segregated (increased network 77 independence) states, both at rest and during tasks (Capouskova et al., 2023; Cohen & 78 D’Esposito, 2016; Shine et al., 2016; Shine & Poldrack, 2018; Wang et al., 2021), with 79 segregation linked to the maintenance of a modular, specialized information processing 80 architecture (Sporns & Betzel, 2016), while integration allows cross-systems communication 81 to meet complex task demands (Bassett et al., 2011; Bertolero et al., 2015, 2018; Capouskova 82 et al., 2023; Cohen & D’Esposito, 2016; Shine et al., 2016). 83 Most evidence linking network segregation and integration to cognitive performance comes 84 from task-based studies. During active task performance, greater integration has been 85 associated with faster and more accurate performance under high cognitive demand (Shine et 86 al., 2016). Consistently, a relationship with task complexity was established: higher efficiency 87 correlated with increased network integration during a working memory task, whereas it was 88 associated with greater segregation for a simpler motor task (Cohen & D’Esposito, 2016). 89 Similarly, in the context of stimulus-response (S-R) learning, it was found that initial, goal-90 directed learning involved increased integration, while later, automated S-R associations were 91 associated with increased segregation (X. Wang et al., 2024). Altogether, these task-based 92 findings highlight the importance of the balance between network segregation to promote, local, 93

4specialized within-network processing for overlearned processes, and integration to flexibly 94 adapt to changing demands (Cohen & D’Esposito, 2016; Shine & Poldrack, 2018). 95 While a clear picture starts to emerge when it comes to task-related cognitive processing, 96 highlighting the relevance of both segregation and integration mechanisms, evidence on how 97 their interplay promotes the offline, at-rest maintenance of behavior remains scarce. While the 98 resting state was found to be characterized by a balance between segregated and integrated 99 states (Wang et al., 2021), individual differences in segregation and integration were also linked 100 to cognitive abilities. Stronger integration was associated with better general cognitive ability, 101 while crystallized intelligence and processing speed involved stronger segregation, and a 102 tendency towards balance supported better memory (Wang et al., 2021). As this suggests 103 opposed effects between the maintenance of global versus narrower processes, questions remain 104 regarding a wider range of cognitive, but also socio-emotional and personality functioning, and 105 to what extent their supporting mechanisms are shared or unique across this diverse range of 106 behavioral domains. In addition, it remains to be determined whether these mechanisms differ 107 across RSNs as a function of the behavioral domain involved. 108 In this study, using data from the Human Connectome Project, we investigated whether 109 individual differences in multiple domains of behavior were predicted by intrinsic FC patterns, 110 and whether these patterns related to specific integration/segregation balance across different 111 RSNs. 112 First, we performed FC prediction of 58 behavioral measures spanning cognitive, socio-113 emotional, and personality domains. The Haufe method (Haufe et al., 2014) was used to gain 114 insights into the relevance of region-level FC. We next performed exploratory factor analysis 115 to explore the latent FC structure associated with behavior. Last, a reliability analysis was 116 conducted both at the global and RSN level to identify whether latent FC factors were 117 associated with reliable changes in network segregation and integration across components of 118 a cognitively characterized RSN atlas. 119

## 2.3 Resting-state fMRI acquisition and preprocessing 136 

The four runs (two sessions, each with LR and RL acquisition) of the resting-state fMRI data 137 present in the HCP dataset were used for analyses. Details regarding the rs-fMRI acquisition 138 are available in Smith et al. (Smith et al., 2013). Briefly, fMRI data were acquired on a 3-T 139 Siemens Skyra with a 32-channel head coil and a gradient-echo echo-planar imaging (EPI) 140 sequence (repetition time: TR = 720 ms, echo time: TE = 33.1 ms, flip angle = 52°, 2-mm 141 isotropic voxel resolution, multiband factor = 8). The minimally preprocessed rs-fMRI data was 142 recovered and each of the two runs (LR and RL acquisition) corresponding to each session 143 (REST1 and REST2) were separately concatenated and subjected to noise regression with 24 144 head motion parameters, eight mean signals from white matter and cerebrospinal fluid, and four 145 grey matter signals. Global signal regression was used based on evidence that it improves 146 performances for functional connectivity prediction of behavior (Li et al., 2019). In-scanner 147 head motion was measured by framewise displacement (FD), and, similarly to Thiele et al. 148 (2024), subjects were only included if mean FD < 0.2 mm, proportion of spikes (FD > 0.25 149 mm) < 20%, and no spikes were above 5 mm (Parkes et al., 2018). Frames with above-threshold 150 FD (> 0.2 mm) were censored and replaced by an interpolation of the two neighboring frames. 151 Fully preprocessed rs-fMRI data were further parcellated into 448 regions of interest (Cremona 152 et al., 2025) (Fig. 1), resulting in 448 timeseries averaged by parcels. 153

## 2.4 Resting-state networks definition 154 

To define resting-state-networks (RSNs), the Groupe d’Imagerie Fonctionnelle Network Atlas 155 (GINNA) was used (Gillig et al., 2025) (Fig. 1). The 448 parcels of the region-level parcellation 156 atlas (Cremona et al., 2025) are assigned to one of the 33 networks (32 cortical, 1 subcortical) 157 included in GINNA (Gillig et al., 2025), in addition to 1 brainstem network. Similarly to 158 Cremona et al. (2025), we used a version of this atlas without overlap (1 region belongs to 1 159 RSN), such that FC measurements are not cross-dependent between RSNs. The choice of this 160 specific network atlas was motivated by i) its previous cognitive characterization relying on 161 neurosynth-based meta-analytic decoding, allowing to put into perspective RSNs relationship 162 with behavior at rest in light of their task involvement; ii) its higher granularity, potentially 163 providing finer associations between behavior and RSNs. 164 165

## 2.5 Functional connectivity 166 

Functional connectivity (FC) was computed for each individual participants and each 167 acquisition session using Pearson correlation as a metric, followed by Fisher r-to-z transform, 168 resulting in 448x448 FC matrices for each individual and each of the two sessions of rs-fMRI 169 acquisition. Last, FC estimates for the two sessions were averaged to produce a single functional 170 connectivity estimate per subject. While it is more common to concatenate all rs-fMRI data 171 prior to computing functional connectivity, this method was shown to be comparable to 172 averaging out after computing functional connectivity for each individual scans (Cho et al., 173 2021). 174 

## 2.6 Functional connectivity prediction of behavior 175 

To predict each of the 58 selected behavioral measures, we employed a nested cross-validation 176 procedure with linear kernel ridge regression (Fig. 2A). This model was chosen as it has been 177 demonstrated to achieve similar performance as deep learning approaches (He et al., 2020) 178 while reducing by several orders of magnitude the computational cost. 179 We used a cross-validation scheme analogous to the one described by Tian & Zalesky (2021), 180 namely, a repeated split-half with nested 10-folds cross-validation procedure. First, participants 181 were split in half. The first half was used as training set, and split into 10 folds for cross-182 validated hyperparameter optimization (regularization parameter alpha, 5 values on a logscale 183 ranging from -3 to 3). The optimal model was further retrained on the whole training set and 184 used to predict behavioral scores from functional connectivity of the second half (test) set. The 185 same procedure was repeated using the second half as training and the first half as testing, such 186 that each split-half iteration resulted in two estimates of performance. 187 This procedure was repeated 100 times by varying the split-half sampling (Tian & Zalesky, 188 2021) in order to obtain stable performance estimates (Varoquaux et al., 2017). The final 189 performance estimation was obtained by averaging across the 100 repetitions. Control over 190 confounding variables was performed following a procedure of regression over the output of 191 the prediction (predicted y) (Dinga et al., 2020). 192

## 2.7 Prediction performance significance 193 

Performance for predicting participants’ behavioral scores from FC was measured as the 194 standardized root mean squared error (sRMSE) (i.e, RMSE divided by variance) computed after 195 accounting for confounds (Dinga et al., 2020). 196 To assess whether each behavioral measure could be predicted from FC better than chance, we 197 compared the distribution of prediction errors to a distribution of null prediction errors 198 generated by permutations. FC matrices were randomly permuted and the whole nested cross-199 validation procedure was repeated. Importantly, confounds were not permuted, so as to preserve 200 the relationship between confounds and predicted variables (Dinga et al., 2020). Similarly to 201 the empirical predictions, the procedure was iterated 100 times, producing a distribution of 202 prediction performance that would be obtained if there was no association between FC and 203 behavior. Due to the half-split cross-validation scheme, each iteration produced two estimates, 204 resulting in a null distribution of n = 200. For each latent factor, the observed performance 205 estimates were compared to the null distributions using a paired t-test (Tian & Zalesky, 2021), 206 followed by Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate correction for multiple comparisons 207 (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995) (q < 0.05). All non-significant behavioral measures were 208 excluded from downstream analyses. 209

## 2.10 Resting-state networks segregation and integration 249 

For each of the k latent CB factors, we investigated network segregation and network 250 integration, both at a global level and at a RSN level (Fig. 2C). Importantly, segregation and 251 integration are not measured directly from participants’ FC matrices, but are derived from 252 predictive connectivity (Haufe) coefficients, and therefore reflect how connectivity patterns 253 associated with behavior are organized. Network integration was operationalized as the average 254 CB patterns between regions belonging to the target RSN and all other regions, while network 255 segregation was operationalized as the average CB patterns of all regions within the target RSN 256 (Fig. 2C). Positive values for integration or segregation indicated a positive association with 257 higher values of the latent factor, while negative values were predictive of lower values of the 258 factor. Global (pooled) estimates of network segregation and integration were obtained by 259 averaging estimates across RSNs. Network metrics significance was assessed post-hoc, based 260 on their reliability across prediction repetitions (95% confidence interval). 261 We operationalized strong evidence for a segregated CB pattern as the joint presence of positive 262 within-network CB weights (segregation) and negative between-network CB weights 263 (integration), indicating that higher behavioral scores are associated with stronger within-264 network connectivity and weaker between-network connectivity. This definition was preferred 265 because it preserves the respective contributions of intra- and inter-network connectivity and 266 avoids additional thresholding and sign-handling choices when applied to signed FC-derived 267 matrices. As a complementary analysis, we also quantified global segregation using the system 268 segregation index (SSI) (Chan et al., 2014) to assess whether the main pattern generalized to a 269 standard graph-theoretical metric. It is further motivated by the necessity, for a RSN to be 270 considered modular, to elicit increased processing while reducing its communication with other 271 RSNs (Avena-Koenigsberger et al., 2018; Sporns & Betzel, 2016). Conversely, strong evidence 272 for an integrated pattern was defined by the opposite configuration, reflecting the switch from 273 internal processing to distributed processing. The presence of a positive within-network CB 274 without reliably negative between-network CB was interpreted as only partial evidence of 275

## 3.1 Functional connectivity prediction of behavior 292 

First, we performed functional connectivity (FC) prediction of behavior to predict individual 293 differences using each of the 58 behavioral measures. Using a half-split cross-validation 294 procedure (Tian & Zalesky, 2021) together with a simple kernel ridge regression model (He et 295 al., 2020), we were able to significantly predict 52 of the 58 included measures (paired t-tests, 296 FDR q < 0.05) (Fig. 3, Table 1). # 4 Discussion 422 

Understanding how the intrinsic brain organization supports interindividual differences in 423 behavior remains a central challenge in systems neuroscience. Using a combination of FC 424 prediction of behavior, interpretability tools and network theoretical metrics, we provide 425 evidence of the correspondence between the intrinsic, RSN brain architecture, and the FC 426 architecture supporting behavior. Using a comprehensive set of behavioral measures from a 427 large sample of healthy young adults, we show that behaviorally relevant connectivity patterns 428 at rest organize into a small number of latent dimensions, and that these dimensions are 429 supported by distinct large-scale architectural principles. Specifically, we show that 430 behaviorally relevant FC organizes into 3 principal latent dimensions summarizing Cognition ,431 

Positive Affect and Negative Affect . Crucially, higher Cognition scores were associated with 432 predictive connectivity patterns reflecting strong global resting-state network segregation, 433 consistent with the interpretation that the intrinsic brain architecture may reflect a functionally 434 specialized cognitive organization. By contrast, Affect did not and was instead associated with 435 a negative system segregation index, suggesting that it is supported by a more flexible, 436 distributed organization in which RSNs do not represent core affective modules. We 437 furthermore highlight differential RSN involvements in Cognition , with strong segregation of 438 RSNs associated with higher-level cognitive processes, and integration of lower-level, visual 439 RSNs, suggesting a hierarchical organization of the intrinsic functional architecture that 440 scaffolds cognitive performance. 441 

## 4.1 Latent connectivity–behavior factors capture shared neural 442 

## substrates across behaviors 443 

The emergence of three latent connectivity–behavior factors indicates that predictive FC 444 patterns are structured around a limited number of shared neural mechanisms despite the wealth 445 of behavioral measures significantly predicted. Importantly, these factors were derived from 446 connectivity–behavior mappings rather than behavioral measures alone, implying that they 447 reflect common neural substrates underlying behavioral variance. The three latent factors 448 combined to explain approximately 25% of the variance, reflecting the high dimensionality and 449

The Cognition factor aggregated diverse cognitive measures 451 spanning working memory, reasoning, language, and processing speed, among others, 452 consistent with the presence of shared neural components supporting cognitive performance 453 (Chen et al., 2022). Positive Affect determinants were more associated with social-emotional 454 components (extraversion, friendship, emotional support), while Negative Affect ones reflected 455 more the experience of negative emotions (neuroticism, feelings of fear anger, and sadness), 456 indicating that these dimensions are not exactly anticorrelated, but reflect distinct processes. 457 However, replication using the Schaefer200 parcellation (Schaefer et al., 2018) resulted in these 458 two affective factors to be regrouped into a single bidirectional Affect factor, suggesting that FC 459 patterns underlying Positive Affect and Negative Affect remain substantially anticorrelated, or 460 that this finding is sensitive to the parcellation scheme employed. 461 Notably, no factor corresponding uniquely to personality traits emerged, despite significant 462 prediction of individual personality measures. Cognition . This contrasts with previous reports made on adolescents 467 indicating shared common connectivity features for personality that largely differed from 468 cognition (Chen et al., 2022). 469 

## 4.2 Connectivity patterns predictive of cognition reflect global 470 

## network segregation at rest 471 

A central finding of this study is that the connectivity patterns associated with higher cognitive 472 ability reflected greater global network segregation and reduced integration at rest. Importantly, 473 networks were defined intrinsically (RSNs), not from their behavioral relevance. Additionally, 474 this pattern remained consistent when the architecture was quantified using the system 475 segregation index, indicating that the main result was not specific to our initial 476 operationalization of segregation and integration. Together with the complementary analyses 477 showing robustness under an alternative parcellation and network scheme at 17 Networks 478 resolution, but not 7 Networks resolution (Schaefer et al., 2018; Yeo et al., 2011), this supports 479 the robustness of the finding while highlighting the relevance of RSN granularity. Network 480 segregation is widely thought to reflect functional specialization and efficient within-system 481 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted July 9, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.02.10.704998 doi: bioRxiv preprint

21 processing (Avena-Koenigsberger et al., 2018; Benso et al., 2025; Sporns & Betzel, 2016), 482 whereas integration dynamically supports coordination across systems when task demands 483 require it (Bertolero et al., 2015; Cohen & D’Esposito, 2016; Shine et al., 2016; Shine & 484 Poldrack, 2018; X. Wang et al., 2024). Within this framework, our results further suggest that 485 connectivity patterns predictive of cognition are more consistent with a segregated intrinsic 486 organization at rest, and therefore with the interpretation that RSNs may capture functionally 487 specialized cognitive components (Barrett & Kurzban, 2006; Benso et al., 2025; Bertolero et 488 al., 2015, 2018; Sporns & Betzel, 2016). 489 These findings do not contradict task-based evidence showing that complex cognitive 490 performance requires increased integration across networks (Cohen & D’Esposito, 2016; Shine 491 & Poldrack, 2018; X. Wang et al., 2024). Rather, they support a complementary account in 492 which segregation and integration play distinct roles depending on brain state. While task 493 execution necessitates transient integration to enable flexible coordination (Shine et al., 2016; 494 Shine & Poldrack, 2018), the resting-state organization may reflect the long-term maintenance 495 of a specialized architecture that reduces the need for large-scale reconfiguration during task 496 engagement (Schultz & Cole, 2016). Consistent with this view, learning studies have shown 497 that faster stimulus–response learning is associated with increased segregation at later learning 498 stages, whereas earlier, goal-directed learning involves greater integration (Wang et al., 2024). 499 Together, these findings suggest that large-scale changes in segregation at rest may index the 500 offline consolidation or efficiency of overlearned processes, while integration remains a key 501 mechanism to enable flexible adaptation to changing environmental demands. 502 

## 4.3 Differential involvement of higher-level cognitive versus visual 503 

## RSNs in Cognition 504 

We further observed that the relationship between cognitive ability and network organization 505 varied across RSNs. Specifically, higher cognitive ability was associated with predictive 506 connectivity patterns reflecting strong segregation of multiple higher-order association 507 networks implicated in self-referential processing, reasoning, language, working memory, 508 theory of mind, and cognitive control (Gillig et al., 2025). This finding extends our current as 509 well as prior evidence for shared neural substrates across Cognition (Chen et al., 2022) by 510 showing that cognitive ability is associated with connectivity patterns reflecting coordinated 511

It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted July 9, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.02.10.704998 doi: bioRxiv preprint

22 segregation across a distributed set of higher-level RSNs, rather than by a single network or 512 localized mechanism. 513 Importantly, the consistency of segregation effects across multiple RSNs within a latent 514 cognition factor suggests that cognitive performance relies on shared but non-specific 515 architectural principles across networks. This aligns with evidence that RSNs do not map one-516 to-one onto task-defined functional systems (Davis et al., 2017; Thompson & Fransson, 2017). 517 Instead, our results support the view that RSNs are not strictly encapsulated cognitive modules 518 (Benso et al., 2025; Noble et al., 2024), but instead represent more elementary processing units, 519 that are flexibly combined during task execution. Supporting this view, task-evoked activation 520 patterns are partly predicted by common resting-state components (Scholz et al., 2024), and, 521 although better task performance is associated with reduced reconfiguration between rest and 522 task, substantial reorganization remains necessary to meet task demands (Davis et al., 2017; 523 Salehi et al., 2020; Schultz & Cole, 2016). 524 In contrast, predictive connectivity patterns linked to higher cognitive ability reflected greater 525 integration of several visual networks. This pattern suggests that lower-level visual systems 526 may play a more integrative role within the intrinsic architecture associated with cognition, 527 potentially facilitating efficient information transfer to higher-order systems. Such an 528 interpretation is consistent with evidence that resting-state connectivity constrains task-evoked 529 information flow and supports activity propagation during cognitive processing (Ito et al., 530 2017). Together, these results suggest that intrinsic RSN organization may provide a531 hierarchical scaffold that supports cognitive performance through differentiated roles of 532 segregation and integration. 533 

## 4.4 Absence of strong segregation signatures for affective factors 534 

Unlike cognition, affective connectivity-behavior dimensions were not associated with reliable 535 global effects in network segregation or integration, and only partial, network-specific effects 536 were observed. Because GINNA’s intrinsic RSN structure has been shown to relate to known 537 cognitive components (Gillig et al., 2025), a possibility remained that the RSN composition 538 used to compute segregation and integration metrics may be optimized for the cognitive 539 dimension, and that different networks may underlie the affective dimension. To test this 540 possibility, new network compositions were obtained by a data-driven clustering independently 541

23 for each dimension. Interestingly, this change did not alter the results, and no new segregation 542 or integration effects were uncovered. This suggests that the lack of network segregation for 543 the predictive connectivity associated with Affect does not only relate only to a misalignment 544 with the intrinsic network architecture, but rather, that the FC basis of Affect is characterized 545 by an absence of homogeneity within and between-systems ( i.e. , clusters regroup both positive 546 and negative edges). All in all, while the present evidence suggest that the intrinsic organization 547 into RSNs provides a basis for a modular cognitive organization, it does not support RSNs as 548 core affective modules (Dan et al., 2023; Lindquist et al., 2012; Lindquist & Barrett, 2012). 549 This aligns with contemporary views that emotional processes are not supported by specific 550 RSNs, but by a reconfiguration of whole-brain, distributed functional networks that overlap 551 with systems involved in processes including, but not limited to, conceptualization, attention, 552 motor function, and executive control (Dan et al., 2023; Lindquist et al., 2012; Lindquist & 553 Barrett, 2012; Pessoa, 2017). 554 Another possibility is that affective traits rely on more distributed and context-sensitive 555 interactions that are not well captured by static segregation metrics. Alternatively, affective 556 variance may be more state-dependent, reducing the stability of trait-level intrinsic signatures. 557 More generally, these findings are consistent with evidence that resting-state functional 558 connectivity has limited or variable predictive power for certain individual differences, 559 particularly outside the cognitive domain (Chen et al., 2022; Ooi et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2020; 560 Wu et al., 2023). While this hinders our ability to firmly conclude on the absence of an intrinsic 561 segregated architecture associated with Affect , our complementary findings of a reliable 562 negative system segregation index for Affect suggests that the weak connectivity features 563 predicting affective measures primarily distribute outside of RSNs as defined in the present 564 study. Though network segregation was also not observed using a data-driven partitioning, this 565 does not rule out the alternative possibility that a segregated pattern might appear with sufficient 566 predictive power. Additionally, the absence of strong segregation effects does not necessarily 567 imply a lack of neural organization for affect, and rather, the present findings suggest that its 568 architecture may not be primarily driven by the intrinsic RSN architecture, or by their large-569 scale segregation–integration balance. 570

## 4.5 Limitations and methodological considerations 571 

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, the present results are necessarily limited by 572 the modest predictive strength of the models. Although many behavioral measures were 573 predicted significantly better than chance, the absolute amount of explained variance remained 574 small, meaning that the connectivity–behavior associations identified here reflect a small but 575 statistically reliable signal rather than strong predictive accuracy. As in all FC prediction of 576 behavior studies, this high prediction error directly constrains model interpretability, since the 577 reliability and biological meaning of feature weights depend on prediction performance (Chen 578 et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). This limitation propagates across the successive steps of our 579 pipeline, from prediction to Haufe inversion, latent factor analysis, and network-level 580 summaries. Nonetheless, our conclusions are based on out-of-sample testing combined with a 581 reliability analysis of feature weights across repetitions of the prediction, which partially, but 582 not completely, mitigates this issue. Accordingly, the present findings should be interpreted 583 cautiously, as describing broad organizational tendencies in behaviorally relevant connectivity 584 patterns rather than providing strong or mechanistic evidence about how intrinsic brain 585 architecture determines behavior. Additionally, the kernel regression model, chosen for its state-586 of-the-art performance (He et al., 2020; Schulz et al., 2020), operates on the space of global 587 similarities between whole-brain FC matrices, therefore reducing the ability to detect sparse, 588 local effects. 589 Second, because latent connectivity–behavior factors depend on the behavioral space sampled, 590 the present findings should be interpreted with respect to this specific 58-measure panel. In 591 particular, differences between cognition and affect may partly reflect differences in construct 592 heterogeneity, psychometric reliability, and sensitivity of the selected measures, in addition to 593 differences in intrinsic functional organization. Next, we used a discrete RSN partition which 594 highlights modular specialization, but may underrepresent hub regions, despite their known 595 relevance for cognition (Bertolero et al., 2018) and in the underlying dynamics of the resting 596 state (Saggar et al., 2022). Gradient-based approaches better capture these intermediary 597 positions and continuous transitions (Margulies et al., 2016). Our findings therefore should not 598 be taken to imply that intrinsic brain organization is exclusively discrete, and future work should 599 test whether this cognitive effect is complemented by hub- or gradient-based signatures of large-600 scale integration. 601

25 An additional limitation concerns residual head motion. Because motion can increase short-602 range connectivity while decreasing longer-range connectivity, it could bias within- versus 603 between-network summaries and thus affect segregation-related metrics (Power et al., 2012; 604 Satterthwaite et al., 2012). Although motion was addressed through nuisance regression, 605 stringent exclusion, and frame censoring prior to FC estimation, residual effects may remain. 606 Recent work suggests that, after stringent censoring, such effects may more often attenuate or 607 distort than spuriously inflate brain-behavior associations, but do not disappear entirely (Kay et 608 al., 2025; Parkes et al., 2018; Pavlovich et al., 2025). Given that prediction performance for 609 Affect was lower than for Cognition, the absence of reliable segregation effects for Affect may 610 partly reflect reduced signal-to-noise in the underlying brain–behavior associations, which 611 could be more susceptible to residual motion-related biases. 612 613 Last, static resting-state FC cannot capture dynamic reconfiguration or causal interactions. 614 Future work integrating task-based and dynamic connectivity analyses (Cutts et al., 2025), as 615 well as longitudinal and interventional approaches, will be critical for clarifying how intrinsic 616 segregation relates to cognition across brain states. 617
