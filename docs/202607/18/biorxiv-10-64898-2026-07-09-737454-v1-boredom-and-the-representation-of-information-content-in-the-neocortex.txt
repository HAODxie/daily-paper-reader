# Summary 24 

Boredom  – a pervasive  mental  state  – promotes  the pursuit of  novel information  by assigning 25 

negative value to monotonous conditions . Yet , how the brain extracts  and represents the  information 26 

content of ongoing sensory experience remains poorly understood.  Here, we combine behavioral assays, 27 

neurophysiological recordings and computational modeling across humans and mice to investigate how 28 

sensory information shapes boredom -related behavior.  In a cross -species choice task, both humans and 29 

mice robustly avoid  monotonous  sources of  sensory  stimulation . We formalize perceived monotony using 30 

empirical entropy  as a measure of information content  and show that  monotony  avoidance scales  directly 31 

with low entropy  and in humans correlates with boredom experience . Human e lectroencephalography 32 

and mesoscopic calcium imaging  in mice  reveal that  the recruitment of  neocortical  activity  track s stimulus 33 

entropy . Two -photon  calcium imaging  in the auditory cortex of mice  further uncovers a stimulus -invariant 34 

population code for entropy, supported by neurons tuned to information content.  A recurrent network 35 

model reproduced this code through  an interplay of  afferent depression and recurrent facilitation. 36 

Together,  we demonstrate  how  the information content of sensory experience is  represented  in cortical 37 

population  activity , providing  a basis for boredom -related  avoidance behavior.  Thus, our findings  link 38 

synaptic and neuronal dynamics to boredom , acting as a safeguard  mechanism  to ensure high information 39 

input to the brain. 40 

# Introduction 41 

Boredom is a  ubiquitous  human experience , intimately familiar to many of us,  aris ing  across 42 

diverse everyday situations  such as in school or workplace environments , particularly under conditions of 43 

monotony or insufficient stimulation 1-5. Boredom  is commonly defined as an aversive state of non -optimal 44 

mental  engagement in which the demands of a situation fail to match an individual’s cognitive capacities 6-

45

. Beyond its subjective unpleasantness, boredom has substantial clinical relevance , where  elevated and 46 

chronic experiences of boredom are associated with a range of psychiatric conditions, including 47 

depression, substance use disorders, and attention -deficit disorders 9-15 .48 

Despite its broad psychosocial relevance , scientific research on boredom is  comparatively  young. 49 

Diverse efforts have been made to quantify boredom  in humans,  primarily utilizing self -report  instruments 50 

such as the Multidimensional State Boredom Scale 16  (MSBS), which captures momentary fluctuations in 51 

experienced boredom 17,18 . Experimentally, boredom is typically induced through prolonged exposure to 52 

sparse  or  repetitive sensory stimulation 19 -22 , for example by presenting  humans with  intuitively boring 53 

videos of mundane activities such as hanging laundry 23 . Although such paradigms possess strong face 54

While at a  phenomenological level, boredom is characterized by negative valence, dissatisfying 57 

arousal , and a subjective slowing of time 6,24,25 , it is also thought to  serve an  important  adaptive 58 

function 18,26 -28 : By signaling odd situations  such as  monotonous  environments , boredom  generates a 59 

strong urge to disengage from the current situation and seek alternative sources of stimulation or 60 

activity 26,29,30 . As such boredom is believed to play an important role to safeguard constant information 61 

input to the brain, which is crucial for continuous learning, behavioral flexibility 31 -37 , and prevents 62 

individuals from  stagnancy in monotony 38,39 . This perspective aligns with broader “info rma vore” theories 63 

of cognition 31,40 , propos ing  that organisms are intrinsically motivated to seek information and maintain an 64 

optimal level of informational engagement 34,41,42 .65 

The idea that the nervous system is sensitive to information content is supported by several lines 66 

of evidence. Neuronal responses throughout sensory systems adapt  and decline  during repetitive 67 

stimulation, whereas rare or unexpected stimuli evoke enhanced neural responses 43 -50 . Moreover, 68 

information -theoretic approaches are widely used in neuroscience to quantify how much information 69 

about external stimuli is encoded in neuronal activity patterns 51 -53 . However, these approaches typically 70 

characterize information from the perspective of an external observer and do not explain how the brain 71 

itself might construct an internal representation of  the information content  of  its sensory input  as such . A72 

translational framework that operationalizes boredom in terms of quantifiable information content across 73 

species , thus enabling experimental access to the underlying neural computations  would therefore 74 

provide a critical step toward s a better understanding of this intriguing mental phenomenon . While 75 

boredom -like behaviors have been described in several  non -human  species 54 -60 , existing paradigms do not 76 

allow precise control or formal quantification of the information content available to the animal. 77 

We here test the hypothesis that  boredom reflects  a mechanism to sense  insufficient information 78 

and ask : How does the brain  abstract, measure and  represent the information content of ongoing sensory 79 

experience? 80 

To this end,  we leverage a  recently developed behavioral choice paradigm reliably induc ing 81 

boredom  in humans  through repeated sensory stimulation , while providing a  simultaneous  behavioral 82 

readout of boredom -driven avoidance behavior 22 . Taking advantage of the non -verbal nature of the task ,83 

we  extend this paradigm and  measure boredom  in both human s and mice as  behavior al avoidance  of 84 

sensory  sources  with low  information content . Using  information theory, we quanti fy experienced 85 

information content  as empirical  Shannon  entropy 61  and investigate the  represen tation of entropy  in 86 

cortical activity , using EEG recordings  in humans  and mesoscopic calcium imaging in mice.  In mice, we 87 

further examine the neural representation of information at the level of single neurons and neuronal 88

# Results 95 

Avoidance of low  sensory  information content reflects  experienced  boredom in humans 96 

To investigate how sensory information content shapes boredom in humans , we adapted  a97 

recently developed  behavioral paradigm 22 . A cohort of 92  young adult, healthy subjects (see 98 

Supplementary Table 1 for demographic details)  participa ted in a  choice task , requir ing them  to choose 99 

between  two buttons on a screen . Unbeknownst  to the subject s, the two buttons represented  either  a100 

monotonous  alternative, leading to the presentation of a single repeated sound stimulus,  or,  a variable 101 

alternative, leading to the presentation of varying sound stimul i randomly drawn from a larger stimulus 102 

library (Figure 1a, Methods).  Sound stimuli consisted of recordings of single spoken words of neutral 103 

meaning (Methods).  As we were interested in the impact of the information content on choice behavior ,104 

rather than sound identity , we randomized  the  assignment of stimuli to the two alternatives across 105 

individuals and tasks. Each  task ended after a defined number of trials and was free from  reinforcement ,106 

apart from the auditory stimulation per se. In addition to the task comparing monotonous versus variable 107 

stimulation, participants also completed control tasks with both alternatives  being monotonous or 108 

variable, thus  providing equivalent stimulation.  We used i ntermittent  psychometric sentiment  ratings on 109 

visual analog scales to assess  the development of  individually perceived boredom,  affect and  arousal 110 

during the task. 111 

Over the course of approximately 100 trials , participants  developed a  bias  avoid ing  the 112 

monotonous alternative over the variable alternative (Figure 1b ,c ; for full statistical details see  separate 113 

Statistical Information  file ). In task conditions with equivalent stimulation, subjects did not show a  bias  to 114 

either side . The procedures led to an overall increase of boredom scores on the Multidimensional State 115 

Boredom Scale (MSBS), a standardized psychometric questionnaire  widely used in boredom research 116 

(Figure 1d, Supplementary  Figure 1a) . Corroborating  previous observations 22 , the extent of monotony 117 

avoidance in individual participants was positively correlated with the  mean boredom ratings during the 118 

task, indicating a reflection of perceived boredom in choice behavior  (Figure 1 e).  Boredom  ratings 119 

gradually built  up over the course of the task, rising in parallel to the increasing choice bias of avoiding the 120

We next quantified  the  empirical  Shannon  entropy 61  within  the sequence of  sound stimuli sampled 147 

from  each  alternative until a given trial , relative to all sampled stimuli so far , providing a measure of 148 

experienced  information content (Figure 1h, see Supplementary Figure 1e and Methods for details).  As 149 

expected, e ntropy  sampled  from the variable alternative gradually built  up over the trials in the task, 150 

whereas it remained at a low level for the monotonous alternative (Figure 1i).  To assess  how  the 151 

difference s in  experienced entropy  between both alternatives  up to a given trial  would affect the  future 152 

choice of participants, we binned the data according to the  entropy  difference  in each trial , and  analyzed 153 

the  upcoming  choice probability  for the alternative providing higher entropy (Figure 1j). Interestingly, we 154 

found that higher entropy sampled from one alternative predicted a higher probability of sampling this 155 

alternative again, suggesting that preference behavior in the task was driven by avoidance of low 156 

information content. To investigate this aspect in greater detail, we used logistic regression to model 157 

individual choice behavior on a single -trial basis and compare d the contribution of sampled entropy to 158 

other choice factors. Specifically, we  used  three  regressors: (i) The sampled  entropy  difference , providing 159 

a measure of how sensitively a subject adjusts its choice to experienced information content, (ii) an 160 

idiosyncratic choice bias , describing an inherent, stimulus -independent p reference  towards a given 161 

side 64,65 , and (iii)  the  previous choice , describing  behavioral inertia to  repeat  the previous choice 162 

independent of the sample d stimuli 66 -68  (Figure 1k, Methods).  The model captured a significant fraction of 163 

variance in decision behavior, predicting  approximately 70% of all choices correctly. Interestingly, 164 

comparing the contribution of all regressors  (Methods) , we found  that entropy  difference  showed the 165 

highest impact on choice behavior  (Figure 1l) .166 

Together, these observations indicate that boredom in humans is directly driven by low sensory 167 

information content, triggering avoidance  of  sensory  sources  associated with  low information  content .168

Information content  is reflected by  increased  cortical  recruitment  and arousal 233 

Our assays in humans and mice  revealed an essential role  of experienced  entropy  in explaining 234 

boredom -related  avoidance behavior , suggesting  that  mice and  humans  share  an internal representation 235 

of information content to guide affective  and behavioral  responses.  We next sought to investigate in how 236 

far information content is reflected in neurophysiological signatures observable in both  species .237 

To this end, we combine d assessments of  overall  cortical activity  while presenting individuals with 238 

sensory  stimuli that systematically var ied  in information content . In addition, we used pupil size 239 

measurements to obtain a physiological readout of arousal 71,72 , motivated by the  observed linkage 240 

between experienced boredom and low degrees of arousal in humans 25 . Various  previous studies have 241 

demonstrated a robust  modulation of sound -evoked responses in the auditory cortex by  the statistics and 242 

predictability of sound events, classically described under terms such as “adaptation”, “repetition 243 

suppression”, or “mismatch negativity” 44,45,47 -49,73 -75 . While  these studies often use categorical comparisons 244 

of responses for expected standard stimuli versus unexpected deviant stimuli, the neural signatures of 245 

different degrees of expectedness on a continuum remains less understood. To address this aspect,  we 246 

used a library of ten neutral, but distinct ly represented  sound stimuli as a basis to create a set of sequence 247 

stimuli systematically varying in information content  (see Methods) . We  construct ed  20 different 248 

sequence  patterns  with varying degrees of repetitiveness, each comprising 10 sound stimuli presented at 249 

inter -sound intervals of 1 second . This  design  allowed us to  quantitatively  describ e the  information 250 

content  conveyed by the  statistical  structure  of the sequence  up to a given stimulus  by computing entrop y251 

(Figure 3a ). While various types of entropy have been proposed, capturing different aspects of stimulus 252 

novelty and surprise 76 , we quantif ied  the basic Shannon entropy congruent to the information measure 253 

that explained well the behavioral choice bias (Supplementary Figure 3, Methods).  In order to control for 254 

response components that are driven by the physical differences of the stimuli, we constructed different 255 

versions of a particular sequence pattern  by  varying the composition of sound stimuli. Sound sequences 256 

for human and mouse experiment s had the same structure, but were constructed from different sound 257 

libraries matching the respective hearing range , and contained 3  versions  (human , i.e. 60 sequences total) 258 

or 5 versions  (mouse, i.e. 100 versions total)  of each sequence pattern  (see Supplementary Figure 4) .259 

To validate our stimulus set with respect to evoked sentiment,  we collected psychometric ratings 260 

from 92 human participants (same sample as for the behavioral experiment  in Figure 1, s ee Methods), 261 

measuring the perceived levels of  boredom , information content , affect  and  arousal  for each sequence 262 

(Figure 3b).  When  compar ing  mean sentiment for the quartile  of sequences  with highest average entropy ,263 

against the quartile with lowest average entropy , we found that the high -entropy sequences evoked 264 

significantly less boredom,  higher  degrees of  perceived information and higher arousal than the low -265 

entropy sequences, d espite the  overall  highly arbitrary character of  the  sound sequences  (Figure 3c). 266

The representation of information content at  the  level of  neuronal population s395 

Going beyond the tuning of individual neurons  to entropy, we  next  investigated  how information 396 

content is encoded  in the structure  of population activity in the auditory cortex. To this end, we  applied a 397 

representational similarity analysis (RSA), which provides an unsupervised estimation of the relational 398 

structure of the population responses evoked by the  sound  stimuli  varying in entropy 92 -95 . The trial -to -trial 399 

reliability of sound -evoked response patterns was highest in the bin with low entropy, and remained at 400 

lower, comparable levels in bins with higher entropy (Supplementary Figure 7a). We  computed the 401 

average population response vectors for each sound and entropy bin , respectively , and  generated  a402 

representational similarity matrix  by computing the pairwise Pearson correlations (Methods).  We then 403 

applied a principal component analysis (PCA) to  the full  representational similarity matrix in order to 404 

visualize the representational structure of sounds in dimension -reduced space 90,91,95 . For each of the five 405 

entropy bins, we displayed the representational similarity matrices and the respective projections in the 406 

shared PCA coordinate system  (Figure 4i) . Here, we made three main observations: First , the relati ve 407 

distances  between  the  activity patterns  evoked by the ten different sounds  remained largely  preserved in 408 

each of the five entropy bins. This was reflected by significant positive correlation s when comparing the 409 

off -diagonal entries of the five representational similarity matrices  (Figure 4j). Second, the  mean  pairwise 410 

similarit ies in a given similarity matrix  increased with entropy (Figure 4k), indicating that sound -evoked 411 

response patterns with high information content were generally more similar. Third,  sound 412 

representations displayed  a systematic  shift with growing entropy along a common axis, closely aligned to 413 

the first principal component (see  trajectories in  Figure 4i ).414 

To  investigate  in how far the direction of shift  with entropy in representational space was shared 415 

or independent for each sound , we estimated the  axes  that best captured  entropy for each stimulus 416 

individually 91  and compared their orientation s (Methods) . We found a significant alignment of 417 

representational entropy axes across sounds, indicating a stimulus -invariant representation of information 418 

content  in cortical activity  (Supplementary Figure 7 b, c).  Given various reports of strong adaptation of 419 

responses in the auditory cortex 44,45,96  and our previous finding that entropy is reflected in the level of 420 

overall cortical activation, we wondered in how far the representational axis capturing entropy essentially 421 

reflects stimulus adaptation.  We therefore estimated the overall representational axis for entropy in our 422 

dataset by considering all sound responses , as well as  the representational axis that best explained 423 

differences in response magnitude  (Figure 4l , Methods ). We  found that  despite an overall positive 424 

correlation  between both axes , they were statistically distinct and  entropy -encoding was not explained by 425 

mere differences in mean  responsiveness (observed data: RP=0.464 , 95% -CI = [0.009 0.560] ;426 

Supplementary Figure 7d,e).  This indicates entropy is not only encoded by a modulation of overall activity 427 

but additionally in the structure of evoked activity patterns. 428

# Discussion 540 

In our study, we  investigated the behavioral and neuronal underpinnings of boredom in humans 541 

and mice. 542 

First, we found that humans and mice react with avoidance behavior to monotonous sensory 543 

stimulation and that empirical entropy  as a measure of information content  predicts the degree of 544 

avoidance. 545 

Avoidance behavior correlate d with reported boredom in humans and  was  associated with low 546 

arousal and negative affect building up throughout the experiment. This is in contrast  to  curiosity, which 547 

is typically linked to higher arousal  and positive affect 18,34,102 , and  corroborat es  that the choice bias  in our 548 

paradigm  primarily reflects boredom  rather than other drivers of exploratory behavior that could in 549 

principle also explain avoidance of monotony . The correlation of avoidance behavior with experienced 550 

entropy, indicates that boredom arises as an  aversive cognitive  response to insufficient information in 551 

sensory inputs. In our experiments, we  focused on  information content conveyed by the structure of the 552 

stimulus sequences  while keeping other aspects of sensory information, such as the specific properties of 553 

individual sounds and additional non -auditory sensory inputs, at a low and stable level by prior habituation 554 

to the general setup. Furthermore, familiarization with the individual sounds  of  the sequences as well as 555 

randomization in the task structure allowed to disentangle specific responses to the information provided 556 

by the sequence structure quantified as Shannon entropy 61 . While more specialized entropy metrics may 557 

capture  more detailed  aspects of novelty and surprise 76 , basic  Shannon entropy significantly predicted 558 

both boredom -related avoidance and neural responses, underscoring its value as a general measure of 559 

experienced information.  It is important to note that the efficient transmission of information depends 560 

not only on the complexity of a transmitted sensory signal, but also on the capacity of a receiver to decode 561 

and interpret the signal 10,33,103 . We assume that basic processing and decoding of the statistical complexity 562 

in a series of inherently neutral sounds may be conserved in humans and mice, showing a low dependence 563 

on species -specific decoding abilities. Apart from observing a similar avoidance of sensory sources with 564 

low entropy in humans and mice, we found that neurophysiological measures of cortical recruitment and 565 

arousal also showed a similar correlation with information content. This further corroborates that shared 566 

neuronal mechanisms in both organisms allow to infer the complexity of its current input in order to 567 

adequately direct attention and behavior 32,34 . Our results align with previous reports in humans 568 

demonstrating a modulation of overall activity in the auditory cortex with the complexity of sound 569 

sequences 104  together with heightened levels of arousal being important for efficient neural processing of 570 

incoming stimuli 82,105 . Furthermore,  the  finding that avoidance behavior in mice is driven by the 571 

experienced information content  aligns with  previous reports of boredom -like behaviors in various non -572

Second, we used  calcium  imaging of neuronal populations with single cell resolution in response 578 

to sequences with varying entropy to assess how an abstract representation of information content may 579 

emerge. 580 

In line with the neuronal tuning of individual neurons to entropy observed in our study, previous 581 

reports have found related effects in other brain areas, identifying single neurons in the parietal cortex of 582 

monkeys 107 , or in the orbitofrontal cortex of mice 108  that respond to the active sampling of information. 583 

Neurons showed a wide range of mixed tuning profiles to entropy and stimulus identity extending to 584 

neurons with predominant tuning to entropy. Our analyses focused on the encoding of information 585 

content emerging from the population of neurons with diverse tuning properties 109 . Observations from 586 

various brain regions support the idea that representations are formed in a lower -dimensional subspace 587 

of neuronal activity often described as a manifold 110 -112 . Analyses such as representational similarity 588 

analysis (RSA) describe the structure of activity patterns within this manifold, based on the idea that the 589 

relatedness of different stimuli is encoded as a higher or lower degree of similarity between the 590 

corresponding activity patterns 92,95,113,114 . Classical studies focused on the encoding of stimulus identity 591 

and their relatedness based on perceptual or semantic properties 115 -117 . Interestingly, also other, more 592 

abstract variables are mapped on the representational structure, such as sensory certainty 118  or task 593 

contexts 119 -121 . The identification of latent variables, e.g. in form of a representational axis capturing the 594 

response variability encoding a specific feature, is a common approach to make an abstract neuronal 595 

subspace functionally interpretable in form of a representational map 91,122 . Here, we identify a major 596 

representational axis, largely invariant from stimulus identity, that aligns with empirical entropy and 597 

enables the decoding of the information content from sampled sound stimuli. Such a representation 598 

allows to infer statistical variability in the structure of incoming sensory stimuli 123,124 , hence providing a 599 

basis for boredom -related behaviors.  Furthermore, we found that encoding of stimulus identity along the 600 

representational axis systematically decreased with entropy, which may be related to the phenomenon of 601 

representational sharpening, describing more distinct activity patterns and enhanced discriminability for 602 

expected stimuli as compared to larger, but more stereotypical activity patterns for unexpected stimuli 125 -

Third,  using theoretical modeling, we  identify  complementary plasticity  mechanisms  at afferent 607 

and recurrent connections as a minimal mechanism  enabl ing  the formation of a representation of entropy 608

This result builds on extensive work on stimulus -specific adaptation, whereby repeated sensory 610 

inputs progressively evoke weaker responses while responses to rare or deviant stimuli are preserved 47,100 .611 

Such adaptation has been linked to input -specific synaptic depression and can emerge from depression of 612 

feedforward inputs, recurrent connections, or both 101,131,132 . Our model extends this framework by 613 

showing that depression alone explains only one hallmark of the  experimental  data , where  afferent 614 

depression generated adaptation to repeated inputs but did not reproduce the  complexity of the  observed 615 

population code for entropy.  A complementary role emerged for facilitation within the recurrent circuit. 616 

Recurrent connectivity provides a natural substrate through which current population activity can depend 617 

on recent sensory history, and activity -dependent changes in recurrent efficacy can transiently retain and 618 

transform information across sequential inputs 97,98,133 . In our model, recurrent facilitation amplified  the 619 

distributed activity  of neurons with mixed tuning that are effectively  recruited by variable stimulus 620 

sequences,  thus  causing population responses to shift coherently along a stimulus -invariant entropy axis 621 

while largely preserving the stimulus -specific organization. Facilitation alone, however, failed to reproduce 622 

adaptation and instead caused responses to repeated inputs to increase. Only the combination of afferent 623 

depression and recurrent facilitation reproduced both principal signatures observed experimentally ,624 

adaptation to repeated stimulation and a population representation of entropy.  This  division of labor  in 625 

our model  suggests that cortical representations of sensory statistics may emerge from interactions 626 

between plasticity mechanisms operating at distinct circuit locations. Afferent depression provides a local 627 

memory of recently encountered inputs, reducing the impact  of  repetitions, whereas recurrent facilitation 628 

integrates activity across changing inputs and converts their recent statistical structure into a distributed 629 

population signal.  This framework complements work showing that the brain tracks sensory statistics at 630 

multiple levels, from item frequency and repetition to transition probabilities and higher -order sequence 631 

structure 134,135  by revealing a stimulus -invariant transformation of population geometry with information 632 

content.  Thus, complementary plasticity of sensory inputs and recurrent network interactions may provide 633 

a parsimonious circuit mechanism for tracking the statistical structure of ongoing sensory experience while 634 

preserving information about stimulus identity.  In the future, it will be of interest to reveal the 635 

implementation of this mechanism in cortical circuits, possibly involving dynamics of synapses at shorter 636 

and longer time -scales and the emergence of local circuit motifs being disinhibited by increasing entropy 637 

in sensory inputs. 638

While m uch of research on boredom in recent years was focused on phenomenological 641 

descriptions of  its experience , it is less well understood what specific factors are inducing this aversive 642 

state. Here, we provide evidence that  it is not specific physical features of sensory inputs, but rather  the 643

appears intuitively plausible, the application of information theoretic measures to describe the 645 

information content of sensory inputs now provides a  translational  framework for future studies to 646 

quantitatively describe and control the factors inducing boredom. We leveraged the mouse auditory 647 

cortex as a model to identify network mechanisms that implement the sensing and representation of 648 

information content, thereby providing a basis for future studies to elucidate how such a representation 649 

may be read out and linked to affect. Likely, multiple representations of information content for various 650

Preprocessing of human  imaging  data  and quantification of evoked responses 855 

Electroencephalography  data 856 

Preprocessing was performed using custom MATLAB R2023a scripts utilizing EEGLAB 160  and 857 

FieldTrip 161  functions. Continuous recordings were downsampled to 250 Hz to optimize computational 858 

efficiency while maintaining temporal resolution for ERP analyses. Line noise (50 Hz and its harmonics) 859 

was removed using Zapline -plus 162 , an adaptive extension of the Zapline algorithm 163  for automated, 860 

parameter -free spatial filtering of power -line artifacts. To improve independent component analysis (ICA) 861 

stability, the data were high -pass filtered at 1 Hz using a two -pass zero -phase FIR filter (pop_eegfiltnew, 862 

order 1650). Data were re -referenced to a full -rank common average reference 164  to preserve rank for 863 

subsequent spatial decompositions. EEG data were subsequently processed using the Generalized 864 

Eigenvalue De -Artifacting Instrument (GEDAI), a model -based denoising approach that uses a theoretical 865 

leadfield matrix to constrain signal decomposition 165 . Assuming  that  neural activity is consistent with a 866 

biophysically plausible forward model of head volume conduction, GEDAI applies generalized eigenvalue 867 

decomposition to isolate components that fit the model and attenuate non -neural activity. This method 868 

was applied prior to ICA to maximize decomposition stability. ICA was performed on the continuous data 869 

using the extended Infomax (runica) algorithm, with PCA dimensionality reduction set equal to the 870 

effective rank of the data 164 . Independent components were classified using ICLabel 166  and rejected if 871 

artifact probabilities met conservative thresholds (muscle, heart, or channel noise ≥ 0.97; eye ≥ 0.90), or 872 

if the probability of brain activity was ≤ 0.01  (number of rejected components per participant: mean=3.75 873 

± SD=1.30) . All flagged components were visually inspected to verify their classification as non -874 

physiological . Following ICA correction, we visually inspected channel -wise power spectral density (4s 875 

segments, 80% overlap, Hanning window) plots for each participant to confirm signal quality. Finally, 876 

continuous data were segmented into epochs from −250 to  1000 ms relative to stimulus onset  and 877 

smoothed  with a moving mean filter over 5 samples (i.e. 20ms) . Because artifact attenuation was 878 

performed at the continuous -data level using GEDAI and ICA, no additional epoch -level rejection 879 

procedures were applied. 880
