# Encoding and Retrieval in Parallel: ERP Correlates of Continuous Recognition Memory for Natural Scenes 

Elena Cesnaite 1,2 and Niko A. Busch ∗1,2 

> 1

Institute of Psychology, University of Münster, Germany

Abstract 1

Human long-term memory for visual scenes is remarkably robust, yet the neural mechanisms 2

supporting memory encoding and retrieval remain poorly understood when both processes 3

must operate at the same time. For instance, this might happen when we encounter a 4

familiar place while simultaneously forming new memories of this encounter. We investigated 5

electrophysiological correlates of visual recognition memory using a continuous recognition 6

task (CRT), in which participants judged a continuous stream of scene photographs as 7

previously seen or new, such that encoding and retrieval occurred in parallel on every 8

trial. To make recognition particularly demanding, stimuli were drawn from only four scene 9

categories. Thirty-one participants performed the task while EEG was recorded, and we 10 

analyzed canonical ERP markers of retrieval (mid-frontal FN400, 300-550 ms; late parietal 11 

effect, LPE, 550-800 ms) and encoding (subsequent memory effect, SME) as a function of 12 

stimulus repetition and lag between consecutive presentations. FN400 showed robust old/new 13 

effects for both repetitions, whereas LPE differences emerged only at the second repetition. 14 

While FN400 amplitude was insensitive to lag, LPE amplitude decreased systematically with 15 

increasing lag, mirroring the behavioral pattern of declining accuracy and slower responses. 16 

A significant SME emerged selectively for images subsequently recognized on both repetitions, 17 

indicating that the SME in continuous recognition is specific for the most robustly encoded 18 

items and reflects the strength of encoding. Together, these findings show that canonical 19 

ERP markers of recognition memory are preserved even when encoding and retrieval operate 20 

concurrently, but their expression depends on how often and how recently an item has 21 

previously been encoded – parameters that can be flexibly manipulated within the CRT. 22 

This demonstrates that the CRT is sensitive to fine-grained temporal dynamics of memory 23 

formation and retrieval that could be missed under standard single-repetition designs. 24

## 1 Introduction 25 

Human memory for visual scenes is strikingly robust. Pioneering studies have shown that people 26 

can remember thousands of images with high accuracy, even after long delays (Brady et al., 27 

2008; Shepard, 1967). Notably, this visual memory is not only long-lasting but also resilient to 28 

interference, even when individuals are exposed to many exemplars from the same scene category 29 

(Konkle et al., 2010). 30 

To better understand the cognitive and neural mechanisms that support such high-capacity visual 31 

memory, recognition memory has been extensively investigated using event-related potentials 32 

(ERPs). This research has enabled the identification of several well-characterized ERP components 33 

associated with memory processes (Voss & Paller, 2017; Wilding & Ranganath, 2011). During 34 

encoding, the subsequent memory effect (SME) reflects differential neural activity for items that 35 

are later remembered compared to those that are forgotten. SME has been found in recall and 36 

recognition tasks, and presumably reflects several cognitive processes that lead to more efficient 37 

encoding, such as detailed perceptual analysis, rote rehearsal, semantic elaboration, or mental 38 

imagery (Voss & Paller, 2017). 39 

During retrieval, early and late ERP old/new effects are typically observed. The early frontal 40 

old/new effect, often referred to as the FN400 (approximately 300–500 ms post-stimulus), is 41 

characterized by more negative amplitudes for new relative to old items at frontal electrodes. 42 

Within dual-process theories of recognition memory, this effect has been linked to familiarity-43 

based recognition, reflecting a graded sense that an item has been encountered before without 44 

retrieval of contextual details. A later parietal old/new effect (often referred to as LPE), typically 45 

emerging between 500 and 800 ms, has been more strongly associated with recollection, that is, the 46 

retrieval of episodic details about the prior encounter. This late parietal positivity is commonly 47 

interpreted as indexing the conscious reinstatement of contextual information accompanying 48 

successful memory retrieval (Curran, 2000; Curran & Cleary, 2003; Liesefeld et al., 2016). 49 

Most ERP studies of recognition memory have used an experimental paradigm with two clearly 50 

separated stages: an initial encoding phase, during which a set of novel stimuli is presented for 51 

later memory, followed by a retrieval phase, during which participants indicate if they recognize 52 

an item as old (previously seen) or new. However, such a strict temporal separation between 53 

encoding and retrieval phases does not align well with everyday memory, where both processes 54 

often occur simultaneously. For example, during a daily commute, encountering a familiar place 55 

may trigger a recognition response (“I’ve been here before”) while simultaneously updating or 56 

strengthening the already-formed memory trace of that place. Similarly, a novel stimulus such as 57 

a newly built construction site may be categorized as new while it is being encoded for future 58 

reference. With repeated exposure, this previously novel location becomes increasingly familiar. 59 

These examples highlight that encoding and retrieval are not always distinct phases but can 60 

occur concurrently. 61

In this paradigm, stimuli are presented in a continuous sequence, and participants indicate for 64 

each item whether it is new or has been seen before. Crucially, this task structure requires 65 

participants to engage in both encoding and retrieval on every trial, rather than being in a 66 

dedicated "encoding mode" or "retrieval mode" as in conventional two-phase paradigms. The CRT 67 

affords several important methodological and theoretical advantages. First, because participants 68 

must both retrieve old items and encode new ones throughout the task, the CRT more closely 69 

mirrors the dynamics of natural memory processing. Second, the strength of memory traces 70 

can be systematically manipulated via the lag between repetitions, i.e. by varying the number 71 

of intervening items between the first and second presentation. This manipulation affects both 72 

temporal delay and the degree of interference from intervening stimuli. Third, because there is no 73 

dedicated retention interval between encoding and test, there is minimal opportunity for rehearsal 74 

or strategic reorganization of the material (Shepard & Teghtsoonian, 1961). Importantly, varying 75 

lag also enables a distinction between recognition based on short-term or working memory (e.g., 76 

at short lags within a few seconds) and long-term memory (at longer lags), providing a unique 77 

window into the temporal dynamics of memory consolidation and forgetting (Friedman, 1990). 78 

Finally, the CRT offers a pragmatic advantage in terms of experimental design flexibility. Unlike 79 

standard paradigms that depend on the completion of both an encoding and a subsequent 80 

retrieval phase, the CRT allows for continuous data collection. Old and new items are interleaved 81 

throughout the session, enabling the analysis of both encoding and retrieval processes at any 82 

point in the stream. This flexibility makes the CRT especially attractive for studies with variable 83 

session lengths, such as online experiments investigating visual long-term memory (sometimes 84 

referred to as a “memory game”; Bylinskii et al., 2015; Goetschalckx & Wagemans, 2019; Kramer 85 

et al., 2023). 86 

Compared to the large body of research using paradigms with separate encoding and retrieval 87 

phases, relatively few ERP studies have employed the CRT to investigate the neural correlates 88 

of visual recognition memory. In a seminal study, Friedman, 1990 presented line drawings of 89 

everyday objects within a CRT paradigm and reported ERP old/new effects that resembled the 90 

canonical early frontal and late parietal components typically observed in standard recognition 91 

tasks. Interestingly, the magnitude of these ERP effects was unaffected by the lag between an 92 

item’s initial and repeated presentation. This insensitivity to lag could suggest that the observed 93 

old/new effects reflect the outcome of a binary recognition decision – classifying an item as either 94 

old or new – rather than a graded signal of memory strength. Alternatively, the stimulus set, 95 

comprising only a single exemplar per object category, may have been highly memorable, and 96 

the maximum lag of 32 intervening items may not have been sufficient to induce substantial 97 

forgetting (Nickerson, 1965). This interpretation is supported by the observation that behavioral 98 

performance remained stable even at longer lags. 99 

Moreover, Friedman, 1990 found no evidence for subsequent memory effects (SMEs). The 100 

absence of SME in a CRT task may imply that SMEs only emerge when cognitive resources can 101

The present study aimed to investigate the neural correlates of visual recognition memory 109 

under continuous, ecologically valid conditions. Specifically, we examined whether conventional 110 

ERP memory effects, such as the SME during encoding and old/new effects during retrieval, can 111 

be observed in a CRT using realistic natural scene photographs, where discrimination between 112 

old and new items is intentionally made challenging by presenting numerous exemplars from 113 

the same scene categories. In addition, we assessed how SME encoding effects evolve across 114 

repeated presentations of the same item, thereby probing the dynamics of encoding processes 115 

under ongoing memory demands. Finally, we investigated how the ERP old/new effect varies as 116 

a function of five lag conditions between stimulus repetitions to better understand how memory 117 

strength and retrieval processes unfold over time in the context of continuous recognition. 118 

## 2 Methods 119 

2.1 Participants 120 

Data were collected from 33 participants (22 female; 4 left-handed, mean age 27 years, SD 5.5 121 

years) with normal or corrected-to-normal vision 1. All participants provided written informed 122 

consent, and received course credit or monetary compensation. The study was approved by 123 

the ethics committee of the faculty of psychology and sports sciences, University of Münster 124 

(approval no. 2016-22-N). 125 

2.2 Stimuli and apparatus 126 

The experiment was written in MATLAB (The Mathworks, Natick, MA, USA) using the 127 

Psychophysics Toolbox (Brainard, 1997). Stimuli were presented on a calibrated LCD monitor 128 

(VIEWPixx/EEG) with 1920 x 1080 pixels resolution and 120 Hz refresh rate, placed at a distance 129 

of 86 cm from the participants’ eyes. Head position was stabilized using a chin rest. 130 

The stimuli were drawn from a pool of 1200 images from 4 different scene categories (forests, 131 

highways, beaches, cities) gathered using Google Image Search. All images were 10 x 7.5 ◦ in 132 

size and presented in grayscale on black background. A gray fixation cross was displayed at the 133

2.3 Procedure 137 

In this CRT (Friedman, 1990; Shepard & Teghtsoonian, 1961), participants were presented with 138 

a stream of images (Figure 1), some of which were repeated, and they decided for each image 139 

whether it had been presented before (old) or whether it was the first time that image was 140 

presented (new). Participants were instructed to respond as fast as possible by lifting their finger 141 

from one of two response keys (left/right CTRL key), indicating whether the image was old or 142 

new. The assignment of key (left/right) and response category (old/new) was counterbalanced 143 

across participants. Images were presented for 500 ms each, followed by an empty screen until 144 

the participant had responded. Feedback was provided by turning the fixation cross green or 145 

red for correct and incorrect responses, respectively, for a duration of 200 ms. Once both keys 146 

were held down again, the next image followed after a variable inter-trial interval, which was 147 

drawn from a truncated exponential distribution with a minimum of 1000 ms and a maximum of 148 

3000 ms. 149 

For each participant, a sample of 600 images was selected from the image pool; 300 were 150 

presented only once and 300 images were presented three times (first presentation as a new image, 151 

second and third presentations as old), resulting in a total of 1200 trials per participant, half of 152 

which featured a new image. Repeated presentations occurred after a lag of 10, 16, 24, 38, or 60 153 

intervening trials. 154 

2.4 EEG Recording 155 

Data were recorded at two laboratory locations in sound-attenuated rooms. One laboratory 156 

location (17 participants) was equipped with a Faraday cage, whereas the other location (16 157 

participants) had no electrical shielding. In both labs, EEG was recorded with a BioSemi Active-158 

Two amplifier system from 64 Ag/AgCl electrodes arranged according to the international 10-10 159 

system and two additional mastoid electrodes. The horizontal and vertical electro-oculograms 160 

were recorded from additional electrodes at the lateral canthi of both eyes and below the eyes, 161 

respectively. Two additional electrodes located adjacent to electrode POz served as reference 162 

and ground. Signals were sampled at 1024 Hz with a 200 Hz low-pass filter. 163

FIR filter, filter order 282, 10 Hz transition bandwidth), and high-pass filtered at 0.1 Hz 166

FN400 and LPE mean amplitudes in the early frontal and late parietal regions of interest were 207

ERP ∼ lag + (1 | participant) ERP ∼ presentation + (1 | participant) 

The SME at frontal and parietal areas was analyzed using a linear mixed-effects model. ERP 212 

amplitudes at the first presentation were predicted by subsequent memory performance, defined 213 

as the number of correct recognitions across the second and third presentations (0, 1, or 2). The 214 

model included subsequent memory as a fixed effect and a random intercept for participant and 215 

was specified as: 216

## 4 Discussion 263 

How do the neurocognitive mechanisms of memory encoding and retrieval operate when both 264 

processes must occur in parallel, rather than in dedicated sequential phases? The continuous 265 

recognition task (CRT) offers a unique window into this question, as participants must simul-266 

taneously encode novel stimuli and retrieve previously seen ones on every trial (Shepard & 267 

Teghtsoonian, 1961). Only few ERP studies have employed this task (see Ellmore et al., 2022), 268 

and the one study most directly comparable to ours – Friedman (1990), using line drawings of 269 

objects – reported two unexpected null results: no subsequent memory effect (SME) and no 270 

modulation of old/new ERP effects by lag (i.e., the number of images in between consecutive 271 

presentations). These findings raised the possibility that the neurocognitive processes supporting 272 

encoding and retrieval are fundamentally altered when both demands compete concurrently for 273 

cognitive resources. The present study tested this hypothesis using naturalistic scene photographs, 274 

a wider range of lags, and multiple image repetitions, enabling a more sensitive assessment of 275 

encoding strength. 276 

Old/new effects 277 

The ERP old/new effect is a widely studied phenomenon in recognition memory research 278 

characterized by more positive-going waveforms for old compared to new items. This effect is not 279 

a single unitary component but is comprised of several spatio-temporally distinct subcomponents 280 

that correlate with different mnemonic processes (Friedman & Johnson Jr., 2000; Voss & Paller, 281 

2017). 282 

We observed robust old/new ERP effects across stimulus repetitions: both FN400 (300–550 ms, 283 

frontal) and LPE (550–800 ms, parietal) showed a positive shift in amplitude for old compared to 284 

new items. While the frontal effect did not distinguish how often an image had been repeated, the 285 

parietal old/new effect only emerged for images presented for the third time, but not yet for images 286 

shown for the second time. A similarly graded shift across multiple repetitions has been reported 287 

by Johnson et al. (1998). These findings confirm that canonical ERP markers of recognition 288 

memory are preserved under continuous recognition conditions, replicating and extending previous 289 

studies (Berman et al., 1991; Friedman, 1990) to naturalistic scene photographs. 290 

Within the dual-process framework of recognition memory (Curran, 2000; Curran & Cleary, 291 

2003), the FN400 is broadly associated with familiarity-based recognition – a relatively automatic 292

While we did not assess familiarity and recollection directly, the progressive increase across 295

CRT, even as participants must simultaneously encode new items on each trial. This stands in 297 

contrast to the theoretical possibility that the dual demands of the CRT might selectively impair 298 

the more resource-intensive recollection process while sparing automatic familiarity. 299 

The differential sensitivity of FN400 and LPE to presentation number also aligns with the 300 

behavioral data. While accuracy and response times improved primarily at the third presentation, 301 

FN400 amplitude was significantly larger at both the second and third presentations relative 302 

to the first. By contrast, the LPE effect emerged only at the third, closely tracking behavioral 303 

improvement. This is consistent with the FN400 reflecting an early, coarse familiarity signal that 304 

does not strengthen with further repetition, while the LPE indexes a more graded recollection-305 

based process that builds with additional encoding opportunities. Taken together with the 306 

selective SME for items recognized on both repetitions, these findings suggest that especially 307 

the third presentation marks a shift toward a more robust, recollection-supported memory 308 

representation. 309 

The interpretation of the FN400 as a neural correlate of familiarity-based recognition, as 310 

proposed by dual-process accounts (Curran, 2000; Curran & Cleary, 2003), has been contested in 311 

the literature. An influential alternative view holds that the FN400 reflects conceptual implicit 312 

memory or fluency – a facilitated processing of previously encountered conceptual information 313 

– rather than episodic familiarity in the strict sense (Rugg & Curran, 2007). Accordingly, the 314 

FN400 old/new effect would not necessarily indicate that a participant consciously recognizes an 315 

item as previously seen, but rather that its conceptual representation is processed more fluently 316 

upon repetition. This distinction is particularly relevant here, since stimuli belonged to only 317 

four scene categories, raising the possibility that our FN400 effect partly reflects category-level 318 

fluency rather than item-level episodic familiarity. However, because new items belonged to the 319 

same four categories as old items throughout the experiment, category-level fluency should have 320 

been equally present for both and thus cannot account for the old/new difference. Nevertheless, 321 

we acknowledge that the precise functional interpretation of the FN400 remains debated. 322 

It is worth noting that recognition in the present study was considerably more demanding than 323 

in most prior ERP work, because participants had to discriminate between numerous exemplars 324 

from the same scene category rather than items from distinct conceptual categories, probably 325 

resulting in substantial categorical interference (Konkle et al., 2010). The robustness of old/new 326 

ERP effects under these conditions underscores the sensitivity of both FN400 and LPE as markers 327 

of visual recognition memory even when conceptual identity cannot serve as a reliable retrieval 328 

cue. 329

Lag effects 330 

A further novel finding concerns the differential sensitivity of the FN400 and LPE to the lag 331 

between consecutive image presentations, which is an indicator of memorial decay (Berman et al., 332 

1991), possibly due to item noise and context drift (Fox & Osth, 2023). While FN400 amplitude 333 

did not vary as a function of lag, LPE amplitude decreased systematically with increasing lag, 334 

mirroring the behavioral pattern of declining accuracy and slower responses at longer lags. This 335 

dissociation suggests that familiarity-based recognition is relatively robust to the number of 336 

intervening items and the interference they introduce, while recollection-based retrieval is more 337 

sensitive to memory strength and degrades more steeply with increasing lag. 338 

Notably, Friedman (1990) observed no modulation of either the FN400-like or LPE-like old/new 339 

effects across lags. As argued in the Introduction, this may have reflected a ceiling effect arising 340 

from the high memorability of distinctive object exemplars and a relatively short maximum lag 341 

of 32 intervening items. By employing a broader lag range (10 to 60 intervening trials) and 342 

stimuli more susceptible to forgetting due to within-category similarity, our design might have 343 

been better positioned to detect lag-dependent modulations. 344 

Subsequent memory effects 345 

The most theoretically significant finding of the present study is the observation of a significant 346 

subsequent memory effect (SME) in the CRT with images, in direct contrast to Friedman (1990). 347 

Critically, the SME emerged only for images remembered on both subsequent presentations, not 348 

for images remembered only once. At the first presentation, images later recognized twice elicited 349 

greater FN400 positivity and greater LPE negativity compared to images never subsequently 350 

recognized, whereas no significant ERP difference was observed between once-remembered and 351 

never-remembered images. 352 

Items encoded only weakly – sufficient for one later recognition but not two – may not have 353 

engaged elaborative encoding processes strongly enough to produce detectable ERP differences 354 

relative to forgotten items. Only images encoded robustly enough to support consistent recognition 355 

across two subsequent encounters showed a reliable SME. This interpretation is in line with the 356 

view that the SME indexes the depth or elaborateness of initial encoding (Voss & Paller, 2017), 357 

and suggests that under the competing demands of the CRT, only the most thoroughly encoded 358 

items leave a detectable neural trace at encoding. 359 

The absence of an SME in Friedman (1990) is thus likely attributable, at least in part, to a 360 

methodological constraint: with only a single repetition per image, subsequent memory could 361 

only be classified as remembered or forgotten without distinguishing between weakly and strongly 362 

encoded items. Our two-repetition design revealed that the SME is not absent in continuous 363 

recognition, but is selective for robustly encoded stimuli. 364 

Notably, while the positive FN400 SME at frontal electrodes is consistent with the direction 365

demand may explain the coexistence of both SME polarities, whereas studies using more easily 379 

discriminable stimuli, where a single encoding strategy may suffice, typically report a single 380 

(usually positive) polarity (Duarte et al., 2004). 381 

Limitations 382 

Several limitations of the present study warrant consideration. First, our stimulus set was 383 

restricted to four scene categories. While this restriction was theoretically motivated – to impose 384 

within-category discrimination demands – it limits the generalizability of the findings to other 385 

scene categories or other stimuli such as faces or objects. Second, the CRT by design does 386 

not permit full separation of encoding- and retrieval-related ERP activity, since both processes 387 

co-occur on every trial. While this is an inherent constraint of the paradigm, it is simultaneously 388 

one of its ecological virtues, as it more closely mirrors the conditions under which memory 389 

operates in everyday life. 390 

Conclusion 391 

The present study demonstrates that the neurocognitive mechanisms supporting visual recognition 392 

memory are not fundamentally disrupted when encoding and retrieval must occur in parallel in a 393 

continuous recognition task. Canonical ERP markers of recognition memory – the FN400 and the 394 

LPE – were robustly observed for naturalistic scene photographs, with amplitudes scaling with 395 

the number of repetitions. Critically, a lag-dependent dissociation between the two components 396 

revealed that recollection, but not familiarity, is sensitive to memory strength in the context of 397 

continuous recognition. Most importantly, we identified a SME that was selective for images 398 

subsequently remembered on both repetitions, reconciling the previously reported absence of the 399 

SME in CRT paradigms with the rich SME literature from two-phase designs. Together, these 400 

findings reveal that the CRT is not merely a pragmatic alternative to conventional paradigms, 401
