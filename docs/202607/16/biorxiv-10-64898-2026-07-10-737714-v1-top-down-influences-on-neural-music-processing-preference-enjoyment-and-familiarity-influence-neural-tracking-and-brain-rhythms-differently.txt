Abstract 

Music enjoyment and familiarity are closely related but often confounded in studies of neural music processing. Here, we investigated their distinct contributions to cortical oscillatory activity and neural tracking of music using electroencephalography (EEG). Thirty -two participants listened to self -selected all -time favourite songs, recent favourite songs, and tempo -matched songs from disliked genres. This novel paradigm dissociated familiarity from enjoyment by including highly enjoyed songs that differed in familiarity. Spectral power and cortical tracking (using Mutual Information) were analysed using linear mixed -effects models with enjoyment and familiarity ratings. Familiarity was associated with increased left -frontal alpha power, whereas enjoyment predicted increased theta and beta power, demonstrating distinct oscillatory signatures for these dimensions. An interaction revealed that the positive relationship between enjoyment and theta power was strongest for highly familiar music. Cortical tracking analyses showed that greater enjoyment was associated with reduced delta -

band tracking, with a significant interaction indicating that this negative relationship was present for highly familiar songs but not for less familiar songs. These findings indicate that enjoyment and familiarity differentially shape neural responses to music and highlight the importance of modelling both factors to disentangle their distinct effects on neural activity during music listening.

Introduction 1

Music has been part of human life all over the world for thousands of years. It surrounds us daily 2

and listening to music can have stress -reducing effects (Linnemann et al., 2016), reduce pain 3

(Costa et al., 2018; Jafari et al., 2012), and decrease depression and anxiety  (Costa et al., 2018; 4

Leubner & Hinterberger, 2017; S ärk ämö et al., 2008) . Moreover, daily music listening can 5

induce greater grey matter reorganisation  (S ärk ämö et al., 2014)  which highlights the 6

potentially profound effect of music on the brain. 7

Music preference is influenced by two broad factors: music -internal and music -external 8

parameters  (Sch äfer & Sedlmeier, 2010) . Music -internal factors include elements such as 9

tempo, harmony, and complexity, while music -external factors relate to the listener’s 10 

characteristics, including age, gender, personality, and cultural background. These preferences 11 

are highly personal (Huang et al., 2020; McDermott et al., 2014; Schaefer, 2016). A study with 12 

infants indicated a preference for music that is common in the culture (Soley & Hannon, 2010). 13 

Familiar music is also often preferred, sometimes referred to as a ‘mere exposure effect’ 14 

(Peretz et al., 1998; Madison & Schi ölde, 2017) . Music appreciation strongly relies on memory 15 

processes and familiarity and has therefore been described as an implicit memory 16 

phenomenon (Peretz et al., 1998; Green et al., 2012). Another explanation for our preference 17 

for familiar music comes from predictive processing, a theory according to which the brain 18 

creates a model to predict incoming sensory input (Fitzgerald & Todd, 2020), and its 19 

connections to the reward system. It has been shown that the better the sensory input matches 20 

the prediction, the stronger the response from dopamine cells, resulting in more pleasure 21 

(Salimpoor et al., 2015). Thus, more familiarity with a musical piece would involve more 22 

accurate predictions, resulting in a more pleasurable experience. 23 

Cortical oscillatory activity can shed light on how the brain processes music, and effects of 24 

preference, enjoyment or familiarity on music processing have been shown in all traditional 25 

frequency bands (i.e., delta (0 –4 Hz), theta (4 –8 Hz), alpha (8 –14 Hz), beta (14 –30 Hz), and 26 

gamma (>30 Hz) ). Frontal theta power has been found to increase when listening to pleasant 27 

music  (Ara & Marco -Pallar és, 2020; Sammler et al., 2007)  and this has also been found to be 28 

stronger when listening to a favourite song compared to a moderately preferred song (Tseng, 29 

2021). Another study found that posterior alpha power increased when listening to a preferred 30 

genre compared to a nonpreferred genre (Hurless et al., 2013). When listening to self -selected 31 

preferred music, an increase in posterior alpha and beta as well as temporal gamma power has 32 

been found (Nakano et al., 2021). These studies tend to convolute enjoyment and familiarity 33 

when self -selected preferred music, which is both enjoyed and familiar, is used to stu dy34 

enjoyment effects. 35 

When the specific effect of music familiarity on oscillatory power was studie d, one study found 36 

differences in all frequency bands (Thammasan et al., 2016). Specifically, listening to familiar 37 

music was associated with stronger frontal delta and posterior alpha power, as well as lower 38 

frontal midline /temporal theta, and lower temp oro -occipital beta and gamma power, than 39 

when listening to unfamiliar music. Another study found different connectivity patterns in the 40 

theta band for pleasant familiar and pleasant unfamiliar songs (Ara & Marco -Pallares, 2021). 41 

Overall, these varied results indicate that preference, enjoyment and familiarity of music might 42 

have distinct effects on cortical music processing. 43 

Beyond brain oscillations, cortical activity has also been found to track slow amplitude 44 

fluctuations in the acoustic signal such as rhythm and tempo (Keitel et al., 2025; Osorio & 45

Similarly, familiarity with music has been associated with stronger neural tracking in some 49

Previous studies looking at preferred compared to nonpreferred music have either used pre -54

Hurless et al., 2013) or have asked participants to bring their own music, with the nonpreferred 56 

songs being sourced from other participants’ music (Nakano et al., 2021). Similarly, studies 57 

looking at familiarity have not always considered music preference (Thammasan et al., 2016). 58 

The current study dissociated between familiarity and enjoyment by presenting participants 59 

with their “all -time favourite” music, which listeners were very familiar with, and their “recent 60 

favourite” music, which listeners were less familiar with, while showing comparable 61 

enjoyment. These preferre d stimuli were tempo -matched with songs from a disliked genre, thus 62 

tailoring the stimuli to each participant's individual preferences. We analysed oscillatory 63 

activity and cortical tracking comprehensively across a wide range of frequencies and tested 64 

the hypotheses that listening to favourite songs would result in higher power (in theta, alpha, 65 

beta and gamma bands) compared to disliked songs. The novel paradigm used in this pre -66 

registered study (https://osf.io/9yuhx ) allowed us to better disentangle familiarity and enjoyment 67 

effects on spectral activity and cortical tracking processes during listening to preferred and 68 

unpreferred music. 69 

Methods 70 

Participants 71 

Thirty -two participants took part in the study (17 female; age range 19 –27 years, M = 22.2, SD =72 

2.5). Participants were native English speakers, with no previous diagnosis of psychological or 73 

neurological disorders. All participants were right -handed (Oldfield, 1971). Thirty -one 74 

participants reported normal hearing (Quick Hearing Check; Koike et al., 1994), while one 75 

reported a score that might suggest slightly diminished hearing (score of 23/60, hearing test 76 

recommended from score 20). Participants also completed a short version of the Goldsmith’s 77 

Musical Sophistication Index (Gold -MSI; M üllensiefen et al., 2014). On average, participants 78 

scored a 4.26 (out of 7) on this questionnaire (range = 2.27 –6, SD = 1.11). Seven participants 79 

reported no musical training, while six participants reported 10 or more years of musical 80 

training. 81

Music stimuli 90 

Participants were asked to provide their favourite songs and disliked genres via an online 91 

session before coming into the lab. We requested them to submit two ‘all -time’ favourite songs 92 

and two ‘recent’ favourite songs. Additionally, we requested participants to choose up to three 93 

disliked genres from the following list: blues, classical, country, electronic, folk, funk, heavy 94 

metal, hip hop, jazz, latin /reggaeton, new age, pop, punk, R&B, rap, reggae, rock, soul or world. 95 

Four songs were chosen as the musical stimuli: 1) an all -time favourite song, 2) a recent 96 

favourite song, 3) a song from the disliked genres that matched the tempo and peak in the 97 

modulation spectrum of song 1, and 4) a song from the disliked genres that matched the tempo 98 

and peak in the modulation spectrum of song 2. To choose disliked songs, we first obtained the 99 

songs’ BPMs online (www.songbpm.com and www.getsongbpm). This allowed us to identify 100 

songs that likely matched the tempo from the favourite songs. Once a potential match was 101 

found, the pair was compared using the temporal modulation spectrum (Ding et al., 2017). The 102 

highest accepted difference in the songs’ peak frequencies was 1 Hz (please note that the pre -103 

registration specifies a maximum difference of .1 Hz, which was a typo). All songs had a 104 

sampling rate of 44,100 Hz and were cut to two minutes in length. A list of all songs is available 105 

in the Supplementary Information (supplemental Table S1). 106 

Experimental procedure 107 

The experiment consisted of two sessions: an online and an in -person session. The online 108 

experiment was created in Gorilla Experiment Builder (Anwyl -Irvine et al., 2020), to collect 109 

participants’ favourite songs and disliked genres. It also contained screening questions to 110 

assess whether the participant met inclusion criteria. We collected information on age, sex, 111 

handedness, neurological and psychological health. During the in -person session, participants 112 

completed the hearing questionnaire (Quick Hearing Check, Koike et al., 1994) and provided 113 

information about the amount of sleep and alcohol consumption the night before testing, 114 

caffeine intake the day of testing, plus their average sleep hours and average caffeine 115 

consumption. This information was used to confirm that participants were fit for testing. 116 

The EEG recording took place in a soundproof booth. Participants were seated at approximately 117 

65 cm from a 24” computer screen (‘iiyama ProLite B2483HS’). Music was played through high -118 

quality, wired headphones (‘Sennheiser HD25 700’). Auditory and visual information were 119 

presented using Psychtoolbox Version 3.0.17 for MATLAB (Brainard, 1997). Instructions with 120 

white text on black background were given on -screen. Participants were instructed to sit as still 121 

as possible and look at a green circle in the middle of the screen while listening to the songs. 122 

The order of the songs was randomised for each participant. Self -reported enjoyment and 123 

familiarity ratings were collected after listening to each of the four music tracks. Enjoyment and 124 

familiarity ratings were measured using Visual Analog Scales (VAS): a 10 cm horizontal line on 125 

paper presented with 'did not enjoy at all' and 'enjoyed a lot' as the two ends on the spectrum 126 

for the enjoyment question, and 'not at all familiar' and 'extremely familiar' for the familiarity 127 

question. Participants were instructed to draw a short vertical line on the scale, depending on 128 

their subjective experience. The lines were scored from 0 –100. 129 

EEG data acquisition and preprocessing 130 

EEG recordings were obtained with a 64 -channel BioSemi ActiveTwo system at a sampling rate 131 

of 512 Hz. The electrodes were placed according to the International 10 -20 system. Eye -132 

movements were recorded throughout the experiment using four electrooculographic (EOG) 133 

electrodes placed at the outer canthus of each eye (for horizontal movements) and above and 134 

below the left eye (for vertical eye movements and blinks). Any electrodes with offsets outside 135 

±20 mV were adjusted before the start of data recording. 136

Preprocessing of the EEG data was done in MATLAB R2023b using Fieldtrip (Oostenveld et al., 137 

2011) and custom -built functions. Data was cut out for each song with a leading and trailing 138 

window of ±2 s (resulting in segments with a length of 124 s per song). After re -referencing to 139 

channel Cz, data were filtered using a high pass filter of 0.1 Hz and a low pass filter of 80 Hz (3 rd 

140 

order Butterworth filter, forward and reverse). To identify noisy channels, we used a custom -141 

built function that assesses the standard deviation of EEG timeseries and highlights channels 142 

that exceed thresholds across channels and within -channels over time. After visually 143 

confirming exclusions, an average of M = 6.41 ( SD = 3.09) channels were interpolated (with no 144 

differences across conditions, all pFDR -values > 0.40). After re -referencing to the channel 145 

average (Bertrand et al. 1985), an Independent Component Analysis (ICA) was carried out on 146 

the combined data from all conditions to identify eye movements and blinks. On average, M =147 

1.78 components ( SD = 0.83) were removed per participant. Furthermore, a 50 -Hz discrete 148 

Fourier transform filter was applied to remove line noise. For frequency analyses, noisy 149 

segments were also identified and removed by segmenting the data across all conditions into 150 

3-s nonoverlapping windows and by removing segments with a z -scored standard deviation 151 

higher than two. An average of M = 6.5 ( SD = 2.38) segments were removed across channels 152 

and participants. 153 

Frequency analysis 154 

Spectral power was calculated using Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) on the segmented and pre -155 

processed data. Specifically, we separately calculated the power for frequencies between 0. 5156 

and 60 Hz (in semi -logarithmic frequency steps; 0. 5–15 Hz: 0.25 – Hz -steps, 15– 30 Hz: 0. 5– Hz -157 

steps, 30 –60 Hz: 1-Hz steps) using a DPSS multitaper (with ±2 Hz spectral smoothing and zero -158 

padding to 4 -s length), for every participant and condition. Power within each participant was 159 

normalised by subtracting the average power across all conditions from the power of each 160 

individual condition (mean centering). 161 

Music envelope preprocessing 162 

The amplitude envelope was extracted from all music pieces using an established procedure 163 

(e.g., Keitel et al., 2018; Gross et al., 2013). Specifically, audio signals were first filtered into 164 

eight frequency bands, ranging from 100 to 8000 Hz, using a third -order Butterworth filter 165 

applied in both forward and reverse directions. These frequency bands were spaced according 166 

to equal intervals along the cochlear frequency map (Smith & Delgutte, 2007). The wideband 167 

music envelopes were obtained by averaging the magnitude of the Hilbert transformed signals 168 

across the eight bands and both audio channels. Envelopes were then down -sampled to a 169 

sampling rate of 150 Hz. 170 

Mutual information analysis 171 

We calculated Gaussian Copula Mutual Information (MI; Ince et al., 2017) between the 172 

continuous EEG signals (down -sampled to 150 Hz) and the music envelopes and their 173 

derivative. This was done in the frequency domain, using a continuous wavelet transform for 174 

logarithmically -spaced frequencies between .25 and 20 Hz (Chalas et al., 2022). All signals 175 

were L1 normalised (Chalas et al., 2022). MI was initially computed at 12 different music -brain 176 

lags (from 8 to 30 sampling points, in 2 -sampling point steps, corresponding to a time window 177 

between ~50 ms and 200 ms after music onset) for each participant and condition. The optimal 178 

lag per participant and condition was chosen based on the highest MI in the delta band (i.e., MI -179 

values averaged between 0.5 to 4 Hz across channels), as acoustic modulations in music are 180 

most prominent and relevant in the delta band (Ding et al., 2017). Only MI computed at the 181 

optimal lag for each participant and condition was used for further analyses. We chose mutual 182 

information because it is a powerful framework for cortical tracking analysis. MI quantifies 183

Statistical analyses 188 

Outlier treatment. Behavioural and neural outlier values were removed before doing statistical 189 

analyses. For behavioural ratings, values larger/smaller than ± 3 SDs from the mean within each 190 

music condition were excluded (no exclusions for enjoyment ratings, 2 exclusions for familiarity 191 

ratings). Likewise, for neural power and MI values, outliers exceeding ± 3 SDs from the mean 192 

were removed. On average, M = 0.86 ( SD = 0.51) power values were removed across channels, 193 

frequencies and conditions. For MI values, an average of M = 0.21 ( SD = 0.42) values were 194 

removed across channels and conditions. 195 

Comparison of behavioural ratings. The ratings for familiarity and enjoyment were compared 196 

across all conditions using two -sided dependent -samples t-tests. All p-values were corrected 197 

for multiple comparisons using FDR correction (within the set of familiarity comparisons, and 198 

within the set of enjoyment comparisons). Additionally, we analysed the relationship between 199 

enjoyment and familiarity using a linear mixed -effects model. Specifically, the model formula 200 

was enjoyment rating ~ familiarity rating + (1 | Participant ID) . Note that the preregistration 201 

specified to analyse the “correlation between familiarity and enjoyment”, but that a linear 202 

mixed -effects model was deemed more in line with the overall analytical approach of the study. 203 

Comparisons between music conditions. Spectral power and MI were first compared across 204 

the four conditions using two -sided dependent -samples t-tests. For spectral power 205 

comparisons, cluster -based permutation tests were performed to identify spatial (across all 206 

electrodes) and spectral (across frequencies between 0.5 to 60 Hz) clusters. We used a Monte -207 

Carlo permutation approach with 5,000 randomisations and an alpha threshold of p = .05 (both 208 

for entering a sample into a cluster and as permutation -based significance threshold). The 209 

minimum number of adjacent electrodes was three, and of adjacent frequency bins was five 210 

(corresponding to a 1 -Hz window in low frequencies, and a 4 -Hz window at higher frequencies, 211 

due to the semi -logarithmic frequency resolution). 212 

For MI, we averaged values into delta (0.25 –4 Hz) and theta bands (4 –8 Hz). This was done 213 

because the tempo of songs varied widely within and across participants (see Supplementary 214 

Table S1). As cortical tracking of music strongly follows the rhythmic properties of the stimulus, 215 

including tempo, a frequency -wise comparison of stimuli with different tempi seems 216 

implausible. MI was therefore compared across conditions for averaged delta and theta bands. 217 

This means the cluster -based permutation correction was only used to identify spatial clusters. 218 

All other parameters were identical to the ones used for comparison of spectral power (i.e., 219 

Monte -Carlo approach with 5,000 randomisations, alpha thresholds of p = .05., minimum of 220 

three electrodes). To provide an effect size for statistical tests, we report Cohen’s d for the peak 221 

electrode/frequency. 222

Discussion 378 

The current study aimed to study the effects of familiarity and enjoyment on music processing 379 

when listening to preferred self -selected music and tempo -matched music from disliked 380 

genres. Including both all -time and recent favourite songs allowed us to assess the effects of 381 

familiarity and enjoyment in a systematic manner. 382 

We hypothesised that listening to favourite songs would result in higher power in the theta, 383 

alpha, beta and gamma band compared to disliked songs, however, we did not find any 384 

differences when comparing all -time and recent favourite songs to their disliked counterparts. 385 

Instead, condition comparisons for spectral power revealed that listening to all -time favourite 386 

songs was associated with weaker delta /theta power than listening to recent favourite songs, 387 

potentially relating to familiarity. The tempo -matched song categories (‘all -time disliked’ and 388 

‘recent disliked’ conditions) showed expected variability in familiarity and enjoyment, which 389 

likely is the reason why comparisons across conditions did not yield more significant results. 390 

Therefore, a mixed model approach was adopted using individual ratings for familiarity and 391 

enjoyment for all songs. These results did not confirm our finding in the delta /theta band. 392 

However, they revealed an effect of familiarity in the alpha band; the more familiar the song, the 393 

stronger was alpha power. Additionally, our mixed models revealed an effect of enjoyment on 394 

spectral power in the beta and theta bands. The more enjoyed a song was, the higher the beta 395 

and theta power. An interaction effect between enjoyment and familiarity on theta power 396 

revealed that the main effect of enjoyment was evident for high -familiar songs, but not for low -397 

familiar songs. 398 

We also found stronger cortical music tracking in the delta band when songs were less enjoyed, 399 

with no effect of familiarity on its own. However, we did observe an interaction between 400 

enjoyment and familiarity, showing strongest tracking when a song was familiar but not 401 

enjoyed. There were no significant differences in music tracking when comparing across 402 

conditions. 403 

Further, a linear mixed -effects model revealed that familiarity predicted enjoyment of songs. 404 

This is in line with the well -established phenomen on of liking familiar music, sometimes 405 

referred to as the ‘mere exposure effect’ (Peretz et al., 1998; Madison & Schiölde, 2017). 406 

Low-frequency power distinguishes between recent and all-time favourite songs 407 

We found a condition difference in delta -theta power between recent favourite and all -time 408 

favourite songs, with stronger low -frequency power during the recent favourite song than the 409 

all -time favourite song (Figure 2). This might suggest that low -frequency power could be 410 

associated with familiarity, however our mixed model results using continuous ratings did not 411 

replicate this finding. We are therefore careful to draw any firm conclusions on this result in low 412 

frequency bands. 413 

There is some evidence that supports this finding. Stronger theta power to unfamiliar than 414 

familiar music has been found (Thammasan et al., 2016; Meltzer et al., 2015). Theta oscillations 415 

have been shown to be involved in the encoding of new information (Klimesch, 1999), increased 416 

working memory demands (Klimesch, 2012), error processing as well as predictive processes 417 

(Arnal & Giraud, 2012; Kučikienė & Praninskienė, 2018; Recasens et al., 2018) . Thammasan et 418 

al. (2016) reasoned that higher theta power during unfamiliar songs is due to participants 419 

paying more attention and to reflect working memory and episodic memory processes. Our 420 

results seem to support this. However, it is important to note that while stronger theta power 421

power is less clear. One study suggested stronger delta power when listening to familiar 424 

compared to unfamiliar songs (Thammasan et al., 2016). However, they did not account for 425 

enjoyment. It is possible that our results indicate a specific effect of familiarity for highly 426 

enjoyed songs, but as stated before, we cannot draw any firm conclusions as this effect was 427 

not replicated in our mixed -model analyses. 428 

Alpha power indexes familiarity 429 

Our results indicated that listening to familiar music is associated with an increase of alpha 430 

power. We found this effect as an effect of self -rated familiarity, when controlling for enjoyment. 431 

This finding is in line with Thammasan et al. (2016), who reported stronger alpha power when 432 

participants listened to 2 -minute excerpts of familiar compared to unfamiliar songs. However, 433 

the opposite pattern has also previously been found, with familiar music associated with 434 

reduced alpha power (or increased alpha desynchronisation ) (Malekmohammadi et al., 2023). 435 

A key methodological difference was that this study used short (10 -second) excerpts, whereas 436 

our study, as well as Thammasan et al. (2016), employed longer musical pieces (2 minutes). It 437 

is therefore possible that the direction of alpha effects depends on the time scale, with 438 

sustained listening to familiar music being associated with increased alpha power. 439 

We initially expected our results to be an indication of predictive processes. However, previous 440 

literature shows that predictive processes are related to a reduction in alpha power instead, 441 

specifically in the context of predictive sounds (Mohanta et al., 2021). Increased alpha power 442 

during music listening (relative to rest) has been associated with internally directed attention, 443 

such as mind -wandering, imagination, and meditative states (Markovic et al., 2017). Extending 444 

this interpretation to our findings, it is possible that greater familiarity facilitates this inward 445 

shift, leading to increased alpha power. In line with this, a prominent account of alpha 446 

oscillations proposed that alpha synchronization reflects the active inhibition of task -irrelevant 447 

processing and the controlled access to stored information (Klimesch, 2012). Under this 448 

framework, increased alpha power during more familiar music listening may indicate greater 449 

reliance on internally generated representations and memory -related processes supported by 450 

long -term knowledge of the musical material (Peretz et al., 1998). 451 

Theta and beta power index enjoyment 452 

We found an effect of enjoyment on theta and beta power, with more enjoyed music associated 453 

with increased theta and beta power. An association between theta power and music evoked 454 

pleasantness is well established (e.g., Ara & Marco -Pallarés, 2020, 2021; Sammler et al, 2007; 455 

Ara et al., 2026 ).456 

Frontal theta power has consistently been implicated in emotional processing. For example, it 457 

has been shown that listening to a participant’s favourite song elicited higher theta, alpha, and 458 

beta power, while no differences emerged between neutral and moderately preferred music 459 

(Tseng, 2021). This suggests that strong subjective preference may be necessary to elicit 460 

pronounced prefrontal theta responses. Another study found increased fronto -central theta 461 

activity during listening to pleasurable music, with theta power scaling alongside arousal and 462 

emotional intensity (Chabin et al., 2020). These findings have been interpreted as a role of 463 

frontal theta power in reward processing and affective engagement with music. A limitation of 464 

the current study is that participants’ emotional responses were not measured, making it 465 

unclear whether the observed theta power increase indeed reflects emotional processing. 466

We also found an interaction effect of enjoyment and familiarity on theta power. Specifically, 467 

the theta increase associated with higher enjoyment seemed to have been driven by songs with 468 

high familiarity (Figure 3). This interaction, and the fact that in the condition comparisons the 469 

only differences found were between the two favourite song categories, could indicate that 470 

enjoyment effects on theta power are strongest for familiar music. This suggests that when 471 

investigating the effect of enjoyment on music processing, it is essential to assess subjective 472 

familiarity with the music. 473 

The literature on beta power and music enjoyment is mixed. Beta power is commonly 474 

associated with predictive processes during listening (e.g., Morillon & Baillet, 2017), but less 475 

commonly with enjoyment. The above study by Tseng (2023) is in line with our findings of 476 

increased beta power when participants listened to their favourite music. Another study 477 

(Hadjidimitriou & Hadjileontiadis, 2012) found stronger beta power when listening to disliked 478 

compared to liked music, a pattern that is opposite of what we found. However, they used short 479 

15-sec unfamiliar music clips, while the current study employed longer, partly self -chosen and 480 

familiar music clips. Prior work has also linked beta activity to reward processing and predictive 481 

timing (Mas -Herrero et al. 2015, Kawasaki and Yamaguchi 2013, Weigmann, 2017). For 482 

instance, it was proposed that enjoyment arises partly from the brain’s ability to anticipate 483 

rhythmic structure, with oscillatory activity supporting temporal prediction and reward 484 

(Weigmann, 2017). However, as we found no effect of familiarity on beta power when 485 

controlling for enjoyment, we cannot conclude that the effect of enjoyment was linked to 486 

prediction processes. Our results show that, when measuring both individual familiarity and 487 

enjoyment and using diverse stimuli, beta power was primarily linked to enjoyment. 488 

Subjective enjoyment predicts cortical delta tracking 489 

We found that more enjoyment was associated with less cortical tracking when controlling for 490 

the familiarity of music (Figure 4). Our results are in line with others that showed that more 491 

enjoyment was associated with lower neural tracking of acoustic temporal features (Hartung et 492 

al., 2025). Interestingly, they found that tracking of higher -level predictions (such as melody) 493 

was instead linked to higher enjoyment and familiarity. They suggested that when tracking of 494 

higher -level predictions was not possible, the tracking was focused on lower -level acoustics 495 

and was thus related to lower enjoyment. It could be argued that one would be less able to 496 

predict melody when one is unfamiliar with a song, and this would explain why the tracking was 497 

focused on lower -level features. However, our analyses revealed an interaction effect of 498 

enjoyment and familiarity, which showed that the main effect of enjoyment was present only in 499 

high -familiar songs (Figure 4). Songs rated as high in familiarity but low in enjoyment may 500 

represent music that participants are frequently exposed to without actively choosing to listen 501 

to them. Popular songs, for example, are often encountered in social settings, on the radio, in 502 

advertisements, films, or through social media, leading to high familiarity without necessarily 503 

eliciting enjoyment. In low -familiar songs, the opposite pattern was found, where more 504 

enjoyment was associated with slightly stronger tracking. This is in line with a previous study 505 

showing increased cortical tracking with more subjective enjoyment, which only used 506 

unfamiliar stimuli (Keitel et al., 2025). Another study found no effect of enjoyment, though they 507 

analysed tracking of spectral flux which could potentially explain the difference (Weineck et al., 508 

2022). 509 

Previous studies have found stronger tracking of familiar music (Weickeck et al., 2022; Doelling 510 

& Poeppel, 2015) as well as stronger tracking of unfamiliar music (Kumangai et al., 2017, 2018) 511 

whereas we found no main effect of familiarity at all. Potentially the differences in stimuli and 512 

methods that were used to analyse tracking could be the reason for these contradictions. In 513

responses, expectation has been found to reduce them (Arnal & Giraud, 2012). However, as we 521 

did not find a direct relationship between familiarity and cortical tracking, we cannot draw 522 

direct conclusions about expectation effects. Our results show the importance of measuring 523 

both subjective enjoyment and stimulus familiarity when studying cortical tracking, as opposite 524 

effects can be found depending on the level of familiarity. 525 

Both categorical predictors, enjoyment and familiarity, were significant predictors for cortical 526 

theta tracking. We refrain from interpreting these effects because they are not reflected in 527 

continuous ratings or condition comparisons. However, the present findings reveal a possible 528 

dissociation between delta and theta band tracking. Previous studies have often looked at delta 529 

and theta tracking jointly, as “low frequency tracking” (Doelling et al., 2019; Harding et al. 2019). 530 

Our results suggest that delta and theta tracking may reflect distinct processes and therefore 531 

should be analysed separately rather than being combined into a single low frequency tracking 532 

band. 533 

Limitations 534 

Several limitations should be considered. Participants likely differed in how they defined and 535 

selected “all -time” and “recent” favourites, introducing variability in familiarity and emotional 536 

salience. The use of post -listening ratings may also have influenced neural responses, as rating 537 

tasks can alter EEG amplitudes across frequency bands (Markovic et al., 2017). Additionally, 538 

anticipation effects may have arisen for the preferred songs because participants submitted 539 

these songs themselves and could therefore anticipate hearing their preferred songs. 540 

Conclusions 541 

The current study applies a novel paradigm, not just aiming to distinguish the neural correlates 542 

between liked versus disliked music but separating familiarity from enjoyment by using all time 543 

and recent favourite music. We highlight the importance of controlling for both enjoyment and 544 

familiarity to disentangle the different effects they have on neural act ivity, as this can lead to 545 

opposing results shown in the literature. 546
