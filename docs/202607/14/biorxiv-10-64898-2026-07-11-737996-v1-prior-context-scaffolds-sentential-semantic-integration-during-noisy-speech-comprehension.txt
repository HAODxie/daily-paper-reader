Abstract 23 

Understanding speech in noise is a central challenge of everyday communication, 24 

yet listeners often succeed by using prior context. How the brain uses such context 25 

remains debated: it may refine predictions about upcoming words, or it may provide a 26 

higher-level framework that helps degraded speech cohere into meaning. Here we 27 

combined simultaneous EEG-fNIRS recording with hierarchical multivariate encoding 28 

models to track how prior context shapes speech processing from acoustics to words and 29 

sentential meaning. Participants listened to natural spoken narratives under clear speech, 30 

noisy speech, and context-supported noisy speech conditions. Context brought 31 

comprehension of noisy speech close to clear-speech levels. EEG revealed that 32 

contextual support reduced neural encoding of lexical surprisal and entropy, indicating 33 

weaker tracking of local word-level prediction demands. In contrast, when context was 34 

available, fNIRS showed enhanced encoding of sentence-level semantic integration 35 

across frontal regions and the right angular gyrus, and stronger angular gyrus encoding 36 

predicted better comprehension. By combining EEG and fNIRS to capture 37 

complementary electrophysiological and hemodynamic signals, this multimodal approach 38 

reveals a hierarchical shift in degraded speech comprehension: prior context does not 39 

simply improve word-by-word prediction, but scaffolds the integration of noisy input into 40 

coherent discourse. 41

Introduction 47 

Understanding speech in noisy environments is a common challenge of everyday 48 

communication. In a crowded room, on a busy street, or through a poor audio connection, 49 

the acoustic signal reaching the listener is often incomplete or unreliable. Yet 50 

comprehension frequently remains possible because speech is rarely interpreted as an 51 

isolated stream of sounds. Listeners can draw on prior knowledge, the topic of 52 

conversation, and the broader meaning of the unfolding discourse to infer what is being 53 

said. Behavioral and neurocognitive studies have shown that contextual information, 54 

including familiar topics, preceding linguistic context, or a brief synopsis, can facilitate the 55 

perception and comprehension of degraded speech [1–5]. However, the neural 56 

mechanisms through which prior context supports speech-in-noise comprehension 57 

remain incompletely understood. 58 

One influential possibility is that prior context helps listeners by strengthening 59 

prediction. During language comprehension, the brain continuously uses preceding 60 

contextual information to anticipate upcoming input [6–9]. A constraining context can 61 

reduce uncertainty about likely words and make unexpected words more salient, thereby 62 

supporting interpretation when the acoustic signal is unreliable. This predictive 63 

mechanism may be especially useful in noisy listening, where bottom-up sensory 64 

evidence is degraded [1,10,11] and listeners may need to rely more strongly on top-down 65 

expectations to identify words and resolve ambiguity [12,13]. From this perspective, 66 

informative prior context could facilitate comprehension by increasing lexical predictability 67 

and reducing uncertainty about upcoming linguistic input. 68 

A second, complementary possibility is that prior context supports comprehension 69 

by providing a semantic scaffold. Rather than primarily improving word-by-word prediction, 70 

context may offer a higher-level framework that helps listeners organize degraded speech 71 

into a coherent representation of the speaker’s intended meaning. Schema-based 72 

theories propose that prior knowledge can provide an organizing structure within which 73 

new information is interpreted, integrated, and remembered [14–17]. This mechanism 74 

may be particularly relevant for naturalistic speech, where comprehension depends not 75 

only on identifying individual words but also on integrating sentences over time, 76 

maintaining the topic of the discourse, and linking new information to what has already 77 

been understood [18,19]. Consistent with this view, previous studies have implicated 78 

regions such as the angular gyrus, a region associated with semantic integration, in using 79 

semantic context to support speech perception under noisy or degraded conditions [20– 80

Although both prediction and semantic scaffolding are plausible mechanisms, their 83 

relative contributions to contextual facilitation during naturalistic speech-in-noise 84 

comprehension remain unclear. Many previous studies have used isolated words, word 85 

pairs, or short sentences, which are well suited for testing specific mechanisms but may 86 

not fully capture the extended, incremental, and discourse-level processes involved in 87 

real-life listening [23,24]. Moreover, prior work has often focused on a single level of 88 

processing, such as acoustic tracking, lexical prediction, or semantic integration 89 

[20,25,26]. As a result, it remains difficult to determine whether prior context facilitates 90 

degraded speech comprehension by broadly enhancing neural encoding across 91 

processing levels, strengthening local lexical prediction, or primarily supporting higher-92 

level semantic integration. 93 

Addressing this question requires both naturalistic materials and a framework that 94 

can separate neural responses to different levels of speech information. Multivariate 95 

temporal response function modeling provides such an approach by estimating how 96 

neural activity tracks continuous stimulus features over time [27,28]. This framework has 97 

been widely used to examine neural encoding of acoustic, lexical, and semantic features 98 

during continuous speech comprehension [29–32]. In the present study, we used this 99 

approach to dissociate neural encoding of acoustic, lexical, and sentential features during 100 

narrative listening. Acoustic envelope encoding indexed tracking of low-level speech 101 

fluctuations; lexical surprisal and entropy indexed word-level prediction error and 102 

uncertainty; and sentence-level semantic dissimilarity indexed how each incoming 103 

sentence was integrated with the evolving discourse context. 104 

We further combined this hierarchical encoding approach with simultaneous EEG-105 

fNIRS recording. EEG captures electrophysiological responses with high temporal 106 

sensitivity, making it well suited for tracking rapid acoustic and lexical dynamics during 107 

continuous speech [29,30]. fNIRS measures changes in cortical blood oxygenation and it 108 

allowed us to examine regional engagement across frontal, temporal, and parietal 109 

cortices that are relevant to higher-level language and semantic processing [12,33]. By 110 

recording these complementary electrophysiological and hemodynamic signals at the 111 

same time, we aimed to characterize how prior context shapes both rapid speech-evoked 112 

dynamics and slower, regionally distributed cortical engagement during degraded speech 113 

comprehension. 114

hierarchy (Fig. 1 C). A lexical-prediction account would predict stronger or more efficient 121

scaffolding account would predict enhanced encoding of sentential semantic integration, 123 

particularly in cortical regions involved in constructing coherent discourse meaning. We 124 

also examined whether these context-sensitive neural responses were associated with 125 

comprehension performance. By combining naturalistic speech materials, simultaneous 126 

EEG-fNIRS recording, and hierarchical encoding models, this study tests how prior 127 

context shapes the neural processing of degraded speech from acoustic encoding to 128 

word-level prediction and sentence-level semantic integration. This approach allows us 129 

to ask not only whether context supports noisy speech comprehension, but also how the 130 

brain uses context to organize imperfect sensory input into meaningful communication. 131

Results 133 

Prior context facilitates noisy speech comprehension 134 

Participants listened to 30 spoken narratives under one of three conditions: clear 135 

speech (CL), noisy speech (NS), and noisy speech preceded by prior context (NSC). In 136 

the NSC condition, prior context was provided as a brief synopsis of the upcoming story. 137 

Each narrative lasted approximately 90 s and was followed by four multiple-choice 138 

comprehension questions. Participants also rated the clarity and intelligibility of each 139 

narrative. 140 

Speech comprehension performance was well above chance level (25%) across all 141 

conditions (CL: 89.84% ± 5.82%, mean ± SE; NS: 85.70% ± 6.79%; NSC: 88.59% ± 142 

7.51%; Bayesian one-sample t-test, all BF 10 > 100). Bayesian pairwise comparisons 143 

provided moderate evidence that comprehension was lower in NS than in CL ( BF 10 =144 

6.78), indicating that acoustic degradation reduced listeners’ ability to extract narrative 145 

content. Comprehension in NSC was higher than in NS, with suggestive evidence for this 146 

difference ( BF 10 = 2.06; Fig. 1 D), while the comparison between CL and NSC provided 147 

moderate evidence for the absence of a difference ( BF 10 = 0.26; BF 01 = 3.85). 148 

Subjective ratings showed a different pattern. Clarity ratings were lower in both noisy 149 

conditions than in clear speech (NS vs. CL and NSC vs. CL, both BF 10 > 100), and there 150 

was modest evidence for the absence of a difference between NS and NSC ( BF 10 = 0.43). 151 

Intelligibility ratings were also decisively lower in both noisy conditions than in clear 152 

speech (both BF 10 > 100). Moreover, there was suggestive evidence that intelligibility 153 

ratings were lower in NSC than in NS ( BF 10 = 2.68). These results indicate that prior 154 

context did not make the noisy speech sound clearer or more intelligible; instead, 155 

contextual support appeared to help listeners answer content-based comprehension 156 

questions despite persistently degraded subjective perceptual quality. 157

Hierarchical encoding models dissociate acoustic, lexical, and sentential speech 159 

processing 160 

To examine how prior context modulated neural encoding at different levels of 161 

speech comprehension, we used multivariate temporal response function (mTRF) models 162 

to relate continuous speech features to simultaneously recorded EEG and fNIRS signals. 163 

Six predictors were extracted from each narrative to capture acoustic, lexical, and 164 

sentential level information (Fig. 1 E): acoustic envelope, lexical onset, lexical surprisal, 165 

lexical entropy, sentential onset, and sentential semantic dissimilarity [7,13,29,31,34]. 166 

Together, these predictors allowed us to ask whether contextual support primarily 167

For each predictor, we quantified its unique contribution by comparing the prediction 170 

accuracy of the full model with that of a reduced model in which the predictor of interest 171 

was removed. This approach allowed us to estimate whether a given speech feature 172 

explained neural activity beyond the variance shared with other acoustic or linguistic 173 

features. Unique contributions were summarized within predefined regions of interest 174 

(ROIs) and compared across listening conditions using Bayesian statistics. 175 

Because EEG and fNIRS capture different types of neural signals, we used modality-176 

appropriate summaries of the encoding results. For EEG, we examined both the unique 177 

contribution of each predictor and the temporal profile of the corresponding TRF 178 

waveform, allowing us to identify when acoustic, lexical, or sentential information was 179 

encoded. Significant temporal clusters were identified using cluster-based permutation 180 

tests, and peak amplitudes and latencies were extracted from these clusters. For fNIRS, 181 

which reflects slower hemodynamic responses, we focused on ROI-level unique 182 

contributions and used TRF waveforms primarily for visualization. This analysis 183 

framework allowed us to test whether prior context altered speech processing similarly 184 

across levels, or instead shifted the balance between lexical prediction and sentential 185 

semantic integration. 186

We next examined whether prior context strengthened word-level predictive 236 

processing during noisy speech comprehension. Lexical surprisal quantified how 237 

unexpected each word was given the preceding context, whereas lexical entropy 238 

quantified uncertainty about likely upcoming words [7,13,29,34,35]. If contextual 239 

facilitation was primarily driven by enhanced lexical prediction, we would expect prior 240 

context to increase neural encoding of these lexical prediction-related features. 241 

In the EEG analysis, lexical surprisal made unique contributions over temporal and 242 

parietal regions within the 400–600 ms window (Fig. 3 A). Cluster-based permutation tests 243 

further identified an N400-like negative TRF component over parietal regions in all three 244 

conditions (cluster-level p = 0.003, 0.005, and 0.018 for CL, NS, and NSC, respectively; 245 

FDR corrected). Bayesian paired-sample comparisons showed that this negative 246 

component was strongly attenuated in noisy speech preceded by prior context (NSC): the 247 

peak amplitude was larger in CL than in NSC ( BF 10 > 100), and larger in NS than in NSC 248 

(BF 10 = 3.02; Fig. 3 C). The comparison between CL and NS provided weaker evidence 249 

for an amplitude difference ( BF 10 = 2.05). Thus, rather than enhancing lexical surprisal 250 

encoding under noise, prior context reduced neural sensitivity to word-level prediction 251 

error. 252 

Lexical entropy made unique contributions over frontal regions within the 400–800 253 

ms window and over parietal regions within the 600–800 ms window (Fig. 3 B). However, 254 

no reliable TRF component was identified in either region (Fig. 3 D). We therefore interpret 255 

the entropy results cautiously: they suggest that lexical uncertainty was represented in 256 

the EEG signal, but they do not provide strong temporal evidence that prior context 257 

enhanced uncertainty-related encoding. 258 

We then examined whether lexical prediction-related encoding was associated with 259 

speech comprehension performance. In NSC, stronger parietal encoding of lexical 260 

surprisal within the 400–600 ms window was associated with better comprehension 261 

accuracy (Spearman’s rs = 0.40, p = 0.031; Fig. 3 E). In addition, a larger parietal N400-262 

like surprisal response was associated with higher comprehension accuracy across all 263 

three conditions (CL: rs = -0.39, p = 0.037; NS: rs = -0.41, p = 0.027; NSC: rs = -0.56, p264 

= .002; Fig. 3 F). No reliable associations were observed for peak latency (all p > 0.1). 265 

These associations suggest that word-level prediction-error processing remained 266 

behaviorally relevant, even though prior context reduced group-level surprisal encoding 267 

under noise. 268

Prior context enhances fNIRS encoding of sentential semantic integration 281 

We next examined whether prior context modulated sentential semantic integration 282 

during noisy speech comprehension. Sentential semantic dissimilarity quantified how 283 

much each incoming sentence differed from the evolving discourse context and was used 284 

to index the integration of new sentential meaning into the unfolding narrative 285 

representation. If prior context supported comprehension by providing a semantic scaffold, 286 

we expected context-supported noisy speech to show stronger encoding of this sentential 287 

semantic feature than noisy speech alone. 288 

The EEG analysis did not reveal reliable encoding of sentential semantic dissimilarity 289 

in any condition (all BF 10 < 1), suggesting that this feature was not robustly captured by 290 

electrophysiological responses in the present analysis. In contrast, the fNIRS analysis 291 

revealed condition-dependent hemodynamic encoding of sentential semantic dissimilarity 292 

across cortical regions. In CL, evidence for a unique contribution was restricted to the 293 

right IFG ( BF 10 = 3.17; Fig. 4 A). In the NS condition, no ROI showed evidence for a unique 294 

contribution (all BF 10 < 0.60), suggesting that acoustic degradation disrupted measurable 295 

sentential semantic encoding in the fNIRS signal. 296 

When prior context was available, sentential semantic dissimilarity encoding became 297 

more broadly expressed. In NSC, evidence for a unique contribution was observed in 298 

bilateral IFG ( BF 10 = 3.51 and 2.84 for the left and right hemispheres, respectively), 299 

bilateral MFG ( BF 10 = 12.08 and 1.87), left superior frontal gyrus (SFG; BF 10 = 1.50), and 300 

the right AG ( BF 10 = 3.10). Direct condition comparisons further revealed that right IFG 301 

encoding was lower in NS than in NSC ( BF 10 = 3.33), with suggestive evidence that NS 302 

was also lower than CL ( BF 10 = 1.79). Evidence for a difference between CL and NSC in 303 

the right IFG was weak and favored no clear difference ( BF 10 = 0.46; BF 01 = 2.17). 304

Onset-related encoding suggests limited contextual modulation of speech 314 

boundary tracking 315 

To determine whether the context-related effects observed for sentential semantic 316 

dissimilarity reflected broader changes in speech-boundary tracking, we further examined 317 

neural encoding of lexical and sentential onsets. Lexical onset indexed word-boundary-318 

related processing, whereas sentential onset indexed sentence-boundary-related 319 

processing. These onset predictors allowed us to assess whether prior context generally 320 

enhanced segmentation of continuous noisy speech, or whether its effect was more 321 

specific to sentential semantic integration. 322 

For lexical onset, EEG analysis revealed unique contributions primarily within the 323 

200–600 ms window over central and temporal regions ( SI Appendix , Fig. S3 C). Over 324 

central regions, cluster-based permutation tests identified an early positive TRF 325 

component within the 0–200 ms window (cluster-level p = 0.028, 0.013 and 0.042 for CL, 326 

NS and NSC, respectively; FDR corrected). Bayesian comparisons showed strong 327 

evidence that the latency of this component was prolonged in NS relative to CL and 328 

shortened in NSC relative to NS (both BF 10 > 10; SI Appendix , Fig. S3 D), indicating that 329 

acoustic degradation delayed word-boundary-related responses while prior context 330 

mitigated this delay. Over temporal regions, lexical onset elicited a positive TRF 331 

component within the 400–600 ms window across all three conditions (cluster-level p =332 

0.031, 0.003, and 0.004 for CL, NS, and NSC, respectively; FDR corrected). This 333 

component showed moderate evidence for prolonged latency in NSC relative to CL ( BF 10 334 

= 7.72), whereas the evidence for amplitude differences or latency differences between 335 

NSC and NS was weaker (NSC vs. CL, BF 10 = 1.36; NSC vs. NS, BF 10 = 1.77; SI 336 

Appendix , Fig. S3 E). Thus, prior context selectively modulated lexical onset encoding, 337 

mainly by altering response timing, rather than producing a broad enhancement of word-338 

boundary tracking. 339

The fNIRS analysis showed a complementary pattern. Lexical onset encoding was 340 

more spatially restricted in NSC than in CL or NS ( SI Appendix , Fig. S3 A and B), 341 

suggesting that prior context did not increase widespread hemodynamic engagement for 342 

word-boundary processing. Instead, when a global semantic context was available, 343 

listeners may have required less regionally distributed engagement for segmenting the 344 

noisy speech stream into individual words. This interpretation should be treated 345 

cautiously, however, because several ROI-level effects were modest. 346 

For sentential onset, EEG analysis revealed unique contributions between 200–600 347 

ms over central, parietal and temporal regions across conditions ( SI Appendix , Fig. S4 C). 348 

However, no reliable TRF component was identified ( SI Appendix , Fig. S4 D). The fNIRS 349 

analysis further showed that regional involvement in sentential onset encoding was 350 

broadly similar between NS and NSC ( SI Appendix , Fig. S4 A and B), with shared 351 

involvement of parietal and sensorimotor regions and no clear evidence that prior context 352 

generally enhanced sentential boundary encoding (NS: BF 10 = 1.33, 1.21, 2.34, 2.99; 353 

NSC: 1.71, 2.52, 2.12, 3.51, for left SPG, right PoCG, SMG and SPG, respectively). No 354 

ROI showed evidence for a unique contribution of sentence onset in CL ( BF 10 < 0.86). 355 

These results suggest that sentence boundaries became more salient under noisy 356 

listening, but that providing prior context did not strongly amplify sentential boundary 357 

tracking. 358 

Together, the onset-related results indicate that prior context had limited and level-359 

specific effects on speech-boundary encoding. Lexical onset responses showed some 360 

context-related modulation, whereas sentential onset encoding was broadly similar 361 

between noisy speech with and without prior context. This pattern supports the 362 

interpretation that the broader fNIRS encoding of sentential semantic dissimilarity in NSC 363 

was unlikely to reflect a general enhancement of sentential boundary responses. Instead, 364 

prior context appeared to affect higher-level semantic integration more clearly than basic 365 

boundary tracking. 366

Discussion 368 

The present study investigated how prior context supports comprehension of noisy 369 

natural speech. By combining simultaneous EEG-fNIRS recording with hierarchical 370 

mTRF modeling, we examined how contextual support modulated neural encoding of 371 

speech information from acoustic tracking to lexical prediction and sentential semantic 372 

integration. Three main findings emerged. First, prior context brought comprehension 373 

performance under noise close to clear-speech levels, although the direct evidence for 374

fNIRS revealed broader encoding of sentential semantic dissimilarity across frontal 379 

regions and the right angular gyrus when prior context was available, and stronger right 380 

angular-gyrus encoding predicted better comprehension. Together, these findings 381 

suggest that prior context scaffolds noisy speech comprehension not simply by 382 

strengthening word-by-word prediction, but by helping listeners integrate degraded input 383 

into a coherent discourse-level representation. 384 

The key advance of this study is that it identifies sentential semantic integration as a 385 

context-sensitive neural process through which prior context scaffolds comprehension of 386 

noisy natural speech. In the present study, prior context did not merely provide additional 387 

information about the upcoming story; it was associated with the re-emergence and 388 

broadening of fNIRS encoding of sentential semantic dissimilarity under noise, 389 

particularly across frontal regions and the right angular gyrus. This pattern supports the 390 

view that a global contextual framework can serve as a semantic scaffold, helping 391 

listeners organize degraded spoken input into an evolving discourse representation [14– 392 

17]. Such a scaffolding account is particularly relevant for naturalistic speech, where 393 

successful comprehension requires listeners to maintain an evolving situation model, 394 

integrate information across sentences into the broader topic of the discourse, and 395 

understand storytellers’ worldview [18,19,36,37]. The involvement of frontal regions and 396 

the angular gyrus further aligns with previous work linking these areas to discourse 397 

comprehension, internally guided semantic integration, and the use of semantic context 398 

during speech perception under adverse listening conditions [18,20,21,37–39]. 399 

Importantly, stronger right angular-gyrus encoding of sentential semantic dissimilarity 400 

predicted better comprehension in the context-supported noisy condition, suggesting that 401 

this integration-related response was behaviorally relevant. Thus, prior context may have 402 

scaffolded comprehension by helping listeners fit imperfect input into a higher-level 403 

representation of narrative meaning. 404 

The lexical-level findings refine this scaffolding interpretation by showing that prior 405 

context did not strengthen word-by-word prediction-related encoding under noise. Lexical 406 

surprisal and entropy capture complementary aspects of predictive language processing: 407 

surprisal reflects the prediction error associated with the current word, whereas entropy 408 

reflects uncertainty about likely upcoming words [7,29,34,35]. Consistent with previous 409

segmentation of continuous speech into word- and sentence-level units, a process that 431 

may become especially important when acoustic degradation alters the availability and 432 

reliability of segmentation cues [42–44]. In the present study, lexical onset responses 433

more spatially restricted fNIRS encoding in the context-supported noisy condition. These 435 

findings are compatible with the possibility that a global semantic scaffold facilitated more 436 

efficient access to lexical boundaries while reducing the need for broadly distributed word-437

boundary tracking: sentential onset encoding was broadly similar between noisy speech 439 

with and without prior context. This pattern contrasts with the broader fNIRS encoding of 440

the semantic-integration effect cannot be reduced to stronger tracking of sentence 442

The distinct patterns observed in EEG and fNIRS also highlight the value of 444 

measuring complementary electrophysiological and hemodynamic signals during 445 

naturalistic speech comprehension [45,46]. In the present study, lexical surprisal effects 446 

were most clearly expressed in EEG responses, whereas context-sensitive encoding of 447 

sentential semantic dissimilarity was most clearly expressed in fNIRS responses. This 448 

dissociation is consistent with the different temporal characteristics of the processes 449 

being measured. Word-level prediction error unfolds rapidly as each word is encountered, 450 

making it well suited to EEG measures of time-sensitive electrophysiological dynamics. 451 

By contrast, sentence-level semantic integration accumulates over longer timescales and 452 

may depend on distributed cortical engagement across frontal and temporoparietal 453 

regions, making it more readily reflected in hemodynamic responses measured from the 454 

cortical areas covered by the fNIRS montage. Thus, simultaneous EEG-fNIRS recording 455 

allowed us to characterize contextual facilitation across complementary neural signals, 456 

revealing attenuated lexical prediction-related responses alongside broader cortical 457 

hemodynamic encoding of sentential semantic integration. More broadly, these findings 458 

illustrate how multimodal recording can help capture the layered nature of language 459 

comprehension, in which acoustic, lexical, and discourse-level processes unfold over 460 

different temporal scales and neural systems during real-life listening. 461 

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these findings. First, 462 

fNIRS is primarily sensitive to cortical regions covered by the optode montage and has 463 

limited access to deeper structures and early auditory regions, including primary auditory 464 

cortex [47,48]. Therefore, the present fNIRS results are best interpreted as reflecting 465 

hemodynamic engagement within the sampled frontal, temporal, and parietal cortices, 466 

rather than a complete account of the neural systems supporting speech-in-noise 467 

comprehension. Second, prior context was operationalized as a brief synopsis rather than 468 

as a clear version of the same passage or a verbatim transcript. This choice was intended 469 

to provide global discourse-level information while avoiding direct access to the target 470 

speech content, but different forms of prior context may engage prediction and integration 471 

mechanisms differently. Future work could compare synopsis-based, sentence-level, and 472 

word-level contextual cues to determine when prior context supports lexical prediction, 473 

semantic scaffolding, or both [4,49–51]. Finally, because lexical and sentential predictors 474 

in the context-supported condition incorporated the synopsis as preceding context, future 475 

analyses should further compare synopsis-informed and synopsis-free predictor models 476 

to clarify how global context is incorporated into computational measures of lexical 477 

uncertainty and sentential semantic integration. 478

In conclusion, this study shows how prior context can scaffold comprehension when 479 

speech is degraded by noise. By combining naturalistic narratives, simultaneous EEG-480 

fNIRS recording, and hierarchical encoding models, we found that contextual support was 481 

more clearly expressed in sentence-level semantic integration than in enhanced word-482 

by-word prediction. These findings suggest that listeners use prior context not simply to 483 

anticipate upcoming words, but to organize imperfect speech input into a coherent 484 

discourse representation. More broadly, the results highlight semantic scaffolding as a 485 

flexible neural strategy for maintaining communication under adverse listening conditions. 486

Participants 489

range, 18–28 years) participated in this study. All participants were right-handed and 491 

reported normal hearing and normal or corrected-to-normal vision. The study was 492

EEG recording and preprocessing 561 

EEG signals were recorded simultaneously with fNIRS during the listening task using 562 

a 29-channel Neuracle EEG system (Neuracle, China). Signals were sampled at 1000 563 

Hz. Electrodes were positioned according to the international 10–10 system, including 564 

FP1/2, FZ, F3/4, F7/8, FCZ, FC3/4, FT7/8, CZ, C3/4, T7/8, CP3/4, TP7/8, PZ, P3/4, P7/8, 565

[54]. The continuous EEG data were first notch filtered to remove 50-Hz powerline noise 570 

and re-referenced to the average of all channels. Independent Component Analysis (ICA) 571 

was then performed to identify and remove artifacts related to eye blinks and eye 572 

movements based on visual inspection. 1–6 independent components were removed per 573 

participant. The remaining components were back-projected to reconstruct the artifact-574 

corrected EEG signals. 575

between 1 and 8 Hz. The frequency range was selected because low-frequency EEG 577 

activity in the delta and theta bands has been widely used to examine neural tracking of 578 

continuous speech and speech comprehension under different listening conditions 579 

[30,55,56]. Finally, EEG signals were segmented into trials from 5 to 80 s relative to 580

Stimulus-response modeling 662 

The multivariate temporal response function (mTRF) approach based on ridge 663 

regression was employed to estimate the relationship between continuous speech 664 

features and neural responses. For each participant, condition and recording channel, 665 

stimulus predictors were used to predict the corresponding EEG or fNIRS response. 666 

Model performance was quantified as the Pearson correlation coefficient between the 667 

observed and predicted neural signals and was Fisher-z-transformed before statistical 668 

analysis. 669 

The regularization parameter λ was selected using leave-one-trial-out cross-670 

validation within each condition and participant. In each iteration, the model was trained 671 

on nine trials and tested on the left-out trial; this procedure was repeated until each of the 672 

ten trials in a condition had served as the test trial. Candidate λ values ranged 673 

logarithmically between 0.1 and 1000, and the λ value that yielded the highest cross-674 

validated prediction accuracy averaged across trials was selected for subsequent 675 

modeling in that condition and participant. 676 

For EEG analysis, TRFs were estimated over the time lags from -200 to 1000 ms, 677 

following previous studies of continuous encoding and lexical-level prediction [26,29–31]. 678 

For fNIRS analysis, TRFs were estimated over a broader time-lag window from 0 to 15 s 679 

to account for the slower hemodynamic response [60]. fNIRS trials were extended by an 680 

additional 20 s, and stimulus predictors were zero-padded accordingly to match the 681 

duration of the fNIRS response [60]. Before model fitting, neural signals were normalized 682 

to ensure consistent scaling across channels and participants, as recommended for 683 

mTRF analyses [27,28]. All mTRF analyses were implemented in MATLAB using the 684 

mTRF-Toolbox [27]. 685
