Abstract 11 

There is significant interest in combined visual and auditory stimulation to entrain 40 Hz gamma 12 

oscillations for the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease and other neurological conditions such as 13 

stroke and insomnia. In this work, we compared another sensory modality —vibrotactile 14 

stimulation delivered with a glove —to visual and auditory stimulation in 15 healthy participants 15 

in terms of electroencephalogram (EEG) responses and subjective experience. We found that 16 

vibrotactile stimulation from the glove could evoke 40 Hz EEG responses in the central, frontal 17 

and, to a lesser extent, occipital cortices. We also observed distinct patterns of functional 18 

connectivity between the two stimulation modalities . Participants preferred the vibrotactile 19 

stimulation over the visual and auditory stimulation. Our study supports future investigations on 20 

vibrotactile stimulation for the treatment of neurological conditions. 21 

Keywords: non -invasive stimulation, vibrotactile stimulation, Alzheimer’s disease 22 

Introduction 23 

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative condition without an effective 24 

pharmacotherapeutic treatment that is estimated to affect 5.7 million in the USA [1] and 30 -35 25 

million worldwide [2] . Brain oscillations are disrupted in AD and other neurological conditions 26 

such as Parkinson’s disease and epilepsy. There is ongoing research in developing non -invasive 27 

methods to restore physiological brain oscillations for these diseases [3] . Recent studies found 28 

that combined visual and auditory stimulation (VAS) at the gamma frequency of 40 Hz reduced 29 

pathologies associated with AD and improved cognitive function in mouse models of 30 

neurodegeneration [4,5] . More specifically, stimulation at the 40 Hz gamma frequency, but not 31 

other frequencies, reduced the accumulation of Beta -amyloid plaques and phosphorylated tau 32 

protein that characterize AD [6] . Furthermore, six weeks of one hour of daily VAS reduced 33 

neuronal and synaptic loss, which are hallmarks of neurodegenerative diseases. VAS had more 34 

beneficial effects than either visual or auditory stimulation alone, including decreasing amyloid 35 

in medial prefrontal cortex [5] . Another modality, namely somatosensory stimulation based on 36 

whole -body vibrotactile stimulation, also decreased brain pathology associated with AD in the 37 

primary somatosensory cortex and primary motor cortex [7] . Other techniques such as vagal 38

certain mouse models [8] .40 

Based on these studies in mouse models, pilot studies in humans with AD are underway with 41 

VAS. Recent clinical studies show promising preliminary evidence that prolonged stimulation 42 

positively impacts neural activity and immune cells [9,10] , as well as improves sleep quality and 43 

activities of daily living in patients with prodromal AD [11] . In a randomized contro lled trial, AD 44 

patients in the treatment group received VAS from neurostimulation glasses for an hour per day 45 

for six months , which resulted in reduced white matter and myelin loss compared to the sham 46 

group, as assessed with magnetic resonance imaging [12] . In addition to AD, gamma oscillations 47 

induced by 40 Hz VAS can rescue functional synaptic plasticity after stroke in mice [13] and 48 

promote sleep in children with insomnia [14] .49 

Clinical application of VTS remains limited, but EEG -based studies have shown that 20 Hz VTS 50 

induces stable responses in the somatosensory cortex over extended periods without signs of 51 

habituation [15] , and other studies have also demonstrated reliable entrainment at 40 Hz in 52 

healthy participants [16,17] . This evidence indicates that VTS can provide a reliable means of 53 

driving brain oscillation activity. Though VAS showed clinical feasibility in AD patients, daily 54 

exposure to one hour of VAS presents a substantial challenge, even among patients actively 55 

seeking treatment as the flashes of light (i.e., light flickering) can limit their vision and prevent 56 

their activities of daily living, and the auditory tone can be difficult to tolerate. For example, 57 

Hajós et al. [18] reported that 18.5 % of participants declined participation during screening and 58 

28% dropped out during treatment. Meanwhile, VTS has the practical advantage of being 59 

applicable during activities of daily living. Most human studies of VTS have concentrated on 60 

active applications such as brain -computer interfaces, rather than on reactive entrainment 61 

responses [17,19] . As a result, evidence regarding its tolerability and neurophysiological effects 62 

in humans remains preliminary. These considerations highlight the need for studies directly 63 

comparing VAS and VTS in humans as a stimulation approach prior to clinical application. 64 

In this study, we investigated VTS as a potential sensory modality for 40 Hz putative gamma 65 

entrainment. The mechanical vibration was applied on the back of the hand, which produces a 66 

sensation like a phone vibrating. We evaluated EEG responses from two minutes of VAS and 67 

VTS to 15 healthy participants in terms of response locations and changes in brain connectivity. 68 

In addition, participants verbally answered a questionnaire to assess the tolerability of the 69 

different stimulation modalities. 70

Data Collection Methods 95 

EEG signals were recorded using 16 dry electrodes from the OpenBCI Ultracortex ’Mark IV’ 96 

EEG Headset, structured in a 10 -20 configuration (see Fig. 2A). We used two reference nodes on 97 

the earlobes. The measured impedances ranged between 15 k Ω and 80 k Ω for all channels, 98 

which are below acceptable thresholds [22,23] . The data sampling rate was 125 Hz. For one 99 

subject, whose head size did not fit the dry electrode headset, gel -type neuroelectric sensors were 100 

used instead. 101 

VTS apparatus : Because of the concentration of sensory endings and receptors on the hand [24] ,102 

we applied a glove -based VTS. We used an eccentric rotating motor (ERM; Yooptop 2300 RPM 103 

12V DC Motor) to produce the desired 40 Hz frequency using a constant voltage (3.65 V). We 104 

used a linear voltage regulator (LM337T), a dual differential comparator , a TO -220 heat sink, 105 

and multiple resistors to ensure a constant voltage supply or shut down the motor when the input 106 

was insufficient. The whole circuit was soldered onto a PCB board. The glove was comprised of 107 

the PCB board, an ERM motor, and a 9V lithium -ion battery. The PCB board and the motor are 108 

inside a 3 -D printed case, sewn onto the glove to stimulate the backside of the hand. We chose 109 

the backside of the hand because it causes less interference with activities of daily living than the 110 

palm. Furthermore, our pilot testing with 5 participants indicated that it was at least as effective 111 

as the palm. When constructing the glove apparatus, a stable frequency of 40 Hz was confirmed 112 

in two subjects in pilot testing. However, during the actual experiments, we observed a frequency 113 

variation of approximately 5%, ranging from 38 to 42 Hz. This deviation was caused by 114 

vibrations in the subject’s hand and the motor itself attached to the glove. In future work, w e plan 115 

to use an accelerometer to monitor the frequency in real -time and adjust the voltage accordingly 116 

to address this limitation in accuracy with VTS .117 

VAS apparatus : For VAS, a sound bar ‘AS500’ from Dell with a top -mounted light source was 118 

used. The light source consisted of 36 Rextin LEDs of 5050 LED (5,0 × 5.0 mm, with three 119 

diodes per package), arranged in a rectangular bar with dimensions of 50 cm in length and 1.2 120 

cm in width. The audio input signal was split between the sound bar and a circuit to 121 

synchronously drive the LEDs. The LED driver input was high -pass filtered at 340 Hz and 122 

applied to an inverting op -amp with a gain of 20, which overloaded the amplifier and generated a 123 

negative output pulse to trigger a 555 timer. The timer was set to produce a 12.5 ms (50% duty 124 

cycle) positive pulse, which was applied to a transistor switch in series with the LED power 125 

source. The audio stimulator pulse was a 1ms -long 10 kHz tone [5] , delivered by a computer 126 

audio file and presented at 58 dB in amplitude. 127

EEG signal preprocessing 130 

The EEG signals were imported into MATLAB 2022a (The MathWorks, Inc.) using EEGLAB 131 

v2024.0 [25] . Sensor locations were assigned based on default boundary element model from 132 

DIPFIT v5.4 [26,27] , and these standardized locations were later utilized for signal source 133 

analysis. EEG signals were filtered using a 60 Hz line noise filter [28] and finite impulse 134 

response band -pass filter between 1 -60 Hz. Re -referencing was performed to the average 135 

reference, with a zero -padded channel included to ensure the data retained full rank [29] . Using 136 

the re -referenced EEG data, we computed 1 -second quantized spectrograms and excluded data 137 

that showed atypical uniform increase across all frequencies, which can be caused by sensor 138 

dislocation, muscle activity or eye blinking [30] .139 

Following noise removal, independent component analysis (ICA) was deployed using AMICA 140 

[31] , an adaptive mixture ICA function to decompose the EEG data into mutually independent 141 

components (ICs) with corresponding topographic projections on the scalp sensors. To 142 

systematically isolate and remove artifact s, the decomposed ICs were automatically classified 143 

using the ICLabel plugin [32] . ICs with a ‘Muscle ’ or ‘Eye ’ probability greater than 50% were 144 

excluded, resulting in an average of 3.33 ±1.54 rejected ICs per participant . The signal source 145 

dipole locations of the remaining ICs were then computed using DIPFIT , which localized each 146 

IC within the Montreal neurological institute template [33] . Each dipole location, initially a 147 

single 2x2x2 mm voxel, was transformed into a dipole density sphere using 3 -dimensional 148 

Gaussian smoothing with a 20 -mm full width at half maximum. This transformation mitigated 149 

inter -subject variability in dipole locations and determined distributed activation patterns across 150 

specific anatomical regions. The resulting dipole density was mapped onto anatomical regions 151 

using an automated anatomical labeling system, enabling consistent localization across subjects 152 

and group -level analysis [34] .153 

EEG signal analysis 154 

We analyzed the relative intensity changes between the stimulation and pre -stimulation periods 155 

to examine whether and where brainwave entrainment occurred. Narrow -band signal -to noise 156 

ratio was computed as the ratio of power at the stimulation frequency to the median power of its 157 

neighboring frequency bins within ±0.75 Hz, 0.25 Hz steps, excluding the target bin, following 158 

frequency -tagging practice [35,36] . We used the median instead of the mean to obtain a robust 159 

estimate against outlier bins. Individual entrainment was defined by a robust z score at the target 160 

stimulation relative to the neighboring -bin distribution, with responders identified at z ≥ 1.96 and 161 

a power increase of at least 3 dB compared to the pre -stimulation period . Condition effects on 162 

response intensity were quantified as relative dB between the stimulation and pre -stimulation 163 

periods and tested at the group level with paired t -tests. 164 

We also investigated the EEG connectivity shift s due to stimulation. In channel -based functional 165 

connectivity analysis, coherence between EEG channel pairs was quantified across canonical 166 

frequency bands . All coherence values were Fisher z -transformed, and statistical significance 167

Results 178 

We compared the effects of the different stimulation modalities on the neural circuit based on the 179 

putative entrainment location on the scalp near the 40 Hz stimulation frequency. For VAS, 14 180 

out of 15 participants exhibited significant putative entrainment in at least one of the 16 181 

channels. Similarly, for VTS, 13 out of 15 participants showed putative entrainment in at least 182 

one channel. In Fig. 2, we illustrate the relative intensity of stimulation onset brain entrainment 183 

at the 40 Hz frequency compared to the pre -stimulation period, averaged over all subjects for 184 

VAS and VTS. As shown in Fig. 2B and 2E, the primary response of the VAS was the occipital 185 

area with a 5.3 ± 3.0 dB intensity increase at the O2 sensor. In Fig. 2C, we show the 40 Hz 186 

response in O2 during the pre -stimulation, stimulation and post -stimulation periods. The other 187 

main response occurred in the frontal cortices adjacent to channel Fz with a 4.2 ± 3.4 dB 188 

intensity increase. VTS showed the highest 40 Hz stimulation -locked response on the frontal -189 

central region with an average 4.8 ± 4.3 dB intensity on sensor Fz. The second most putatively 190 

entrained sensor is C3 in the left hemisphere, as participants wore gloves on their right hand. 191 

Nearly half of the participants (n=7) demonstrated strong 40 Hz stimulation -locked responses in 192 

the occipital and adjacent parietal regions with VTS (with more than 3 dB intensity increase). 193 

We excluded the possibility that this putative entrainment was due to the mechanical vibration of 194 

the motor (between 38 to 42 Hz) , as we verified that the accelerometer (placed near the occipital 195 

regions) did not exhibit a peak near 40 Hz frequency. In Fig. 2D, we show the power spectral 196 

density mean and standard deviation on a predominantly entrained channel (Fz). The response 197 

amplitude to the VTS was larger than that of the VAS. We did not vary the stimulation intensity 198 

in this study. Variations in the motor frequency (between 38 -42 Hz due to the glove apparatus) 199 

resulted in a more widespread response near the 40 Hz target with VTS , averaging at 39.97 ±200 

1.32 Hz over the 15 participants. The 40 -Hz putative entrainment was not observed in the post -201

Discussion 243 

Non -invasive 40 Hz stimulation has recently received considerable attention because of 244 

promising experiments conducted on mouse models of neurodegeneration [4,5] and stroke [13] .245 

Several studies are now underway in AD patients to assess the feasibility and safety of VAS and 246 

to assess the effects of the stimulation when applied over long durations on the neural circuitry 247 

[9 –11] . In this study, we examined EEG responses from healthy participants to VAS and VTS 248 

and gathered participants' feedback on their preferred stimulation modality. 249

processing [39,40] . Similarly, VAS also stimulated the somatosensory cortices (see Fig. 2A), 262 

which may encode visual information in working memory [41] . Our work shows that a cost -263

recent work was unsuccessful at this task [42] . This discrepancy may be due to differences in 265 

hardware considerations or device desig n. Nevertheless, given the absence of a dedicated sham 266 

or alternative -frequency control condition, we cannot rule out the possibility that these observed 267 

EEG dynamics partly reflect modality -specific evoked responses rather than pure cortical 268 

entrainment [43,44] .269 

During VAS, we observed a distinct reduction in long -range connectivity . Specifically, pathway -270 

relevant connectivity from the occipital area to the frontal area decreased in the beta band ([45] ;271 

see Fig. 4A-C). Furthermore, our channel -based coherence analysis revealed a significant 272 

reduction in gamma -band coupling between posterior and anterior sites (P4 -F4) during 273 

stimulation. These findings suggest that VAS may induce a distinct , immediate modulatory 274 

effect, potentially by prioritizing sensory -driven activity that transiently suppress es long -range 275 

posterior -to -anterior communication .276 

In contrast, VTS did not induce significant connectivity change during the stimulation period ,277 

possibly indicating that vibro tactile stimulation requires a longer integration window or operates 278 

through different neural pathways than visual -auditory inputs . VTS elicited a significant increase 279 

in interconnectivity within the anterior frontal region in the gamma band during the post -280 

stimulation period. This highlights a possible mechanistic difference: while may VAS induce 281 

rapid, transient network reconfiguration (reduced connectivity ) between posterior and anterior 282 

regions, VTS appears to promote delayed or sustained local integration within the anterior frontal 283 

cortex. The absence of immediate locking in VTS, followed by a post -stimulation increase in the 284 

gamma band , suggests that VTS could potentially be associated with a cumulative 285 

electrophysiological change , though the definitive underlying neuroplastic mechanism s remain to 286 

be validated with a sham control and increased electrode density . Thus, VTS might be more 287 

advantageous for applications requiring sustained cort ical excitability rather than immediate state 288 

changes. Because our analysis was performed with only 16 EEG electrodes, future work with 289 

high -density EEG could improve the spatial resolution of our findings. A limitation of our study 290

is that we did not include a sham or different stimulation frequency. For this reason, we cannot 291 

assess whether the observed EEG changes are specific to the 40 Hz VAS or VTS or reflect more 292 

general effects of sensory stimulation or attention, especially on a connectivity basis. Future 293 

studies could incorporate appropriate control conditions to better isolate frequency -specific and 294 

modality -specific effects. 295 

Prior work has shown that VAS is more effective than visual or auditory stimulation alone in 296 

reducing AD pathologies [5] . The brain adjusts for the differences in transmission and sensory 297 

processing times when integrating different sensory modalities [46] . Teplan et al. [47] 298 

investigated VAS and observed a stabilized angular difference after a certain period of sustained 299 

stimulation. We also found a phase difference between the auditory and visual 40 Hz stim ulation -300 

locked responses (see Supplementary F ig. 1). Future studies should investigate the optimal lags 301 

(if any) between sensory modalities to avoid negative interference at 40 Hz and improve 302 

treatment outcomes, which will be especially important as technologies with more sensory 303 

modalities are developed. 304 

Based on the participants’ preliminary feedback on the stimulation, all but one participant 305 

preferred the VTS modality. The participant who preferred VAS also noted that the VTS could be 306 

easier to tolerate if applied daily. However, the fixed order of conditions (VAS preceding VTS in 307 

the study) may have introduced an order effect, potentially involving subjective factors reported 308 

during post -experimental interviews, such as a cumulative fatigue or a sense of relief once the 309 

initial stimulation was completed. Consequently, given this non -counterbalanced design and the 310 

short stimulation duration, our observations regarding participant preference and tolerability 311 

should be regarded as preliminary. Future studies should employ randomized or counterbalanced 312 

designs to control for potential order effects. We confirmed that the physiological baseline (pre -313 

stimulation resting state) remained consistent between the VAS and VTS conditions, as there 314 

were no statistically significant differences (p > .05) in RPDC values or 40 Hz power. Since the 315 

stimulation would likely require daily sessions over months or even years, treatments can be 316 

burden some for individuals. Therefore, VTS represents a potential alternative sensory modality 317 

to VAS, warranting further investigation into whether it could offer greater comfort and mobility, 318 

in addition to reducing limitations on social interactions and engagement with the external 319 

environment. As our study observations were based on short -term exposures (2 -minute 320 

stimulation periods), the tolerability of extended daily use remain s to be tested in future 321 

longitudinal studies. Moreover, for individuals with reflex epilepsy, such as photosensitive 322 

occipital lobe epilepsy, non -visual stimulus options may minimize risk. The incorporation of 323 

other stimulation modalities could help address the diverse needs of patients and enhance 324 

treatment outcomes. Extending treatment duration and improving compliance through such 325 

options may also be critical for promoting neural plasticity and supporting recovery. 326

We confirmed that both VAS and VTS evoked near 40 Hz gamma oscillations in the sensory and 328 

frontal cortices , while producing distinct patterns of functional connectivity . Specifically, VAS 329 

was associated with an immediate and transient reduction in long -range coherence between 330 

posterior and anterior regions. In contrast, VTS preferentially enhanced local intra -frontal 331 

gamma connectivity during the post -stimulation period, suggesting a potential sustained after -332 

effect and a different mode of network engagement. 333 

Although our study was limited to a fixed stimulation intensity and short -term exposure to the 334 

stimulation , it provides a proof -of -concept for modality -specific gamma modulation. Our 335 

participant population were healthy young adults, which may limit generalizability to elderly or 336 

Alzheimer’s disease populations. Future studies could explore optimized stimulation parameters 337 

(such as frequencies and intensities) , longer intervention periods, and the inclusion of clinical 338 

populations —such as those with Alzheimer ’s disease —to establish the therapeutic potential of 339 

these differing stimulation paradigms. 340
