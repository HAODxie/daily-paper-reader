# Brain Control of a Computer Cursor for Online Target Selection - A Non-Invasive BCI for Continuous Movement Decoding

ABSTRACT 2

Non-invasive brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have substantially advanced in the field of 3

continuous cursor control over the past decade. Yet, current methods lack key control aspects 4

such as initiation and termination of cursor movements as well as evaluation in real-world 5

applications. In this study, we introduce a framework for continuous, electroencephalography-6

based cursor control that supports both active movement and no-movement states, thereby 7

allowing for inactive periods of the user when no control input is desired. We demonstrate its 8

applicability in healthy participants and show its performance in real-world application through the 9

selection of targets on a screen. This demonstrates that participants can leverage the continuous 10 

control cursor control and the intentional starting and stopping of motions to effectively select 11 

targets on a screen through dwell-time selection. On average, 7.1 out of 40 targets were correctly 12 

selected (level of significant performance: 4.5 targets), while experienced BCI users achieved 13 

an average of 12 .8 targets. The proposed framework additionally demonstrates compatibility 14 

with motor-impaired people without residual hand motions since it does not rely on observable 15 

movements for model training. 16 

> Keywords: electroencephalography,brain-computer interface,continuous cursor control, hand movement, non-invasive

1 INTRODUCTION 

Continuous cursor control is a prerequisite for intuitive human-computer interaction. Yet, most non-invasive 18 

brain-computer interface (BCI) systems emphasize discrete classification rather than continuous motion. 19 

For severely motor-impaired patients, such as patients with Locked -in Syndrome (LIS), the control of 20 

a continuous input method is especially valuable since these patients rely on caregivers or eye tracking 21 

systems as their only form of communication. Current invasive BCIs have substantially progressed in this 22 

area and demonstrate remarkable continuous control of computer cursors (Wilson et al., 2025; Deo et al., 23 

2024; Dekleva et al., 2021; Degenhart et al., 2018). 24 

However, invasive methods require complex surgical interventions which are associated with medical 25 

risks and may thus not be feasible for all patients. They also require a lengthy planning and screening 26 

phase during which participants do not benefit from the BCI. Non-invasive BCIs can offer a practical 27 

alternative. Due to their straightforward and quick application, they can be made available to nearly 28 

all patients at a fraction of the cost and effort of invasive BCIs. However, non-invasive BCIs utilizing 29

electroencephalography (EEG) deal with a low signal-to-noise ratio and oftentimes poor performances 30 

in distinguishing fine motions of the hand or other body parts (Edelman et al., 2024). While the online 31 

classification of discrete (hand) movements versus rest and the utilization in matrix spellers can be achieved 32 

with high accuracies and low rates of false positives per minute (Suwandjieff et al., 2026; Crell et al., 33 

2026; Ibanez et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2014; Fatourechi et al., 2007; Scherer et al., 2004; Pereira et al., 2021), 34 

reliable EEG-based cursor control remains an unsolved problem. 35 

Prior work on this topic has largely followed two approaches: (i) the direct decoding of continuous 36 

kinematics and (ii) high-rate classification of discrete movements. In the first approach, continuous 37 

kinematic variables such as position, velocity, or direction are regressed directly from EEG. This approach 38 

facilitates closed-loop, intuitive trajectory control by mapping attempted or executed movement to 39 

kinematics. Prior studies utilizing this approach have demonstrated online cursor control by directly 40 

regressing hand position in both able-bodied and motor-impaired participants (Mondini et al., 2024, 2020; 41 

Pulferer et al., 2022). Without online application, other studies further showed promising methods for the 42 

continuous regression of speed, movement direction and the simultaneous decoding of both (Lu et al., 43 

2026; Crell and M ¨uller-Putz, 2024b; Kobler et al., 2020b) from hand motions. The second approach detects 44 

discrete gestures at high temporal resolution and converts them into directional commands. Although 45 

this pseudo-continuous strategy lacks the natural, intuitive control associated with direct regression of 46 

continuous movements from a single end-effector, it was proven to enable directional control of virtual 47 

cursors in participants (Edelman et al., 2019; Suma et al., 2020; Forenzo et al., 2024). 48 

While both approaches demonstrate the feasibility of generating continuous trajectories from non-invasive 49 

EEG, evaluation is largely restricted to pursuit tracking tasks, with performance primarily quantified using 50 

correlation coefficients or trajectory error metrics. Importantly, none of the current studies systematically 51 

assess real-world cursor control applications such as target selections, leaving the practical applicability of 52 

continuous EEG-based cursor control largely insufficiently established. Moreover, current continuous and 53 

pseudo-continuous EEG cursor control studies do not explicitly define or evaluate a robust stopping or 54 

no-movement state. Cursor motion is typically updated continuously, requiring sustained neural modulation 55 

to suppress movement rather than enabling deliberate idle periods (Wolpaw and McFarland, 2004). The 56 

absence of a dedicated stopping mechanism poses a critical barrier for real-world use, where stable 57 

holding, target dwelling, and intentional inactivity are essential. While some previous work has successfully 58 

identified start and stop of continuous movements by detecting movement on- and offsets (Crell and 59 

M¨ uller-Putz, 2024a), an extension to continuous cursor control has not been attempted so far. 60 

To address these limitations, our study proposes a continuous cursor control framework that enables 61 

explicit no-movement periods of the cursor while preserving adequate directional control during movement 62 

periods. We further demonstrate, for the first time in EEG-based BCIs, successful target selection with 63 

explicit no-movement periods using continuous EEG decoding. 64

2.3 Correlation of Movement and Selection Performance 136 

The correlation between the accuracy of the movement classification during the online task and the 137 

selected targets was 0.628 (p = 0 .012 ). The correlation between the angle error of the movement direction 138 

(i.e., the error between the angle of the executed motion and the cursor motion) during the online task 139 

and the selected targets was −0.480 (p = 0 .070 ). Thus, the accuracy of the movement detection was 140 

significantly correlated with the target selection performance, while the directional mismatch between the 141 

executed hand movement and the cursor motion, although showing a trend of influencing the number of 142 

selected targets, was not significantly correlated with the target selection performance. 143 

We further investigated the correlation of the movement detection accuracy and the angle error of the 144 

validation set (calibration paradigm) with the online target performance measures to estimate whether the 145 

validation measures were indicative of the online performance. Correlations for motion detection accuracies 146 

and angle error (validation vs. online) were 0.658 (p = .008 ) and 0.556 (p = 0 .032 ), respectively. The 147 

correlation between the validation motion detection accuracies and validation angle error with the online 148 

target selection performance were 0.781 (p = 0 .001 ) and −0.592 (p = 0 .020 ), respectively. Validation set 149 

performances were thus indicative of the performance during the online task. The correlation between the 150 

validation motion detection / angle error and the online target selection performance is shown in Figure 2 F 151

3 DISCUSSION 

The presented continuous cursor control framework demonstrates sufficient precision to enable target 207 

selection in a substantial number of participants. Compared to prior EEG-based BCIs for cursor control 208 

(Mondini et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2026; Forenzo et al., 2024), our study demonstrates continuous control 209 

based on free hand movements not aligned to fixed motions (e.g., pursuit tracking tasks) and allows for 210 

no-movement states between continuous motions. Overall, the study shows the feasibility of the proposed 211 

data collection and decoder training procedure for application in an online environment and indicates the 212 

practicality of the approach in real-world scenarios. 213 

While invasive BCIs routinely enable high target selection accuracies of over 90 % (Patrick-Krueger 214 

et al., 2024), the achieved target selection rate of 17 .7 % overall and 32 .1 % for experienced users in this 215 

study is substantial for a non-invasive, EEG-based BCI. Comparisons between previous BCIs for cursor-216 

controlled target selection are difficult due to differences in the task implementation such as time-outs, 217 

dwell-period or boundary behavior. Further and to our knowledge, no EEG-based (non-invasive) BCIs exist 218 

that enable the achieved form of free, continuous motion from hand movement and real-world applicability 219 

as demonstrated with the target selection task. In our study, target selection was enabled by the introduction 220 

of a no-movement state, which is essential for natural control and real-world applications. The introduction 221 

of the start-and-stop capabilities in the current framework is therefore an important contribution to the state 222 

of the research in this field. 223 

Specifically, while existing studies controlling a cursor based on modulation of neural signals 224 

demonstrated sufficient control of the cursor movement (Wolpaw and McFarland, 2004), they lack the 225 

intuitive control achieved from direct utilization of hand movements and require an extensive learning 226 

phase. Additionally, such control methods do not enable idle no-movement periods or dwell-time selection. 227 

The substantial number of selected targets during the online target selection task, especially for the group 228 

of experienced users, shows that participants could effectively utilize the framework to execute planned 229 

cursor movements. The variation in the number of selected targets between groups of experienced and 230 

inexperienced users indicates the importance of familiarity of users with BCI-controlled interfaces. We 231 

consequently found an improvement of the ratio of successful target selections with time during the online 232 

task, further indicating a dependence of the performance on the familiarity with the task and interface. 233 

However, the increased correlation of the decoded hand kinematics found in the validation data also points 234 

towards differences in the collected training data between experienced users and novices which cannot 235 

be explained by familiarity with BCI-controlled interfaces. In line with this observation, previous studies 236 

have found a positive influence of user training on BCI performance (Abu-Rmileh et al., 2019) and cortical 237 

activations related to movement (Kaiser et al., 2014). Overall, this indicates that the achievable performance 238 

of the proposed framework is close to the performance of experienced users in this study. 239

Our framework achieved overall higher performance than previous studies for the decoding of kinematics 240 

from continuous hand movements. The correlation of the decoded velocities in x- and y-direction on 241 

the validation data from the calibration task exceeded previously obtained values of around ∼ 0.45 ( vx)242 

and ∼ 0.35 ( vy) (population averages) (Borra et al., 2023) or lower (Lv et al., 2010; Bradberry et al., 243 

2010). In accordance with these results, we similarly found reduced correlation for velocities in y-direction 244 

compared to those in x-direction. Similar reduced correlation in y-direction was also observed for the 245 

position instead of the velocities in related work (Mondini et al., 2020). Previous studies on the detection 246 

of continuous movement vs. rest activity reported lower accuracies than our achieved results with recent 247 

studies demonstrating an MCC of ∼ 0.6 (Crell et al., 2026). The high correlation of the speed and accuracy 248 

of the movement vs. rest detection is an important finding of our study. Nearly all participants obtained 249 

significantly better than random accuracies for the movement detection and, overall, ratings of control over 250 

the movement speed were high. Participants also verbally reported a feeling of confidence that ”the cursor 251 

moved when they wanted it to move” during the online task. 252 

The reduced correlation between the executed and decoded speed in the online task can mostly be 253 

attributed to the reduction in uniform motions. During the guided motions in the calibration task, visual 254 

feedback was directly given to the participants and their movement speed was kept at a reduced, nearly 255 

constant rate. Since participants could move freely during the online task, motions tended to be more 256 

diverse in the executed speed and duration and thus lead to reduced correlation with the decoder output. 257 

During the auditory movement task, where participants were clearly instructed to move for a certain 258 

duration at a constant pace, the obtained correlation of the speed was close to the performance on the 259 

validation set. 260 

While the movement detection successfully allowed start-and-stop movements with variable durations 261 

during the online task, the correlation of the decoded movement direction with the executed movement 262 

dropped below the level of significant performance in most participants. This behavior did not improve 263 

during more controlled motions in the auditory movement task. While directional correlation for 264 

experienced BCI users remained slightly higher than those of less experienced participants, the obtained 265 

angular error indicates near-random performance for the movement direction. This is also indicated by 266 

low subjective ratings of control over the cursor movement direction. In previous studies of online cursor 267 

control based on pursuit tracking tasks with similar calibration and online tasks, no such performance 268 

drops were reported (Mondini et al., 2020). We therefore assume that the change in directional decoding 269 

capabilities stems from differences in the neural patterns between the guided (calibration) and free (target 270 

selection) online task. The selection of targets despite the missing directional control may be attributed to 271 

start-and-stop motions with differing duration: if participants started a movement directed at the target, 272 

they could extend the motion, while they would immediately stop the movement if the direction was wrong. 273 

This is also emphasized by the significant correlation of speed and target selection performance compared 274 

to the non-significant correlation of the number of selected targets with the angle error. More broadly, this 275 

observation has important implications for the BCI field, as many studies evaluate movement decoding 276 

exclusively in offline settings using visually guided paradigms. In such cases, offline performance may 277 

overestimate the degree to which the decoder captures neural correlates of movement itself and may not 278 

accurately predict performance during self-paced online control. The marked discrepancy between offline 279 

and online directional decoding observed in the present study highlights the importance of validating 280 

continuous-control BCIs in realistic online environments. 281

Additionally, while the current study was conducted with able-bodied participants and executed hand 314

4.1 Participants and Setup 318 

Fifteen participants ( 10 females and 5 males with an average ( ± standard deviation) age of 26 .7 ± 3.5319 

years) took part in the study. EEG data of each participant was obtained from 60 channels. The EEG setup 320 

consisted of a standard 10-10 setup with additional electrodes at PPO1h and PPO2h. Channels at Fp1, Fpz, 321 

Fp2, F9, F10, FT9, FT10, T9, T10, TP9, TP10, P9 and P10 were excluded. Four electrooculography (EOG) 322 

electrodes were positioned on the outer canthi of the eyes and above and below the left eye. EEG and EOG 323 

data was recorded with biosignal amplifiers (BrainAmp, Brain Products GmbH, Gilching, Germany) at a 324 

rate of 500 Hz .325 

Motion capture data (coordinates along x- and y-axes along the table orientation) of the right index finger 326 

was obtained with a custom marker-based tracking system mounted above the hand of the participant. The 327 

tracked area ( 34 × 63 cm ) was covered in a friction-reducing surface. Participants were positioned 100 cm 328 

in front of a screen with the chair height adjusted to fit the height of the eyes with the center of the screen. 329 

The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and was approved by the Ethics 330 

Committee at Graz University of Technology (EK-49/2024). Participants received monetary compensation 331 

for their time and provided written informed consent before study participation. 332 

4.2 Calibration Paradigm 333 

During the calibration paradigm (Figure 2 A), participants controlled a blue cursor (participant cursor) on 334 

the screen with their right hand. Movements of the hand were tracked with a motion capture system and 335 

mapped to the cursor while applying a constant speed. Specifically, the participant cursor was controlled 336 

using a fixed speed, regardless of the actual speed of the hand movement, and the direction of the movement 337 

in the form of the velocity in x- and y-direction. Participants were therefore only able to control the direction 338 

and the timing of the cursor movement, but not the speed at which the cursor moved. This behavior was 339 

chosen since it ensured a high accuracy of the executed movement with the movement intended from the 340 

visual paradigm. Intended movements of the participant cursor were cued by a displayed trajectory and a 341 

red cursor (paradigm cursor). This cursor had an additional tail comprising the last 0.8 s of the movement. 342 

Trajectories comprised 3 to 7 targets (four trials per number of targets) indicated by white circles and black 343 

lines between the targets. The start position of each trajectory was in the middle of the screen. 344 

At the start of each trial, participants positioned their right index finger at the center of the tracked area. 345 

Paradigm and participant cursors were reset to the center of the screen. After 2.0 s , the paradigm cursor 346 

moved from the starting point to the first target. As soon as the cursor reached the target and the tail of 347 

the cursor disappeared, participants moved their cursor along the black line to the target. After a variable 348 

waiting period, the paradigm cursor would then move towards the next target and cue the next participant 349 

movement. After the last target, the next trial would be displayed and the cursors were reset to the starting 350 

position. 351 

The complete task comprised twelve runs of ten trials each. Trajectories had an average duration of 352 

47 .6 s . Participants could take breaks after each run, but had a mandatory break after the sixth run to reduce 353 

(unnoticed) fatigue. 354 

Prior to the calibration task, participants executed an eye-movement paradigm comprising specified eye 355 

movements. The eye movements consisted of rest, horizontal and vertical eye movements and blinks in 356

4.3 Online Task 359 

The online target selection task comprised eight runs of five targets each and had an average duration of 360 

21 .6 min . Participants controlled the speed and direction of a blue cursor through decoded EEG and had to 361 

select targets on the screen using this cursor. A target could be selected by positioning the cursor within 362 

the target for 3.0 s . The required duration of 3 s was indicated by a green progress circle within the target 363 

(Figure 1 B). While the cursor was positioned within a target, the target gradually filled with the progress 364 

circle. The circle expanded from the center of the target to visually indicate the progress of the dwell-time 365 

selection to the participants. If the cursor left the target, the progress circle would recede again at the same 366 

rate as when growing. The shrinking of the progress circle would start only 0.2 s after the cursor left the 367 

target. If the cursor re-entered the target, the progress circle would start to grow from its current expansion 368 

in case it was not completely shrunk. The target was selected when the progress circle completely filled the 369 

target. When a target was selected, the next target was immediately displayed. The next target was also 370 

displayed when a time-out of 30 s was exceeded. Targets were randomly generated at a fixed distance from 371 

the cursor position at the time of the target generation. 372 

At each new run, participants started at the center of the screen. However, the cursor position was not 373 

reset after a target selection or time-out. If participants reached the outer bounds of the screen, the cursor 374 

was mapped to the corresponding opposite side of the screen. 375 

4.4 Auditory Movement and Observation Tasks 376 

Eye movements are a major source of artifacts in EEG measurements. Since not only eye blinks but also 377 

rotations of the eye when following a visual object generate electrical artifacts with a high amplitude that 378 

can be measured directly, especially in frontal channels, the issue of decoding movement instead of gaze 379 

needs to be carefully addressed. To this extent, two specific tasks were introduced, the auditory movement 380 

and the observation task. During both tasks, participants did not control a cursor. 381 

In the auditory movement task (Figure 3 B), only a fixation cross was shown. Participants were instructed 382 

to focus the fixation cross with their gaze and move their hand according to auditory commands given from 383 

a speaker. These commands comprised up , down , left , right and the diagonal movements up-left , up-right ,384 

down-left and down-right . On hearing a command, participants moved their hand in the corresponding 385 

direction for approximately 2.0 s and then waited for the next command. Commands were issued every 386 

6.0 s with 20 commands in total. 387 

The observation task (Figure 3 C) was visually similar to the online task. In this task, the cursor was not 388 

controlled by the participant but instead moved automatically along the optimal trajectory to select the 389 

targets. In total, 20 targets were displayed and selected. Participants were instructed to keep their hand at 390 

the resting position and follow the moving cursor with their eyes. 391 

4.5 Data Processing 392 

EEG was first bandpass-filtered between 0.3 and 70 Hz (Butterworth filter, 4th order) and powerline noise 393 

was removed using a 50 Hz notch filter. Eye artifacts were eliminated with a correction matrix derived 394 

from the eye-movement data collected prior to the calibration paradigm (Kobler et al., 2020a). EEG was 395 

then lowpass-filtered at a cutoff frequency of 40 Hz and re-referenced to the common average of all EEG 396 

channels. The data was then subsampled to a sampling rate of 125 Hz .397

4.6 Decoder and Calibration 401 

To obtain values of the movement vector, we trained a model to predict the amplitude (speed) and the 402 

direction (velocity in x- and y-direction) from windows of the neural data. We utilized a CNN-LSTM 403 

model based on the EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018) architecture. The detailed model architecture can be 404 

found in the supplementary material (Table S6). Previous research suggested that the combination of CNN 405 

and LSTM enables higher performances than single CNNs for movement tasks (Chen et al., 2022; Lu 406 

et al., 2026). Conceptually, the EEGNet architecture extracts relevant spatial and spectral features while the 407 

LSTM layer introduces temporal dependency on the previous state of the model output. This is especially 408 

relevant in the case of continuous movements, since the direction of each step during a continuous motion 409 

is, to some extent, dependent on the direction of the previous step. To further ensure smooth subsequent 410 

predictions of the model, we applied a custom loss function (sMSE, smooth mean-squared error ) during 411 

training, comprising the mean squared error (MSE) and an additional smoothness term penalizing large 412 

differences between consecutive model predictions (Equation 1). 413

4.7 Offline Evaluation 444 

We calculated the trialwise Pearson correlation coefficient between the decoded and the intended speed 445 

and velocity in x- and y-direction on the validation set. We additionally calculated the trialwise Pearson 446 

correlation coefficient between the executed and the decoded motions. We further obtained the accuracy and 447 

the Matthew’s Correlation Coefficient (MCC) for the detection of movement vs. rest after thresholding the 448 

decoded speed. The MCC was chosen since it is robust to class imbalance between the rest and movement 449 

class (Chicco and Jurman, 2020). The directional accuracy was assessed by obtaining the direction from 450 

the decoded velocity in x- and y-direction and calculating the angular error between the decoded and the 451 

intended/actual angle of the movement vector. The angular error (in degree) was wrapped at ±180 ◦ to 452 

account for circular properties of the angle. 453 

We further obtained subject-wise levels of significant performance for the accuracy of the movement 454 

detection and the angle error. This was achieved by estimating the distribution of a random classifier 455 

through simulation ( n = 10000 ) and utilizing the 95 th percentile of this distribution as a level of significant 456 

performance (M¨ uller-Putz et al., 2008). 457 

4.8 Online Target Selection Task Evaluation 458 

The Pearson correlation coefficients, MCC, movement accuracy and angular error for the decoded 459 

movement during the online target selection task were calculated similar to the metrics during the calibration 460 

paradigm. All metrics were calculated between the decoded kinematics and the kinematics derived from 461 

the executed movement. Trialwise correlation was calculated between the appearance of a target and the 462 

disappearance of this target in the online target selection task. 463 

Targets were deemed correctly selected if the complete target circle was filled out by the progress circle. 464 

While a time-out of 30 s was applied to the visualization of the circles, targets would also be deemed 465 

correctly selected if they were selected within 0.5 s after the timeout. 466 

The statistical evaluation of the number of selected targets was based on a conditional randomization 467 

test of the executed cursor movements of each participant (Crell and M ¨uller-Putz, 2026). This method was 468 

applied to evaluate whether the observed number of selected targets was significantly ( α = 0 .05 ) better 469 

than random performance. For this, we generated random targets that were not overlapping with the actual 470 

target for each trial and evaluated the number of random targets that were selected based on the simulated 471 

dwell-time. We then obtained the null distribution of random performance by repeating this procedure 472 

for 1000 times. We obtained the 1 − α percentile of the null distribution above which performance was 473 

considered significantly better than chance for each individual participant. 474 

We identified trends over runs for the target selection performance by using a population-averaged 475 

generalized estimating equations (GEE) logistic model (Liang and Zeger, 1986). GEE was selected since it 476 

can analyze correlated binary outcomes from repeated measurements within subjects. The target selection 477

4.9 Behavioral Analysis 486 

Participants were asked to evaluate their perceived control over the cursor after completing the online 487 

target selection task. The questionnaire comprised three visual analog scales on which participants rated 488 

their control over the cursor speed (movement vs. rest), the movement direction and the general feeling of 489 

control over the cursor. Each scale ranged from zero (no control) to ten (complete control). We tested for 490 

statistically significant differences between the perceived control of the speed and the direction with a paired 491 

t-test. Ratings were further evaluated regarding their correlation with the target selection performance based 492 

on the Pearson correlation coefficient. We also evaluated the correlation between the perceived control of 493 

the speed and the direction with the accuracy of the movement detection and the angle error, respectively. 494

Horizontal (x) and vertical (y) eye movements were extracted during both tasks from the EOG data by 497
